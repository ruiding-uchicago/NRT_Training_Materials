Solution-Processed Ultrathin Semiconductor Films for High-Performance Ammonia Sensors

First published: 26 September 2021 https://doi.org/10.1002/admi.202100493Citations: 5

Abstract
A high-performance ammonia sensor based on a donor-acceptor conjugated polymer, bithiophene and bis(2-oxoindolin-3-ylidene)-benzodifuran-dione (PBIBDF-BT), is designed and fabricated. To improve its sensing performance, a polymer semiconductor ultrathin film with a minimum thickness of ≈4 nm is prepared using a phase separation method. The relationship between the ammonia sensing performance and film thickness is systematically studied. The ammonia sensor based on an ultrathin film shows a high sensitivity (S = 953) and a response time of less than one second (Tres = 0.63 ± 0.11 s). The preparation of ultrathin film sensors by the phase separation method not only expands the existing preparation methods of ammonia gas sensors but also provides a simple and effective design strategy for the fabrication of rapid-response gas detection sensors in the future. In addition, the advantage of the solution phase separation method to control the thickness of the ultrathin film helps to understand the gas sensing mechanism and the sensing behavior of ultrathin film devices.

1 Introduction
The detection of ammonia gas is of great importance for various industries, food storage, health detection, and atmospheric environmental monitoring. Highly sensitive sensors that can quickly detect toxic gases enable people to quickly respond to gas hazards and minimize harm.[1-3] High-performance gas sensors based on organic field-effect transistor (OFET) have attracted significant attention in recent years because the current amplification effect of OFETs gives them superior gas-sensing performance to traditional resistive gas sensors.[4-10] There are many gas sensing mechanisms in the OFETs have been described such as the gate-bias-controlled charge trapping, formation of shallow traps in the organic semiconducting film and the influence of the analyte gas molecules on grain boundaries resistance for polycrystalline organic semiconductor film.[11] But in general, the induced current of an OFET gas sensor is directly related to the ability of the target analyte to diffuse into the active channel of an OFET device.

To facilitate the exposure of the charge-transport layer and improve the sensing performance of OFET-type gas sensors, there are three main methods: porous films,[12-16] air-dielectric devices,[17] and ultrathin films.[18-22] Compared with porous film and air-dielectric devices, the ultrathin film method has better performance,[23-26] a simpler process,[27] and lower costs.[28, 29] The ultrathin organic semiconductor layer increase the contact area with gas molecules and reduces the diffusion path of gas molecules in an organic semiconductor before the analyte molecules interact with the charges in the charge-transport layer, thereby improving the sensing performance of the entire device.[11, 30] Researchers have already proposed many methods to prepare ultrathin film sensor devices, including the conventional deposition methods,[31] spin-coating,[19] Langmuir-Schaefer,[32] bar-coating,[33] solution-shearing[34, 35] etc. Katz et al. fabricated ultrathin 5,5“-bis (4-hexylphenyl)-2,2”-bithiophene (6PTTP6) field-effect transistors with 0.75 monolayer thick coverage. The sensitivity of a dimethyl methylphosphonate sensor was 110.[31] Zhang et al. demonstrated that the thickness of two 2-(1,3-dithiol-2-ylidene)malononitrile moieties (NDI-DTYM2) films could be precisely controlled via a simple spin-coating method. The ultrathin film NH3 sensor possessed a response time of 3 s, recovery time of 18 s, and sensitivity of 120.[19] Ponomarenko et al. developed the first highly sensitive gas sensor based on Langmuir-Schaefer 1,3-bis[11-(7-hexyl[1]-benzothieno-[3,2-b][1]benzothien-2-yl)undecyl]-1,1,3,3-tetramethyldisiloxane (O(Si-Und-BTBT-Hex)2) monolayer OFETs, which displayed a response time of 60 s and sensitivity of 78.[32] Noh et al. demonstrated an effective method for depositing ultrathin polymer films using a bar-coating method. The thickness of the polymer film could be precisely controlled to 1–2 organic molecular layers. The OFET sensor based on a 2 nm polymer film displayed an NH3 gas sensitivity of 82.[33] Although there have been many studies on ultrathin film gas sensors, there are still some unresolved problems. For example, the response and recovery times of OFET sensors are generally long, the sensitivity is low, and the quantitative relationship between performance and film thickness is still unclear. A simple and efficient strategy is needed to solve the above problems and promote further development of gas sensors. Solution phase separation is proved be an effective method to prepare ultrathin films.[21, 23, 36] Self-assembled conjugated polymer films can be obtained through vertical phase separation in blending systems, and the microstructure of the ultrathin film can be controlled by changing the concentration of the conjugated polymer. This allows ultrathin films with different thicknesses to be obtained by a simple transfer-etching method. Although there has been a lot of work on high performance gas sensors,[37-39] the advantages of phase separation may become a new strategy for constructing high performance OFET sensors.

In this paper, an OFET ultrathin-film ammonia sensor with high sensitivity and low response time was prepared by a solution vertical phase separation method. A donor-acceptor (D-A), conjugated polymer based on bithiophene and bis(2-oxoindolin-3-ylidene)-benzodifuran-dione (BIBDF), namely PBIBDF-BT, and polystyrene (PS) were selected as the active layer and additive, respectively, and ultrathin OSC films were prepared by etching PS with ethyl acetate. The film thickness was controlled by adjusting the solution blending ratio and its thicknesses were studied by high-resolution atomic force microscopy (AFM). A high-sensitivity PBIBDF-BT ultrathin-film OFET ammonia sensor was obtained, and the relationship between the ultrathin film thickness and ammonia sensing performance was studied.

2 Results and Discussion
2.1 Ultrathin Film Fabrication and Characterization
Figure 1a illustrates the diagram of the bottom-gate/top-contact OFET sensor based on a PBIBDF-BT ultrathin film. Blending semiconductor materials with insulating polymers is an effective method to control the film thickness and surface morphology, the blend ratio is the polymer weight ratio. Our group has reported some papers based on the vertical phase separation of two polymers. Research on this aspect has been more in-depth.[23] Ultrathin-film devices with excellent performance can be manufactured by this solution-processing method. Figure 1b illustrates the fabrication process of the PBIBDF-BT ultrathin films. Due to the incompatibility of PBIBDF-BT and PS, the blended film underwent vertical phase separation during spin-coating. The blended solutions with different PBIBDF-BT/PS ratios were spin-coated on the Si substrate with a PVA sacrificial layer. The sacrificial layer (PVA) is conducted for better separation of the blended film from the Si substrate in water to realize the flip, transfer and etching of the film. PBIBDF-BT ultrathin films with different thicknesses were obtained by a series of transfer etching processes shown in Figure 1bi–vi.

The morphological evolution of the PBIBDF-BT/PS composite film was systematically studied to analyze the phase separation process. The blending ratio of the two polymers was changed, and the surface structure of the blended films was analyzed by AFM. Figure 2a–f shows the AFM images of the PBIBDF-BT ultrathin films with different PBIBDF-BT/PS ratios, and Figure S1 in the Supporting Information shows the AFM images of the top surface of the PBIBDF-BT/PS composite films. It can be clearly observed from AFM images that PBIBDF-BT phase forms nanofibrillar microstructure on the top surface of the blended film when the PBIBDF-BT content is very low. The spatial arrangement of the microstructures ranges from a sparse nanofibrillar network (Figure 2a) to nanoflakes (Figure 2b) depending on the blending concentration. The thickness of the nanofibrillar network and nanoflakes was about 2.0–4.0 nm, which is considered to be the submonolayer of PBIBDF-BT (Figure 2a',b'). Figure 2c–f shows that at blending ratios greater than 1/80, the PBIBDF-BT nanofibrillar formed a continuous film after the transfer-etching process on the top surface of the blended film. The thickness of the ultrathin film was calculated by analyzing the well-defined steps formed by rapidly scraping the film with tweezers using an AFM. As shown in Figure 2c',f', the thicknesses of the ultrathin films prepared using this phase separation method were 3.9 ± 0.3, 4.8 ± 0.3, 6.2 ± 0.3, and 6.9 ± 0.9 nm when the concentrations of PBIBDF-BT/PS were 1/80, 1/40, 1/20, and 1/10, respectively. As the content of PBIBDF-BT was further increased, more pits and grooves appeared on the surface of the blend films. Since PBIBDF-BT and PS cannot undergo complete vertical phase separation during spin coating, some PBIBDF-BT molecules will aggregate and embed in the PS layer as the PBIBDF-BT content increases. The relationship between film thickness and blending concentration is shown in Figure 3a, and the ultrathin films with precisely controllable thicknesses can be prepared according to this relationship. The UV–vis spectra of PBIBDF-BT ultrathin films prepared with different blending ratios are shown in Figure 3b. The films prepared by phase separation method showed good transparency. When the concentration of PBIBDF-BT/PS in the blends was less than 1/20, the transparency of the prepared films was greater than 90%, indicating a very thin thickness. The results discussed above indicate that the solution phase separation method can be used to accurately control the morphology and structure of ultrathin films, which provides the opportunity of gas sensors that require short paths for the gas molecules to the OFET channels.

2.2 Electrical Characteristics of Ultrathin Film OFETs
Figure 4a shows the transfer curves of bottom-gate/top-contact OFETs of PBIBDF-BT ultrathin films with film thicknesses of 3.9 ± 0.3, 4.8 ± 0.3, 6.2 ± 0.3 , and 6.9 ± 0.9 nm. The corresponding output curves are shown in Figure S2 (Supporting Information). The device was operated under a drain-source voltage VDS of −80 V and a gate-source voltage (VGS) from 0 to −80 V. As the film thickness increased from 3.9 ± 0.3 to 6.9 ± 0.9 nm, the hole mobility increased from 3.4 × 10−4 to 1.5 × 10−1 cm2 V−1 s−1. The increase in device hole mobility was related to film thickness, the orderly aggregation of semiconductors, and film continuity. When the thickness of the ultrathin film is very low, the charge is transferred between the small amounts of fine nanofibers. The trap density in these parts is usually higher and the barrier is larger, resulting in a lower mobility and a larger threshold voltage. When the thickness of the ultrathin film increases, the nanofibers become denser and the coverage rate increases, resulting in an increase in mobility and a decrease in threshold voltage (Table 1).[23] Since the conduction channels of the ultrathin-film OFET were in direct contact with the air, the PBIBDF-BT polymer with a high LUMO energy level (−4.03 eV) formed electron traps in the air and degraded the electron transport performance of the ultrathin film OFET device. The ultrathin-film devices displayed no electron transport performance and the electrical properties of the PBIBDF-BT ultrathin film are related to the thickness of the film.

2.3 Selectivity of OFET-Type Ammonia Sensors
Different analytes will cause different changes in the channel current of OFET gas sensors. The OFET sensor based on a 3.9 nm PBIBDF-BT ultrathin film was exposed to different types of organic solvent vapors to test its gas selectivity. The gas testing device was set up as shown in Figure 5a. Organic solvent vapor was generated by the principle of saturated vapor pressure, and the concentration varied from 5700–6500 ppm. The ammonia concentration was fixed at 10 ppm. The parameters of the entire sensing test process are as follows: the drain voltage was a constant −80 V, the gate voltage was a pulse voltage with a frequency of 0.2 Hz, Voff = 0 V, and Von = −60 V. The sensitivity, response time, and recovery time of the sensor under different organic solvent vapors are shown in Figure 5b. The detailed data of the sensing performance are shown in Figure S3 (Supporting Information). The test results show that the sensitivity of the device to 10 ppm ammonia gas is much higher than that of other solvent vapors at concentrations of 5700– 6500 ppm, and the response/recovery times of the device are lower than other gases. These results show that the prepared OFET gas sensor has excellent selectivity toward ammonia.

2.4 Sensing Performance of Ammonia Sensor Based on OFETs with Ultrathin Semiconductor Film
To further investigate the performance of the ultrathin film OFET sensor to ammonia gas, devices with different film thicknesses were placed under various ammonia gas concentrations for sensing tests. The ammonia gas concentration atmosphere was changed from 0 ppm (pure nitrogen) to 10 ppm. Figure 6 shows the transfer characteristic curves of the OFET device with different film thicknesses when the ammonia gas concentration was 0, 2 , 4 , 6 , 8 , and 10 ppm. The OFET gas sensor prepared with different thicknesses of PBIBDF-BT ultrathin films had the maximum IDS in a pure nitrogen environment (0 ppm), and the transfer characteristic curves IDS showed a downward trend upon increasing the ammonia concentration. According to the transfer characteristic curves of the device under different ammonia concentrations, the mobility and threshold voltage were calculated, and the results are shown in Figure 7. As the ammonia concentration increased, the mobility gradually decreased, and Vth showed the same change trend as the mobility. This is because the charge dipole effect between ammonia gas and semiconductor increases the number of energy perturbations in the semiconductor and the dipole trapping effect of ammonia molecules on the semiconductor/insulating layer interface formed traps that changed the contact potential at the interface.[11] As the thickness of the PBIBDF-BT ultrathin film decreased, the device exhibited higher ammonia sensitivity.

Stability and repeatability are important properties for a sensor. Different ultrathin film devices were tested with a constant drain voltage of −80 V and the gate voltage was a pulse voltage with a frequency of 0.2 Hz, Voff = 0 V, Von = −60 V. The current (IDS) reached its maximum value when the device was operated under a high-purity nitrogen environment. When the device was exposed to 10 ppm ammonia gas, IDS dropped significantly in a short time. As the ammonia gas was removed, the current recovered to its original value. Figure S4 (Supporting Information) shows the cycle test performance of the sensors based on different PBIBDF-BT ultrathin films, indicating that the OFET gas sensor has excellent stability and repeatability. The response and recovery time of the sensor are defined as the time corresponding to a 90% change in the current. Table 2 shows the calculated response and recovery times of the sensor using different film thicknesses. As the semiconductor content decreased, the response time of the sensor to 10 ppm ammonia gas was accelerated, and the fastest response time was 0.63 ± 0.11 s. Figure 8a shows the corresponding results of the device with a 3.9 ± 0.3 nm thick film tested for several cycles under different ammonia concentrations. When the NH3 gas concentration changed from 2 to 4, 6, 8, and 10 ppm, the source and drain currents showed regular changes. Figure 8b shows the limit of detection (LOD) of the ultrathin film OFET sensor.

3 Conclusion
A simple and controllable phase separation method for producing composite films with vertically layered PBIBDF-BT/PS was proposed, and the morphological evolution and phase separation mechanism of PBIBDF-BT/PS composite films were investigated. A series of ultrathin films with different thicknesses were obtained from composite films using a simple transfer-etching method, which allowed the thickness of the PBIBDF-BT ultrathin film to be accurately controlled between 3.9 ± 0.3 and 6.9 ± 0.9 nm. The electrical performance and the gas sensing performance of the OFET based on PBIBDF-BT ultrathin films were systematically studied. The results showed that these OFET sensors have high selectivity for ammonia and good sensitivity to external ammonia stimulation (S = 953, Tres = 0.63 ± 0.11 s). Through comparative tests and dynamic calculations, the quantitative relationship between ultrathin film thickness and sensing performance parameters was shown. This work not only expands the current preparation methods of OFET gas sensors but may also provide a simple and effective design scheme for the construction of fast response detection gas sensors. The ability of the solution phase separation method to control the ultrathin film thickness is useful for understanding the gas sensing mechanism and sensing behavior of ultrathin film devices.

4 Experimental Section
Materials
Semiconductor polymer (PBIBDF-BT; Mw = 58 852 g mol−1) was synthesized as previously reported.[40, 41] Chloroform and PS (Mw = 2000 g mol−1) were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich Chemical Co., CYTOP and PVA was purchased from Asahi Glass Co., Ltd.

Preparation and Characterization of PBIBDF-BT Ultrathin Films
The SiO2/Si and Si substrates were first heated in a 7:3 ratio of concentrated sulfuric acid and hydrogen peroxide solution for 90 min, and then washed with distilled water and dried under a nitrogen stream. CYTOP was spin-coated onto the SiO2/Si substrate at a speed of 3000 rpm for 60 s and then heat-treated in a heated press plate at 180 °C for 15 min. PVA was spin-coated onto the Si substrate as a sacrifice layer at a speed of 2000 rpm for 40 s, and then a chloroform solution containing different ratios of semiconductor polymer/PS was spin-coated onto the PVA-treated Si substrate at a speed of 4000 rpm at room temperature for 40 s. The PS concentration was fixed at 20 mg ml−1, and the concentration of the PBIBDF-BT solution was changed according to the blending ratio. The prepared films were placed on the water surface, and then films were reversed using CYTOP-treated SiO2/Si substrates to take the films from the water. After etching with ethyl acetate to remove PS for 30 s, the ultrathin films were annealed at 150 °C for about 15 min under a nitrogen atmosphere. Finally, the source and drain electrodes (W = 1000 µm, L = 100 µm) was formed by depositing a gold layer on the films using the shadow mask. The surface morphologies and the thickness of the polymer ultrathin films were investigated using AFM (Nanoscope, Veeco Instrument, Inc.).
