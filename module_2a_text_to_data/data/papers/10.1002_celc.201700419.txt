Abstract
Semiconductor-based biosensors are suitable for the detection of small molecules such as glucose as long as they have molecular charges. However, biological samples include various ionic molecules such as proteins; thus, their nonspecific adsorption should be prevented to reduce background noise. In this study, we optimized a field-effect transistor (FET)-based glucose sensor, that is, a glucose transistor, for more precise and highly sensitive glucose sensing. Using the glucose transistor modified with 4-mercaptophenylboronic acid (PBA) on a gold gate electrode, we found that the detection sensitivity to glucose was increased to approximately 60 mV/decade from about 40 μM. This means that the PBA on the gate induced a diol-binding site with glucose through an equilibrium reaction, resulting in an increase in the density of negative charges in the PBA-glucose conjugate. Moreover, an oligoethyleneglycol (OEG) monolayer was coated on the gate as a molecular sieve interface so that the PBA molecules were embowered in the OEG membrane, but low-molecular-weight glucose could pass between the OEG molecules and reach the PBA molecules at the gate. The detection sensitivity of the biosensor with the optimized glucose transistor to glucose remained, to some extent, even in a solution including albumin. Thus, a platform based on the optimized glucose transistor is available for the practical use of a novel sensor that can detect glucose even at the low concentrations included in biological fluids.

1 Introduction
In recent years, the number of diabetic patients has rapidly increased worldwide and many approaches have emerged for establishing diagnostic glucose sensors and systems. Most glucose-sensing devices utilize the enzymatic reaction between glucose oxidase (GOD) and glucose owing to the high selectivity, convenience and low cost, the detection principle of which is enough to analyze a glucose level of about 100 mg/dl (about 5.5 mM) in blood.1-3 However, this principle has several disadvantages; commercially available glucose sensors need blood sampling, which is accompanied by pain to measure blood glucose, and their detection sensitivity based on this principle is insufficient at low glucose levels in biological fluids such as sweat, tears, and saliva, which are focused on as samples for noninvasive glucose measurement instead of blood samples.

To detect glucose at low concentrations in such biological fluids, we need to develop some biosensing methods as alternatives to methods using conventional glucose sensors. For example, high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) can be utilized to detect glucose molecules at low concentrations,4, 5 but this method requires expensive and non-portable systems unlike commercial glucose sensors. Some properties required for future glucose sensors include ease of use with portability, a low cost, and no requirement for blood sampling. Therefore, more highly sensitive and precise devices should be developed in a portable format, because the glucose levels in biological fluids such as tears, [6–11] saliva,12, 13 and sweat14 are about one-tenth to one-hundredth of those in blood.

Field effect transistor (FET)-based biosensing systems have been proposed for some applications such as label-free DNA sequencing,15-17 immunological assay18 and cellular functional analysis.19-21 In particular, FET-based biosensors are suitable for the detection of small molecules such as glucose as long as they have molecular charges. In the case of saccharide sensing, the coating of phenylboronic acid (PBA) derivatives on the gate surface is effective for the detection of low-molecular-weight saccharides such as glucose based on the principle of FETs, because the binding of PBA molecules with saccharide increases the density of negative charges in their conjugate on the basis of the equilibrium reaction.22-26 However, the sensing surface needs to be improved by modifying functional molecules to enhance the signal-to-noise (S/N) ratio when measuring electrically charged small molecules in biological fluids that include impurities with charges. As one of the surface modifications, the molecular sieve effect is available for filtering of impurities on the sensor surface. This is because even small molecules are detectable for FET-based biosensors if only they have intrinsic molecular charges, and they can easily approach the surface by passing through molecular sieve molecules, whereas impurities such as proteins are blocked by the filtering layer. Moreover, semiconductor technologies should meet the prospective demand for glucose sensors by enabling the production of miniaturized and arrayed devices using conventional semiconductor processes, thus realizing portable devices with multiple functions.

In this study, we optimized a FET-based glucose sensor, i. e., glucose transistor, to monitor change in glucose concentration on the order of μM, which is expected to contribute to biological fluid sensing in the future. In particular, we focused on suppressing the nonspecific adsorption of proteins such as albumin, that is, increasing the S/N ratio, resulting in the more highly sensitive and precise detection of low-molecular-weight glucose.

Experimental Section
Surface Modification of Extended Au Gate from FET Device
A glass ring well was first attached to an Au gate electrode using an epoxy resin, and the Au gate electrode was cleaned for 30 min by UV radiation. The sensing area was kept at 0.5 cm2 (r=0.4 cm). It was then immersed in an ethanol solution including 1 mM PBA overnight at 25 °C. The modified Au gate surface was washed with an ethanol solution and ultrapure water.

Measurement of Sensitivity to Glucose
We evaluated the sensitivity of the prepared glucose transistors to glucose using a semiconductor parameter analyzer (B1500 A, Agilent). We prepared 10 mM glucose in a phosphate-buffered solution (PBS; 25 mM Na2HPO4 and 25 mM KH2PO4, Wako). It was then added to the glass ring well filled with the buffer solution to change the glucose concentration from about 70 μM to 2 mM. The gate voltage (VG) – drain current (ID) electrical characteristics were measured at various glucose concentrations. The change in VG as a result of PBA-glucose binding was examined as the shift in the threshold voltage (VT), and the correlation between the VT shift and the glucose concentration was analyzed. The VT shift was measured at a constant ID of 1 or 2 mA and a drain voltage (VD) of 1 V. Moreover, the change in the surface potential (ΔVout) at the gate was measured at a constant ID (1 mA) and VD (1 V) using the source follower circuit shown in Figure S1(a); thus, the detected ΔVout was regarded as the change in the source-gate voltage (ΔVS), which was equal to −ΔVT. Using this system, the surface potential of the FET can be monitored in real time. All the measurements were performed at pH 7.0.

Prevention of Nonspecific Adsorption for Optimized Glucose Transistor
To reduce the non-specific adsorption of proteins, the optimized glucose transistor for protein blocking was fabricated utilizing an oligoethyleneglycol (OEG; hydroxyl-6-ethyleneglycol (EG6)-undecanethiol, Dojindo). In this case, the Au gate electrode was immersed in a mixed solution of PBA and OEG. PBA and OEG were mixed in a ratio of 1:9 at concentrations of 100 and 900 μM, respectively.

To confirm the effect of protein blocking on the electrical signal, the VT shift was compared between two types of transistor; one was the optimized glucose transistor with the OEG/PBA mixed monolayer and the other was the original glucose transistor with the PBA monolayer. To evaluate the sensitivity of the biosensors with glucose transistors to glucose in samples including albumin, 10 mM glucose was added to the glass ring well filled with the buffer solution to change the glucose concentration from about 70 μM to 2 mM including 4 g/l albumin. The correlation between the VT shift and the glucose concentration was also analyzed for the optimized glucose transistor.

2 Results and Discussion
2.1 Detection Sensitivity of Glucose Transistor
Figure 1 shows the principle of detection by the glucose transistor. The principle is based on the potentiometric detection of changes in the charge density at the gate insulator, on which the specific binding between glucose and probe molecules occurs for molecular recognition. The PBA derivatives were modified on the basis of the thiol group-Au covalent binding on the sensing surface of the gate. That is, the Au gate electrode of the extended-gate FET was employed to utilize this convenient modification process.26, 27 The gate surface of the glucose transistor was immersed in a measurement solution together with a Ag/AgCl reference electrode. Basically, ionic or molecular charges at the gate interact electrostatically with electrons in silicon crystals through the thin gate insulator and induce electrical signals owing to the field effect, resulting in a change in ID change at the channel. Eventually, the change in the charge density caused by the binding of glucose to PBA can be detected as the VT shift of the glucose transistor at a constant ID because the PBA derivatives change from the neutral trigonal form to the anionic tetrahedral form. The PBA derivatives form a reversible covalent complex with 1,2- or 1,3-diol, and the adjacent cis diol of various saccharides normally forms a cyclic ester with boronic acid.28 In general, the diol binding to boronic acid decreases the pKa of boron-containing functional groups.29, 30 As boronic acid is a Lewis acid, it can react with a water molecule and change from the neutral trigonal form (1) to the anionic tetrahedral form (2), as shown in Scheme 1. The same reaction is also true for the diol-boronic complex or the boronate ester (3), which reacts with a water molecule to become anionic (4). As mentioned above, the pKa of the boronate ester (pKa2) is lower than that of boronic acid (pKa1), and the density of negative charges increases in proportion to the concentration of diols. Therefore, we can monitor the concentration change of glucose by detecting the change in the density of negative charges using the FET-based glucose transistor chemically modified by PBA molecules.

2.2 Preventive Effect of OEG on Nonspecific Adsorption of Proteins
Biological fluids such as sweat, saliva, and tears include a lot of components other than glucose such as proteins, sodium chloride, and urea, although their concentrations are lower than those in blood.35 The background noise caused by these components needs to be removed to specifically detect glucose molecules. The effect of proteins such as albumin on the electrical response was evaluated using the original glucose transistor, but the glucose response was almost lost in the buffer solution, as shown in Figure 4. Albumin was added at a concentration of 4 g/l, which is reported to be the approximate concentration in tears, for example.35 It is well known that albumin is easily bound to an Au surface owing to thiol-Au binding derived from cysteine residues of albumin.36-38 The effect of adhesive albumin on electrical signals cannot be neglected when Au electrodes are used, because the thiol-Au binding results in the electrical signals generated by the negative charges (R-S−) introduced into Au by the electrochemical reaction R-SH+Au→R−S-Au+1/2 H2. Therefore, we fabricated a functionalized glucose transistor with an OEG-SAM, which blocks protein adsorption, as shown in Figure 5. In general, most proteins tend to attach onto hydrophobic surfaces but are hardly adsorbed onto hydrophilic surfaces. OEG has some valuable properties as a bio-interface such as its hydrophilic affinity for a solvent due to hydrogen-bonded water molecules, steric hindrance, and high motility. Therefore, it is possible to reduce the non-specific adsorption by utilizing the OEG membrane.39 The effect of protein blocking on the OEG/PBA-modified Au surface is shown in Figure 6. As shown in Figure 6(a), it is obvious that the change in VG for the OEG/PBA-modified Au electrode after the addition of albumin was much smaller than that of the electrode without the OEG. In particular, controlling the OEG/PBA ratio of OEG/PBA-SAM to 9:1 may be effective for protein blocking. The surface characteristics based on OEG were also evaluated in terms of the water contact angle, as shown in Figure 6(b). Depending on the amount of OEG, the OEG membrane induced the hydrophilic surface of the Au gate, resulting in the prevention of protein adsorption. Furthermore, the glucose sensitivity of the optimized glucose transistor with the OEG/PBA ratio of 9:1 recovered to about 20 mV/decade for a concentration change of glucose molecules even in the buffer solution including albumin, as shown in Figure 7, although the sensitivity was slightly lower than that of the glucose transistor in the buffer solution without albumin, as shown in Figure 2. This result showed that the arrangement of OEG/PBA derivatives on the semiconductor-based transistor enhanced its functionalities to reduce the nonspecific adsorption of proteins as well as to detect glucose specifically. The above results suggest that the change in glucose concentration can be monitored effectively even in biological fluids including some proteins using the OEG/PBA-modified gate FET.

2.3 Estimation of Limit of Detection (LOD) and Limit of Quantification (LOQ) for Glucose Transistors
In this study, the LOD and limit of quantification LOQ for glucose when using the glucose transistors were calculated from the single logarithmic plots in the range of about 70 μM to 2 mM shown in Figures 2 and 7, applying the Kaiser limit.42 Table 1 shows the calculated values of LOD and LOQ for glucose using each glucose transistor. Considering the calculated LOD, the OEG/PBA-modified gate FET should have the ability to detect glucose at lower concentrations of 3–4 μM even in a buffer solution including albumin, while LOQ was approximately 45 μM. In a previous work, La Belle et al. calculated LOD and obtained a value of 43.4 μM from the result of experimental measurements in the range of 200 μM to 1 mM using an existing glucose sensor, which was integrated with glucose oxidase and a microfluidic system.43 The enhancement of the detection limit was expected because of the direct detection of molecular charges, without an intervening enzymatic reaction, on the basis of the field effect using the glucose transistor, compared with the improved amperometric and coulometric principle combined with a glass capillary, which enabled the detection of 10 μM glucose at 50 °C and 50 μM glucose at room temperature.44 Therefore, a platform based on the glucose transistor is suitable for sensing glucose with a high S/N ratio at room temperature, even in biological fluids such as tears, in which the glucose levels are about one-tenth to one-hundredth of those in blood (5.5 mM).

3 Conclusions
In this study, we investigated the fundamental properties of a glucose transistor chemically modified by PBA derivatives. In particular, the immobilized density of the PBA membrane, which is closely related to electrical signals, was evaluated on the basis of the VT shift of a FET device. The possibility of a reversible response of the glucose transistor was demonstrated on the basis of the equilibrium reaction of the PBA-glucose conjugate. The glucose transistor showed high sensitivity to glucose from about several tens μM. Furthermore, OEG as the molecular sieve interface played an important role in reducing the nonspecific adsorption of albumin for the glucose transistor. Thus, a platform based on the glucose transistor is suitable for high-S/N-ratio glucose sensing even in biological fluids, which may result in the blood-collection-free glucose monitoring of diabetes patients.
