An extended-gate field-effect transistor (EGFET) is an important device which is studied as a pH sensor. The objective of this research is to characterize and study nanostructured bismuth oxide (Bi2O3) film for pH sensing applications. Bi2O3film was developed in a two-step process through the electron-beam evaporation technique and subsequently by thermal annealing at 150°C in an oxygen atmosphere. The film was characterized using field emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM), energy-dispersive x-ray spectroscopy (EDS), x-ray diffraction (XRD), and x-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS). FESEM characterization revealed that the film is nanostructured, with an average grain size of ~ 100 nm. EDS analysis confirmed the presence of bismuth and oxygen in the sensing film. XRD and XPS characterization techniques were used to confirm the formation of Bi2O3on the bismuth surface. The film showed good pH sensing behavior, with sensitivity of 51.32 mV/pH. The nanostructured bismuth oxide film was analyzed for pH sensing on the EGFET-based pH sensor platform. This study indicates the usefulness of Bi2O3nanostructured film for pH sensing applications.

The development and fabrication of chemical sensors for biological and environmental monitoring applications has been an area of great research interest for more than a decade. Advancements in point-of-care (POC) devices have led to the miniaturization of these sensors.1,2,3Chemical sensors with various metal oxide/nitride sensing layers play a very significant role in medical, industrial, domestic, food processing, and many other applications. pH is a significant biological parameter for human health, and monitoring of pH parameters helps in the diagnosis of various diseases and improves therapeutic treatment.4Continuous measurement of pH in cells is also very important in phagocytosis and endocytosis processes. pH sensors are used for blood pH monitoring, biological and chemical analyses, wastewater monitoring, and clinical detection.5,6,7These sensors should be accurate, cost-effective, reliable, and durable, with ease of maintenance. A large range of electrochemical sensors have been studied for pH measurement.8,9,10In 1970, Bergveld first proposed the ion-sensitive field-effect transistor (ISFET) pH sensor for neurophysiological measurement.11The structure of the ISFET is similar to that of the metal–oxide–semiconductor field-effect transistor (MOSFET), but the metallic gate is replaced by an ion-sensitive membrane which is in direct contact with the pH solution. However, this structure has drawbacks such as low current sensitivity, poor long-term stability, and a complicated fabrication process. In 1983, van der Spiegel et al. introduced the extended-gate field-effect transistor (EGFET) as an alternative for the fabrication of the ISFET.12This configuration has many advantages including low cost, a simpler passivation and packaging process, light and temperature insensitivity, and long-term stability.13,14The working principle of both the ISFET and EGFET is the same; the only difference is that in the EGFET configuration, low-impedance membranes are preferred. The EGFET consists of two parts: a MOSFET and a sensing electrode. The sensing electrode is immersed in buffer solution, which is electrically connected to the gate of the MOSFET for measurements. This configuration allows us to replace only the sensing electrode, instead of a complete device.15,16Since the realization of Ta2O5as a sensing film for EGFET in 1981 by Matsuo et al.,17several researchers have reported different sensing films, such as Al2O3,18Er2O3,19CuO,20ZnO,21and TiO2.22In this study, we have focused on the preparation of nanostructured bismuth oxide film and its application for pH sensing. Bismuth oxide is an important material that is environmentally friendly, low-cost, and abundant in nature.23It is very significant in solid-state technology because of its unique properties including wide bandgap, high refractive index, high dielectric permittivity, and good photoconductivity. Because of these specific properties, it has gained attention in applications such as photocatalysts, fuel cells, gas sensors, oxygen pumps, and ionic conductors.24,25,26Bismuth oxide has been synthesized and developed by deposition techniques such as electrodeposition, chemical bath deposition, chemical vapor deposition, atomic layer deposition, solvothermal deposition, sputtering, and various physical vapor deposition methods.27,28,29,30,31,32,33,34In our earlier work, Na3BiO4-Bi2O3film was developed by the low-cost electrodeposition technique.35In Na3BiO4-Bi2O3film, vertically and horizontally aligned nanostructures were found. It was observed that bismuth oxide formed hydroxyl groups at the surface, which play a key role in pH sensing. These hydroxyl groups, when coming in contact with the electrolyte, either lose or accept protons. As a result, charges develop at the film surface and an electric double layer is formed at the film and electrolyte interface. This phenomenon may be due to the material's nature, the electrolyte's contribution, the electrode–solution interface, and the electrochemical reaction occurring on the electrode.35In the case of Na3BiO4-Bi2O3, sensing was performed only in alkaline medium, as the film starts to degrade below pH 7. To further explore bismuth material, and to overcome the issues with electrodeposition concerning film uniformity and possible impurities, we have adopted the e-beam evaporation technique, which is a well-defined standard technique for the deposition of bismuth oxide film. The present work is a step towards analyzing Bi2O3nanostructured film for pH sensing applications. To the best of our knowledge, there is no available report in the literature on the fabrication of a Bi2O3nanostructured film-based EGFET pH sensors.

The EGFET device fabrication process started with a 3-inch-diameter <100>p-type silicon wafer, having 1–10 Ω cm resistivity and 380 µm thickness. The silicon (Si) wafer was cleaned by the standard RCA cleaning method to remove any contamination and particles present at the surface of the wafer. After cleaning, the thermal oxidation process was carried out at 11,000°C to grow field oxide with thickness of ~ 1 µm in a sequence of 15 min dry, 3 h wet, and 15 min dry oxidation cycles. SiO2film was thermally grown on the silicon as an insulating layer. Subsequently, on the Si/SiO2stack, first a thin film of Ti (200 Å) and then Pt (2000 Å) was deposited by sputtering. The platinum film is a conducting film and acts as an underlying electrode for the sensing film, which transfers the charge developed at the film surface to the gate of the MOSFET.

After platinum deposition, bismuth film with thickness of ~ 2000 Å was deposited by electron beam evaporation from bismuth granules (purity > 99.99%, purchased from HiMedia). The film thickness was measured using the Dektak XT 2D surface profilometer (Bruker) as shown in Fig.1. The bismuth layer thickness was measured because it affects the film sensitivity and transfers the charge potential from the surface to the underlying electrode. The film deposition was carried out at 8 × 10−7kPa chamber pressure and the voltage and current were fixed at 6 kV and 8 mA, respectively. During pH sensing, the potential at the sensing surface is generated due to protonation, which results in a change in the surface potential of the film.36This surface potential causes an electric field which changes the FET conductance, and hence a change in the drain-to-source current of the connected MOSFET.37An EGFET-based pH sensor consists of an extended electrode. The extended electrode was fabricated using silicon as substrate. On the silicon substrate, SiO2/Ti/Pt/Bi2O3layers were grown/deposited, in which bismuth oxide acts as a pH-sensitive film. When the sensing electrode is immersed in buffer solution, protonation/deprotonation takes place and charge is developed at the film surface. The potential is generated due to the developed charge and transferred to the gate of the MOSFET through the underlying platinum electrode. The potential changes with the pH buffer solution and subsequently causes a change in the drain current of the MOSFET. Thus, the pH sensor indicates the alkaline or acidic status of the analyte. The status of various chemical processes can be correlated with the pH of the analyte; it plays a significant role in various processes like chemical equilibria in biofluids of living organisms, nutrient availability, enzyme activity, and microbial gene expression.38,39,40,41,42,43The present Bi2O3-based pH sensor can be used for blood pH monitoring, which is used as a marker for organ failure and the evolution of chronic wounds.44,45,46It is also an outcome predictor of intensive care unit (ICU) patients suffering from acid–base disorders.47A schematic cross-sectional view of the EGFET device is shown in Fig.2. The as-deposited films were annealed in a 4-inch tube furnace at 150°C in an oxygen atmosphere for 1 h. Annealing was done to form bismuth oxide. Further, the device was diced from the wafer and encapsulated using DuPont®nonconducting epoxy and leaving the sensor area open. This nanostructured Bi2O3sensing film was inspected for pH sensing in different buffer solutions of pH 6, 7, 10, and 12. The sensitivity of the film was analyzed from the transfer characteristics and pH versusVrefgraphs (detailed explanation shown in the “Electrochemical Characterization” section).

The experimental setup for the pH detection of the EGFET sensor with bismuth oxide as a sensing layer is shown in Fig.3. The encapsulated sensing chip is connected to the commercially available MOSFET MC14007UB. The sensing chip and the Ag/AgCl reference electrode (Metrohm) were immersed in buffer solutions (Certipur®, Merck) of pH 6 (citric acid/sodium hydroxide), 7 (disodium hydrogen phosphate/potassium dihydrogen phosphate), 10 (boric acid/potassium chloride/sodium hydroxide), and 12 (disodium hydrogen phosphate/sodium hydroxide) for pH sensing. It was found that Bi2O3-based EGFET showed a good sensing response for pH value 6–12, but the film started degrading when it was immersed in a solution below pH 6. Biocompatibility and good pH sensitivity make this Bi2O3film suitable for various EGFET-based chemical and biological pH sensors.48The schematic diagram of the electrical characterization setup is shown in Fig.4.

The pH sensing mechanism of the EGFET-based pH sensor can be best explained by the site binding model. The oxide film generally contains hydroxyl groups. According to the site binding model, when the metal oxide film is immersed in an electrolyte, transfer of charges takes place which can either donate or accept a proton. As a result, different surface sites, O−, OH and OH2+, are formed at the surface of the film as shown in Fig.5.

Structural studies were carried out by x-ray diffraction (XRD, PANalytical powder diffractometer) in a 2-theta range of 20°–80° using a CuKαtarget. Morphological studies were performed by field emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM) using the Nova NanoSEM. Elemental studies were conducted by x-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) using an Omicron instrument in a vacuum greater than 10−9kPa. To avoid charging effects, a C 1sscan was also taken along with Bi 4fand O 1sscans.

XRD studies were carried out to analyze the crystal structure of the prepared film. Sharp and intense peaks of the spectrum represent the polycrystalline nature of the film. The random orientation of planes also shows polycrystalline behavior; however, the most intense peak belongs to the (121) plane. To analyze the peaks, profile fitting was done in the obtained spectrum. In Fig.8, the red solid line shows the fitted data and the black dotted line shows the raw data. Formation of complete Bi2O3in monoclinic phase and space group P21/c were matched with JCPDS card no. 01-071-0465. The unit cell refined values of the XRD pattern area= 5.8 Å,b= 8.1 Å, andc= 7.5 Å andα= 90°,β= 112.97°, andϒ= 90°. XRD is a bulk sensitive technique; to analyze chemical bonding and the elemental compositions, XPS was performed.

XRD spectra of Bi2O3nanostructured film.

XPS studies were performed to analyze the elemental and chemical composition of bismuth oxide. The obtained data were deconvoluted to identify the individual peaks. After deconvolution of the spectra, three pairs of peaks were observed. The first pair of peaks observed at binding energy of 163.6 eV and 158.3 eV corresponded to Bi2O3; these peak positions are in good correspondence with the results of Gupta et al.50The second pair appeared at 162.16 eV and 156.6 eV, corresponding to Bi0, and the highest peaks at 161.5 eV and 156.1 eV corresponding to elemental bismuth of Bi 4fedge, 4f5/2,and 4f7/2, respectively.51The oxygen 1speak was also deconvoluted, in which two peaks appeared at 529.1 eV and 531.2 eV corresponding to oxygen 1sand hydroxyl group, respectively.51The chemical composition was confirmed by XPS, which confirmed the formation of Bi2O3and the presence of the hydroxyl group on the surface. The EGFET was tested for pH sensing for different pH values. By changing the reference voltage, constant voltage and constant current measurements were taken for the different pH solutions. The transfer characteristics of the sensor are shown in Fig.10. Here,VdsandIds(µA) were fixed, and voltage (Vref) was swept from 0 V to 3 V. The sensitivity of the device was calculated using linear fitting and is shown in Fig.11. Sensitivity of 51.32 mV/pH was observed. The pH sensitivity of the nanostructured Bi2O3sensing film is considerably better than that of the other sensing films. Various research groups have demonstrated several sensing films for pH sensing. The comparison of different sensing films is shown in TableI. In this study, Bi2O3nanostructured sensing film was developed by e-beam evaporation. The higher sensitivity is attributed to the nanostructured morphology of Bi2O3. It is clear from the FESEM results that the film is nanostructured. Nanostructures have a high surface-to-volume ratio and hence have a greater number of binding sites, which increases the pH sensitivity. As compared to the other films discussed in the table, the developed nanostructured Bi2O3film shows good pH sensitivity of 51.32 mV/pH. Bismuth oxide is also a biocompatible material, so it can be used for biosensing applications such as blood pH monitoring, which is a marker for the diagnosis of various human health problems.

AbstractAn extended-gate field-effect transistor (EGFET) is an important device which is studied as a pH sensor. The objective of this research is to characterize and study nanostructured bismuth oxide (Bi2O3) film for pH sensing applications. Bi2O3film was developed in a two-step process through the electron-beam evaporation technique and subsequently by thermal annealing at 150°C in an oxygen atmosphere. The film was characterized using field emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM), energy-dispersive x-ray spectroscopy (EDS), x-ray diffraction (XRD), and x-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS). FESEM characterization revealed that the film is nanostructured, with an average grain size of ~ 100 nm. EDS analysis confirmed the presence of bismuth and oxygen in the sensing film. XRD and XPS characterization techniques were used to confirm the formation of Bi2O3on the bismuth surface. The film showed good pH sensing behavior, with sensitivity of 51.32 mV/pH. The nanostructured bismuth oxide film was analyzed for pH sensing on the EGFET-based pH sensor platform. Advancements in point-of-care (POC) devices have led to the miniaturization of these sensors.1,2,3Chemical sensors with various metal oxide/nitride sensing layers play a very significant role in medical, industrial, domestic, food processing, and many other applications. pH is a significant biological parameter for human health, and monitoring of pH parameters helps in the diagnosis of various diseases and improves therapeutic treatment.4Continuous measurement of pH in cells is also very important in phagocytosis and endocytosis processes. pH sensors are used for blood pH monitoring, biological and chemical analyses, wastewater monitoring, and clinical detection.5,6,7These sensors should be accurate, cost-effective, reliable, and durable, with ease of maintenance. A large range of electrochemical sensors have been studied for pH measurement.8,9,10In 1970, Bergveld first proposed the ion-sensitive field-effect transistor (ISFET) pH sensor for neurophysiological measurement.11The structure of the ISFET is similar to that of the metal–oxide–semiconductor field-effect transistor (MOSFET), but the metallic gate is replaced by an ion-sensitive membrane which is in direct contact with the pH solution. However, this structure has drawbacks such as low current sensitivity, poor long-term stability, and a complicated fabrication process. In 1983, van der Spiegel et al. introduced the extended-gate field-effect transistor (EGFET) as an alternative for the fabrication of the ISFET.12This configuration has many advantages including low cost, a simpler passivation and packaging process, light and temperature insensitivity, and long-term stability.13,14The working principle of both the ISFET and EGFET is the same; the only difference is that in the EGFET configuration, low-impedance membranes are preferred. The EGFET consists of two parts: a MOSFET and a sensing electrode. The sensing electrode is immersed in buffer solution, which is electrically connected to the gate of the MOSFET for measurements. This configuration allows us to replace only the sensing electrode, instead of a complete device.15,16Since the realization of Ta2O5as a sensing film for EGFET in 1981 by Matsuo et al.,17several researchers have reported different sensing films, such as Al2O3,18Er2O3,19CuO,20ZnO,21and TiO2.22In this study, we have focused on the preparation of nanostructured bismuth oxide film and its application for pH sensing. Bismuth oxide is an important material that is environmentally friendly, low-cost, and abundant in nature.23It is very significant in solid-state technology because of its unique properties including wide bandgap, high refractive index, high dielectric permittivity, and good photoconductivity. Because of these specific properties, it has gained attention in applications such as photocatalysts, fuel cells, gas sensors, oxygen pumps, and ionic conductors.24,25,26Bismuth oxide has been synthesized and developed by deposition techniques such as electrodeposition, chemical bath deposition, chemical vapor deposition, atomic layer deposition, solvothermal deposition, sputtering, and various physical vapor deposition methods.27,28,29,30,31,32,33,34In our earlier work, Na3BiO4-Bi2O3film was developed by the low-cost electrodeposition technique.35In Na3BiO4-Bi2O3film, vertically and horizontally aligned nanostructures were found. It was observed that bismuth oxide formed hydroxyl groups at the surface, which play a key role in pH sensing. These hydroxyl groups, when coming in contact with the electrolyte, either lose or accept protons. As a result, charges develop at the film surface and an electric double layer is formed at the film and electrolyte interface. This phenomenon may be due to the material's nature, the electrolyte's contribution, the electrode–solution interface, and the electrochemical reaction occurring on the electrode.35In the case of Na3BiO4-Bi2O3, sensing was performed only in alkaline medium, as the film starts to degrade below pH 7. To further explore bismuth material, and to overcome the issues with electrodeposition concerning film uniformity and possible impurities, we have adopted the e-beam evaporation technique, which is a well-defined standard technique for the deposition of bismuth oxide film. The present work is a step towards analyzing Bi2O3nanostructured film for pH sensing applications. To the best of our knowledge, there is no available report in the literature on the fabrication of a Bi2O3nanostructured film-based EGFET pH sensors.Experimental DetailsThe EGFET device fabrication process started with a 3-inch-diameter <100>p-type silicon wafer, having 1–10 Ω cm resistivity and 380 µm thickness. The silicon (Si) wafer was cleaned by the standard RCA cleaning method to remove any contamination and particles present at the surface of the wafer. After cleaning, the thermal oxidation process was carried out at 11,000°C to grow field oxide with thickness of ~ 1 µm in a sequence of 15 min dry, 3 h wet, and 15 min dry oxidation cycles. SiO2film was thermally grown on the silicon as an insulating layer. Subsequently, on the Si/SiO2stack, first a thin film of Ti (200 Å) and then Pt (2000 Å) was deposited by sputtering. The platinum film is a conducting film and acts as an underlying electrode for the sensing film, which transfers the charge developed at the film surface to the gate of the MOSFET.After platinum deposition, bismuth film with thickness of ~ 2000 Å was deposited by electron beam evaporation from bismuth granules (purity > 99.99%, purchased from HiMedia). The film thickness was measured using the Dektak XT 2D surface profilometer (Bruker) as shown in Fig.1. The bismuth layer thickness was measured because it affects the film sensitivity and transfers the charge potential from the surface to the underlying electrode. The film deposition was carried out at 8 × 10−7kPa chamber pressure and the voltage and current were fixed at 6 kV and 8 mA, respectively. During pH sensing, the potential at the sensing surface is generated due to protonation, which results in a change in the surface potential of the film.36This surface potential causes an electric field which changes the FET conductance, and hence a change in the drain-to-source current of the connected MOSFET.37An EGFET-based pH sensor consists of an extended electrode. The extended electrode was fabricated using silicon as substrate. On the silicon substrate, SiO2/Ti/Pt/Bi2O3layers were grown/deposited, in which bismuth oxide acts as a pH-sensitive film. When the sensing electrode is immersed in buffer solution, protonation/deprotonation takes place and charge is developed at the film surface. The potential is generated due to the developed charge and transferred to the gate of the MOSFET through the underlying platinum electrode. The potential changes with the pH buffer solution and subsequently causes a change in the drain current of the MOSFET. Thus, the pH sensor indicates the alkaline or acidic status of the analyte. The status of various chemical processes can be correlated with the pH of the analyte; it plays a significant role in various processes like chemical equilibria in biofluids of living organisms, nutrient availability, enzyme activity, and microbial gene expression.38,39,40,41,42,43The present Bi2O3-based pH sensor can be used for blood pH monitoring, which is used as a marker for organ failure and the evolution of chronic wounds.44,45,46It is also an outcome predictor of intensive care unit (ICU) patients suffering from acid–base disorders.47A schematic cross-sectional view of the EGFET device is shown in Fig.2. The as-deposited films were annealed in a 4-inch tube furnace at 150°C in an oxygen atmosphere for 1 h. Annealing was done to form bismuth oxide. Further, the device was diced from the wafer and encapsulated using DuPont®nonconducting epoxy and leaving the sensor area open. This nanostructured Bi2O3sensing film was inspected for pH sensing in different buffer solutions of pH 6, 7, 10, and 12.
