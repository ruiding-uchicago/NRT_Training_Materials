Sensing layers of pH extended-gated field effect transistor sensors were fabricated by spraying multi-wall carbon nanotubes (MWCNTs) on In2O3coated glass substrate and then were undergone oxygen plasma treatment by microwave chemical vapor deposition. The pH-sensitivity of the sensors increases as a function of oxygen plasma treatment duration. The Raman and X-ray spectra analysis showed that the chemical composition of the O2plasma-treated MWCNTs/In2O3surface depends on the plasma conditions used. The influence of the different plasma conditions versus the sensitivity of pH sensors has also been studied. Furthermore, the treatment time period can operate more efficiently by increasing flow rate of oxygen during plasma treatment.

The sensors for pH detection such as the optical-fiber-based pH sensors, mass-sensitive pH sensor, metal oxide pH sensors, conducting polymer pH sensors, ion-sensitive field effect transistors (ISFETs)-based pH sensors, pH-image sensor, have already developed in the past. pH sensors which are usually made by using semiconducting oxides as the sensing material are important devices that can be employed in control of chemical processes, medical applications, and environment [1]. Among them, the development of ISFET is a relatively new technique in pH measurement which is as an alternative to the glass electrode. Many works have been reported novel designs of glass-less and small-sized sensor for inexpensive, portable pH measurement system [2,3]. Recently, high sensing performances of gateless ISFETs with minimized structure were reported [4–6].

Besides, an EGFET which is another structure to isolate FET from the chemical environment has a lot of advantages, such as light insensitivity, simple to package, flexibility of shape of the extended gate area. Moreover, Many studies have focused on the use of metal oxides in the sensing membranes of EGFET such as tin oxide (SnO2) [7], zinc oxide (ZnO) [8], Titanium dioxide (TiO2) [9], ruthenium oxide (RuO2) [10] and tungsten trioxide (WO3) [11]. Currently, nano materials play an important role as active material in semiconductor pH sensors due to their high surface-to-volume ratio [12]. MWCNTs which have outside walls and central hollow cores also can induce very large surface area [13]. Since their discovery in 1991 by Iijima [14], various methods have been developed to produce carbon nanotubes, including pyrolysis [15], laser vaporisation [16], arc discharge [17], and chemical vapor deposition [18]. Moreover, modification of Carbon nanotubes [19,20] and their applications such as electronic devices [21], gas sensors [22], and conductivity enhancement [23] also have been reported. However, plasma treatments provide a wide range of various functional groups depending on plasma parameters such as pressure, gases used, power and duration of treatment.

In this paper, O2plasma has been introduced for MWCNTs/In2O3sensing layer treatment by using microwave CVD with various plasma conditions. The MWCNTs/In2O3composite film was achieved by spraying MWCNTs on the In2O3coated glass substrate. The current–voltage measurement of the pH sensor was performed at room temperature. Furthermore, sensitivity of the EGFET to pH was carried out from the change of resistance with solution concentration.

The fabrication process of the sprayed MWCNTs-based pH sensor is depicted in Fig.1. As seen in the fabrication process, deposition of Indium thin films on glass substrates was first performed at room temperature (RT) in a pure Ar (99.999 %) ambient using a 2 inch 99.99 % purity Indium ceramic target by (radio frequency) RF magnetron sputtering for 1 h. The chamber base pressure was approximately 2.0 × 10−2torr. The flow rate of Ar gas was 20 sccm and the RF power was 100 W. Note that, the target was pre-sputtered with DC power of 20 W for 30 minimums before deposition.

Fabrication process flow for the oxygen plasma treatment of MWCNTs/In2O3sensing layer

Then a 2 mg of as received MWCNTs (Iljin Nanotech Co. Ltd., average diameter 30 nm) powder with 10 mg of dispersant (Type: PVP K30) was ultrasonically dispersed in 10 ml of anhydrous ethanol for 30 min. The MWCNT suspension was then sprayed on the Indium coated glass substrate maintained at 90 °C for 40 min with a portable spray gun connected to an air compressor.

Following MWCNTs deposition, the composite thin film sample was sectioned into 10 small pieces and followed by O2plasma treatment at seven different time periods: 200 s, 300 s, 350 s, 400 s, 450 s, 500 s and 600 s, respectively. The typical plasma treatment parameters were 40 sccm O2flow rate, 25 °C substrate temperature, 1,600 W RF power, and 10 torr. Figure2shows a schematic of the experimental set-up of microwave CVD for plasma treatment of the composite thin film samples used in this study. The samples were loaded into the chamber separately. The desired O2plasma flow rate and RF power were obtained by using a computer-controlled system. Following the plasma treatment effect characterizations, we fabricated EGFET devices on a glass substrate using MWCNTs/In2O3thin film to isolate FET from an occasionally severe chemical environment. There are two parts of the EGFET: one is a sensing structure containing the surface of the sensitive layer and the other is a Metal–Oxide–Semiconductor Field-Effect Transistor (MOSFET) structure. The sensing layer was then connected to a silver wire and encapsulated using epoxy, keeping provision for a 5 mm × 5 mm sensing window. This layer was connected to the gate of the commercially available n-type MOSFET (FET IC4007). The n-type MOSFET was connected to a Keithley 237 current–voltage meter. Note that the drain-source voltage (VDS) was maintained to be constant at 0.3 V and the reference electrode voltage was increased from 0 to 3 V while the drain current was measured. Schematic diagram of the measurement system is shown in Fig.3.

Figure6a shows the Raman spectra of MWCNTs/In2O3sensing layer before and after oxygen plasma treatment. It can be seen clearly that two sharp peaks spectra show at approximately 1,325 cm−1(D band) and 1,596 cm−1(G band). As we know, the D band is due to disordered or amorphous-phase carbon, and the G band is due to graphite or ordered-phase carbon [29–31], which means that the structure of carbon nanotube can be modified by oxygen plasma treatment with varying treatment time period. The intensity ratio of the D to G bands (ID/IG) analysis was performed in order to evaluate the degree of perfection of the MWCNTs. Figure6b shows the intensity ratios of the D band over the G band for the MWCNTs. It was found that the ID/IGvalue increased dramatically while the plasma treatment time period increased to 400 s, and then slightly increased as treatment time over 400 s. It was also found that all the ID/IGvalues of MWCNTs after oxygen plasma treatment are higher than that before oxygen plasma treatment. These phenomena imply again that the structure of carbon nanotube can be modified by oxygen plasma treatment. Owing to the ions of the O2plasmas have high energy and orientation during the plasma treatment, they can turn the structure of some carbon nanotubes into carbon particles or amorphous carbon [32]. This means that the oxygen plasma treatment creates defects on the surfaces of carbon nanotubes.

Figure7shows the X-ray diffraction (XRD) patterns for the samples before and after oxygen plasma treatment for different times at 1,600 W. The peak near 284 eV in the spectrum is generated by photoelectrons emitted from the C1s core level. The peak near 533 eV is generated by photoelectrons emitted from the O1s core level. These spectra depict the presence of carbon and oxygen in the sample. Such a result indicates that the longer oxygen plasma treatment time period may effectively generate oxygen species on MWCNTs [33,34].

For the purpose of confirmation, any possible oxygen species generation on MWCNTs due to the atmosphere, we further studied plasma treatment effect by increasing gas flow rate to 200 sccm. Table2depicts the influence of plasma treatment time period on MWCNT/In2O3surfaces at a gas flow rate of 200 sccm, plasma power of 1,600 W, and pressure of 10 torr. It can be seen that the sensitivity of pH sensor is 32.34 mV/pH at the plasma treatment time period of 300 s, and thereafter slightly decreases. This means that the oxidation of MWCNT/In2O3by plasma can be achieved more rapidly by tuning the flow rate. Such a result indicates that higher oxygen flow rate during plasma treatment may effectively generate oxygen species on MWCNTs without significantly decreasing the sensitivity of pH sensor. For the industrial application of MWCNT/In2O3treatment with plasma, saving time is crucial.

In this study, the MWCNT/In2O3sensing layer of EGFETs was synthesized on glass substrates and the influence of the oxygen plasma treatment on the pH sensing characteristics of MWCNT/In2O3composite films was investigated. Experimental results showed that the pH sensitivity of MWCNTs/In2O3materials before oxygen plasma treatment is poor and that the MWCNTs/In2O3materials after oxygen plasma treatment have good pH sensitivities of 33.94 mV/pH at 450 s of treatment time period. It was proved that the pH sensitivity of MWCNTs/In2O3materials was greatly improved by oxygen plasma treatment. Furthermore, the treatment time period can operate more efficiently by increasing flow rate of oxygen during plasma treatment.
