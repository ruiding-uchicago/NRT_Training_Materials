Abstract
In this work, hydrothermal (HT)-synthesized gallium oxide (Ga2O3) nanorods were applied on indium tin oxide (ITO) glass substrates to create a dual-functional pH-glucose sensing device. Extended-gate field-effect transistors (EGFETs) were designed for pH and glucose sensing, and the response to pH levels ranging from 1 to 11 was tested using both chemical bath deposition (CBD) and high-pressure hydrothermal methods. Two sensor configurations, Ga2O3(CBD)/ITO/glass and Ga2O3(HT)/ITO/glass, were immersed in solution pH1 to 11 and cultured medium for hydrogen ion and glucose sensing. The results demonstrated that, for pH sensing in different samples, both IDS-VDS analysis and IDS-VGS analysis showed superior linearity and sensitivity with the HT method. For glucose detection, the HT method was selected for further experiments due to its maintained concentration-dependent relationship and selectivity towards various common sugars. When using culture medium DMEM as the sample (4.5 mg/ml glucose concentration), the accuracy of glucose estimation reached 99%. Stability and biocompatibility tests were also conducted, and the results indicated consistent performance. In summary, the HT-synthesized Ga2O3 nanorods exhibited high sensitivity, selectivity, and stability without affecting cell growth. Therefore, they hold great potential for applications not only in general blood glucose sensing but also in studying cell microenvironment metabolism.

1. Introduction
With the advancements in technology and the improvement of economic conditions in the 21st century, there has been a notable enhancement in people's quality of life. This progress in material civilization and extended human lifespan has had an impact on dietary habits [1]. The consumption of refined and diverse diets, coupled with excessive intake of meat, fat, alcohol, and high-sodium foods, can lead to a decrease in blood pH and an increase in sodium ion concentration, thereby increasing the risk of diseases such as cancer and heart disease [2], [3], [4], [5]. As life expectancy continues to rise, the focus of healthcare has shifted towards chronic diseases and preventive medicine. Early detection and real-time monitoring of various diseases can be achieved through biomedical sensors, which play a crucial role in maintaining a high-quality life [6], [7], [8]. Consequently, the development of highly sensitive, miniaturized biomedical sensing components and systems capable of conducting multiple physiological parameter tests represents both a challenge and an opportunity in the field of medical testing [9], [10].
According to the World Health Organization, diabetes, a prevalent chronic disease, currently affects over 420 million individuals worldwide. This condition is widespread in both developing and developed countries, particularly in middle-income regions like Taiwan. Diabetes can be categorized into two types based on the main symptoms: Type I, characterized by insulin deficiency, and Type II, characterized by reduced insulin utilization. Regardless of the type, strict monitoring of blood glucose levels can delay the onset and progression of diabetes-related complications, significantly reducing mortality rates associated with diabetes [11], [12], [13]. Consequently, the development of an efficient and cost-effective glucose monitoring method is essential. However, the glucose test strips commonly available in the market predominantly rely on enzyme-based approaches, such as glucose oxidase and glucose dehydrogenase [14], [15]. The main drawbacks of these strips are their high cost and limited shelf life (approximately 3–6 months) [16]. The objective of this study is to develop a non-enzymatic glucose sensor based on gallium oxide nanorods.
The domain of chemical sensors encompasses a wide array of applications, such as ion sensing, electrochemical detection, biological sensing, and gas detection. Significant advancements have been made in the field of chemical sensors, especially in the fields of biochemical detection, high-throughput screening and microfluidics. In recent years, several sensing membranes have been developed for pH and chemical detection purposes [17]. Metal-oxide materials [18], [19], [20] have been widely studied for pH sensing applications due to their excellent electrical and pH sensing properties. Since these sensors require immersion in liquids, the extended gate field-effect transistor (EGFET) method is an ideal approach to prevent contamination and interference. T The approach in this study involves carefully packaging the sensor probe and immersing it into the test sample, where it acts as a gate connected to the back-end Metal Oxide Semiconductor Field-Effect Transistor (MOSFET). This configuration is referred to as an Extended-Gate Field-Effect Transistor (EGFET). This method effectively extends the lifespan of the sensor probe, reduces interference and leakage, and lowers costs, making it a favorable option for biomedical sensing [21], [22]. The objective of this study is to develop a device capable of long-term immersion in cell culture medium to investigate cell metabolism within the environment. The use of conventional ion-sensitive field-effect transistors (ISFETs) poses a higher risk of damage and requires stricter packaging requirements, as well as potential instability with more structures immersed in the sample. Therefore, an Extended-Gate Field-Effect Transistor (EGFET) was chosen as the dual-sensing mode for pH and glucose measurements [6], [23], [24]. Over the past 20–30 years, nanomaterials have experienced rapid development and found numerous applications. In the field of sensing, it has been discovered that nanomaterials, due to their high surface-to-volume ratio, are more suitable for capturing ions and molecules compared to planar structures [25]. Furthermore, the nanomaterial-based sensing probe, in combination with the subsequent EGFET measurement module, is expected to serve as a promising platform for future detection, characterized by low cost and high sensitivity.
Gallium oxide can be prepared using various methods, each with its own advantages and disadvantages [26], [27], [28]. Based on previous research findings, it has been established that compared to other sensing materials such as SnO2, IrO2, AZO, etc. [29], [30], [31], Ga2O3 possesses several advantageous properties. These include high thermal stability, long-term stability, high breakdown electric field, high electron mobility, wide bandgap, and most importantly, biocompatibility. These attributes are crucial for the development of biosensors. As a result, numerous studies have been conducted to explore the application of various forms of gallium oxide in the field of sensing. In previous studies, the material characteristics using the chemical deposition method (CBD) have been extensively investigated. In this research, we build upon this foundation and employ the hydrothermal method to reduce the preparation time and focus on the application of gallium oxide sensors for glucose detection [28]. These chemical synthesis methods allow for the production of Ga2O3 nanoparticles with varying structures, resulting in different surface area-to-volume ratios and aspect ratios [32], [33]. We also observed that the different fabrication methods can influence the nanoscale structure, which, in turn, affects the electrical and optical sensing properties of the material. Ga2O3 was selected as the sensor material due to its superior performance in pH and glucose sensing. In this study, we applied this sensor for precise glucose detection and have prepared it as a potential tool for future microenvironmental research.
2. Result and discussion
2.1. Fabrication of Ga2O3 films on glass substrates with ITO coating
In this study, to fabricate a stable device capable of detecting pH values and glucose concentrations, we utilized glass as the substrate based on previous experimental conditions. After acid washing, electron gun evaporation was employed to deposit ITO as the conductive layer, while gallium oxide was used for the sensing layer deposition. [28]. Briefly, the glass substrate undergoes a thorough cleaning using the RCA process and is then dried with a nitrogen gun. An electron beam evaporation deposition system (Peva-400e) is utilized for the deposition of ITO. The following are the deposition conditions: ITO target material composed of 95% In2O3 and 5% SnO2; O2 flow rate of 9 sccm; working pressure of 5 × 10–4 torr; temperature of 270 °C; and a heater setting of 7.47 KV.
In this study, Ga2O3 nanorods were deposited on the ITO glass prepared in the above-mentioned experiments. Based on previous research, the chemical bath deposition (CBD) method and the Hydrothermal (HT) method were employed for nanorods growth, and subsequent comparisons were made. The drug ratios for both synthesis methods were set at a 1:1 proportion of gallium nitrate hydrate and hexamethylenetetramine (HMT) mixed uniformly as precursors under the same conditions. In the CBD group, the ITO glass was immersed in the mixed solution and heated at 95 °C for 1 h, while the HT group utilized a high-pressure autoclave, heated to 140 °C for 30 min. The schematic representation of the synthesis is shown in Fig. 1. The following are the chemical reaction equations for the synthesis: [34]:
(1)C6H12N4 + 6 H2O → 6HCHO + 4NH3
(2)NH3 + H2O → NH4+ + OH-
(3)2 Ga(NO3)3 + 3 H2O → 2 Ga3+ + 6OH- + 3 N2O5
(4)N2O5 → NO2(g) + NO(g) + O2(g)
(5)3OH- + Ga3+ → Ga(OH)3
(6)Ga(OH)3 → GaOOH + H2O (140 °C)
(7)2GaOOH → Ga2O3 + H2O (annealing 500 °C, 90 min)

After the deposition of Ga2O3 nanorods, they were subjected to annealing at 500 °C for 1 h. Subsequently, the processed samples were cut into 1 cm2 size, and wire connections were made to the conductive layer of the thin film using silver paste. The samples were then placed in an oven at 120 °C for 10 min to allow the silver paste to solidify. Careful encapsulation of the connection points was achieved using epoxy resin, and the samples were further placed in an oven at 120 °C for 20 min to avoid exposing the silver paste to air. Finally, a square area of 1 mm2 was defined on the sample surface as the sensing probe using epoxy resin. The remaining area was completely covered with epoxy resin, and the samples were dried in the oven until fully cured (Fig. 1).

Fig. 2 illustrates the nanostructure variations of different Ga2O3 fabrication processes. From the SEM images, it can be observed that in the HT process (Fig. 2A and S1), after 30 min of deposition time, the number of nanorods structures on the HT surface is greater and more uniformly sized compared to the CBD process (Fig. 2B). This may have an impact on the stability of pH and glucose concentration variations in subsequent steps. Furthermore, EDS analysis reveals that the proportion of Ga2O3 is higher in the HT process (Fig. 2C) compared to the CBD process (Fig. 2D), further confirming that the HT process yields a greater quantity of Ga2O3 nanorods per unit area compared to the CBD process. XRD analysis was employed to illuminate the crystalline conditions of both from an alternative perspective (Fig. S2). In accordance with previous study [35], [36], [37], it can be observed that the crystal structures of both, whether CBD or HT process, belong to the crystalline phase of α-Ga2O3 and exhibit good crystalline orientations on the (104) crystal plane.

2.3. Analysis of IDS-VDS current sensitivity
In this analysis, we utilized the characteristics of a MOSFET to detect changes in pH values. The sensing head was immersed in the test liquid and connected to the gate of the MOSFET. The machine applied a voltage of 3 V to the reference electrode connected on the other side as VGS, while different intensities of VDS (ranging from 0 V to 3 V) were adjusted. (Fig. 3). In Fig. 3A, the IDS-VDS curve of the 2N7000 model MOSFET was initially measured, and it was observed that the IDS gradually increased and then leveled off at around 0.015 A when VDS exceeded 0.6 V. The 2N7000 MOSFET, being a commercially available device, exhibited relative stability, resulting in a smooth IDS-VDS curve. By leveraging this stability, we minimized the impact of instability factors and focused solely on optimizing the sensor film for pH sensing and cell measurements. As the pH concentration increased, the transconductance shifted towards negative values, leading to a gradual flattening of the IDS-VDS curves. We determined the sensitivity of hydrogen ions based on the saturation current from the saturation region. As the concentration of hydrogen ions increased, the saturation current decreased, and we observed a linear relationship. The sensitivity of the observed saturation current variation was calculated using the following formula [38]:
(8)pH current sensitivity = ΔIDS/ΔpH

Fig. 3B illustrates the linear pH current sensitivity of 46 μA/pH with a linearity of 99.5% for the Ga2O3 (HT)/ITO/glass structure, indicating its pH response within the range of pH 1–11. Similarly, the Ga2O3 (CBD)/ITO/glass structure exhibited a pH current sensitivity of 45 μA/pH with a linearity of 99.3% (Fig. 3c). These results indicate that the deposition of gallium oxide facilitates the detection of hydrogen ions by the sensor. From the results, it can be observed that there is little difference in the response of the two different fabrication processes to pH values, with both yielding a sensitivity of approximately 45 μA/pH. Despite the relatively short deposition time in this study (30 min), the sensitivity to pH values is consistent with previous research. This may be attributed to the utilization of saturation curves as the basis for pH sensing, which is more closely related to the crystalline structure rather than the quantity of nanorods. Further exploration is warranted in this regard. Additionally, the high coefficient of determination values, exceeding 0.99, indicate that both fabrication methods exhibit excellent stability in hydrogen ion absorption, making them suitable for pH sensing applications.
However, the stability in DMEM medium differs from that in pH buffer solutions. The right panels of Fig. 3B and 3C represent the stability of Ga2O3 fabricated using the HT and CBD processes in the culture medium. It can be observed that the CBD process exhibits significant fluctuations even after reaching saturation, especially beyond what we anticipated. This may be attributed to interference from glucose in DMEM, leading to an unstable situation. Therefore, for subsequent experiments, we selected Ga2O3 fabricated using the HT process as the functional layer for glucose sensing.
2.4. The analysis of voltage sensitivity
In Fig. 4, we examined the response of the sensing head to pH at different angles. IDs-VGS is another characteristic of the MOSFET, and by observing such features, we can compare the pH sensing capabilities of different Ga2O3 probes produced using different processes. Fig. 4A illustrates the original characteristics of the commercial MOSFET (model 2N7000) used as a reference benchmark in this study. From the results, it can be observed that the response starts at 2 V, and IDS increases linearly until 4 V, after which it reaches a saturated state. Through this mechanism, when the sensing head at the gate reacts with hydrogen ions, it causes a change in gate voltage and alters the current channel. By analyzing the magnitude of these changes, we can infer the pH response characteristics of different Ga2O3 probe fabrication processes.

From Fig. 4B and 4C, the transfer characteristic curves of hydrogen ion capture under the HT and CBD processes can be observed. The gallium oxide fabricated through the HT process exhibits a pH voltage sensing property of 58.6 mV/pH, with a coefficient of determination of 0.98 (Fig. 4B). On the other hand, the gallium oxide fabricated through the CBD process (Fig. 4C) demonstrates a sensitivity of 57.7 mV/pH, with a coefficient of determination also around 0.98. Interestingly, compared to similar literature in the past, the deposition time in the hydrothermal method has a greater impact on the transfer curve than the saturation curve. A difference of 30 min in deposition time resulted in a difference of approximately 10% in the sensing capability. For the occurrence of this phenomenon, it may be caused by many factors. Firstly, compared to the hydrothermal process, the Ga2O3 nanorods produced by the hydrothermal method are observed to have more nanoparticles generated in the same amount of time, which aids in the adsorption of glucose molecules by the Ga2O3 nanorods, leading to a better response. Secondly, from SEM images, it can be observed that the nanostructures of the Ga2O3 nanorods are finer and longer, implying a higher surface area, which enhances the efficiency of glucose sensing. In the right graphs of Fig. 4B and 4C, it can be observed that both samples exhibit smooth curves on the IDS-VGS plot after immersion in DMEM medium, and they have a high linearity in the working region, making them suitable for further glucose sensing applications.
2.5. Hysteresis measurement of Ga2O3 sensor
In previous experiments, it was demonstrated that the Ga2O3/ITO/PET sensor fabricated using two different processes exhibited excellent measurement sensitivity to different pH values. However, when considering a sensor that is intended to be immersed in a liquid, sensitivity to environmental changes becomes a crucial factor. To evaluate the short-term environmental response, this study employed hysteresis measurements by starting at pH 7 and changing the pH every 5 min. The pH cycle followed the sequence: pH 7 - pH 5 - pH 7 - pH 9 - pH 7, completing one cycle within 25 min. Since the pH values within this range are typically encountered in real-world applications, we adjusted the pH range to better align with actual scenarios, compared to the previous measurements of pH 7 - pH 4 - pH 7 - pH 10 - pH 7. First, the hysteresis property of the HT process was examined. From Fig. 5A, it can be observed that there was a response of − 0.128 V at acidic pH 5, a response of 0.187 V at pH 9, and upon completing one cycle and returning to pH 7, there was no significant deviation from the initial value. Fig. 5B shows the hysteresis effect of the CBD process, which exhibited similar behavior to the HT process and demonstrated good stability after the cycle. Lastly, for comparison, Fig. 5C represents the ITO material, which also exhibited stable characteristics.

2.6. The drift behavior of the Ga2O3 sensor
In the study of microenvironments, long-term stability is crucial. Thus the stability test of the sensor's lifespan is of utmost importance. To evaluate long-term stability, we employed drift characteristic analysis. The AD620 was used as a signal amplifier in the drift characterization module. The results are shown in Fig. 6, where we conducted analyses in different test samples (pH 7 solution and culture medium) separately (Fig. 6A and 6B). The estimated drift rates for ITO were 3.8 mV/h and 3 mV/h, respectively. Ga2O3 (HT) exhibited drift rates of 3 mV/h and 3.1 mV/h, while Ga2O3 (CBD) exhibited drift rates of 3.8 mV/h and 5.6 mV/h. The results demonstrate that Ga2O3 exhibits both short-term and long-term stability in the pH 7 buffer. However, in comparison, HT exhibits superior stability in the culture medium. Therefore, the HT process was chosen for further use as a glucose sensing probe.

2.7. Glucose Sensing and Selective Analysis Using the Ga2O3 nanorods functional layer
From Fig. 3, it can be observed that Ga2O3 fabricated using the HT process shows a response to DMEM. Previous studies [39] have speculated that Ga2O3 may exhibit a response to glucose when applied in cellular detection. In this study, we utilized the HT process to enhance the stability of Ga2O3 nanorods growth, as demonstrated in Fig. 6.
In Fig. 7A, we conducted current sensitivity measurements using different concentrations of glucose dissolved in physiological saline solution. We selected glucose concentrations within the human range, specifically 0, 1, and 4 mg/ml as samples. The device exhibited a measurement sensitivity of 3.2 mA/mg*ml−1 for glucose, with a linear relationship represented by y = −0.0032x + 0.0466 and an R2 value of 0.99 (Fig. 7B). It displayed a high concentration-dependent characteristic. To further apply this device for glucose detection, we conducted selective analysis by mixing it with structurally similar substances. The mixture solution contained 4 mg/ml of glucose, 4 mg/ml of lactose, and 4 mg/ml of fructose. Fig. 7C shows that the drain current of the mixture solution closely resembled that of 4 mg/ml glucose, with a difference of only 3.01%. This indicates the good selectivity of Ga2O3 towards glucose and its resistance to interference from similar structured compounds. Since this device is intended for future analysis in cellular microenvironments, it is necessary to verify its sensing capability in culture media. As shown in Fig. 7c, we used commercial culture medium (DMEM, high glucose, GBICO, MA, USA) as the sample for detection. With the known glucose concentration in the medium being 4.5 mg/ml, the accuracy calculated from the measurement results was 99.9%. This level of accuracy holds great significance for various application developments in the future.

2.8. Biocompatible of probe
In order to further develop the device for monitoring glucose variations in microenvironments, its impact on cells must be monitored. We conducted preliminary validations on the sensor probe's influence on cell metabolism using both cell morphology and MTT assays. First, the probe was immersed in the culture medium (Fig. 9A). In Fig. 9B, it can be observed that the cells at D0 have not yet fully adhered, but their overall shape appears round and intact. Fig. 9C shows the morphology of the sensor device co-cultured with cells for three days. Firstly, the overall arrangement of cells is orderly, and they are fully adhered, indicating good growth conditions and no cytotoxicity within at least three days. Secondly, there are no toxic voids around the sensing chips, which is advantageous for subsequent testing. However, issues related to genotoxicity will be further investigated in future studies. Additionally, we observed the condition of cells on the sensing window, and while there were few cells attached, there was no evidence of cytotoxicity (Fig. 9D) [40], [41]. Future research could explore surface crystal modification to prevent cell adhesion on the sensing window, thus minimizing interference factors to the greatest extent possible. MTT, 3-(4,5-Dimethylthiazol-2-yl)−2,5-diphenyltetrazolium bromide, was employed from another perspective to assess cellular metabolism and activity [42]. The mechanism of MTT involves its metabolism by mitochondria, resulting in the formation of purple formazan, which can be dissolved by Dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) for cell population counting using a colorimetry method. Hs68 cells, which are dermal fibroblasts and serve as normal cells, were used as the target for toxicity testing. This culture setup only requires Dulbecco's Modified Eagle's Medium (DMEM) medium with fetal bovine serum (FBS), without the need for additional additives, making it highly valuable for detection. Based on the results in Fig. 9E, it can be observed that whether the sensor is immersed in the culture medium or not, it has minimal impact on cellular activity. Considering the comprehensive analysis of cellular morphology, we can conclude that this device has little effect on cellular metabolism, which is promising for its future applications in microenvironment monitoring.

3. Material and methods
3.1. Reagents and materials
MTT, DMEM, FBS, PBS, and culture media were obtained from GIBCO BRL company (Gaithersburg, MD, USA). The powders of glucose, lactose, and fructose were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich Chemirich Corporation, based in St. Louis, MO, USA. The ITO target material was acquired from STAREK Scientific Co., Ltd., situated in Taoyuan City, Taiwan, ROC. All other chemical reagents and reaction buffers were obtained from Sigma-Aldrich, Inc. (St. Louis, MO, USA).
3.2. The fabrication process of the functional sensing films
Before the deposition process, the glass substrate was subjected to a standard cleaning procedure using the RCA (Radio Corporation of America) process. The substrate was initially cleaned with acetone, followed by rinsing to remove any residual solvent. Subsequently, the substrate was cleaned with a mixture of methanol and hydrofluoric acid, and finally rinsed carefully with deionized (DI) water to remove any remaining residue, and dried with a nitrogen gun. The next step involved depositing the target material, ITO (Indium Tin Oxide), onto the glass substrate using an e-beam evaporation deposition system (Peva-400e).
After completing the ITO deposition, Ga2O3 nanorods were deposited using both the chemical bath deposition CBD and the HT methods. Subsequently, the sensing films were packaged and produced as glucose/pH value detection probes. The high-pressure hydrothermal method is a commonly used process for fabricating gallium oxide thin films. In this process, we mixed 5 mmole of hexamethylenetetramine (HMT) and 5 mmole of gallium nitrate hydrate, and placed them in a high-pressure autoclave. The autoclave was set at a temperature of 140 degrees and maintained at this temperature for a reaction time of 30 min. This fabrication process utilizes HMT as the reducing agent and gallium nitrate hydrate as the source of gallium. Under high temperature and high pressure conditions, these reactants undergo a reaction, resulting in the formation of gallium oxide thin films. The high-pressure hydrothermal method provides an environment conducive to crystal growth and allows for the formation of uniform and dense gallium oxide thin films within a relatively short period of time.
3.3. Packaging process
The sensing film underwent a packaging process to ensure minimal interference and contamination during sample immersion. Firstly, the thin films were cleaned using deionized water and dried in an oven at 120 °C for five minutes. Next, wires were securely bonded onto the thin films using Ag paste. The sensor, along with the wire bonds, was then incubated in an oven at 120 °C for an additional five minutes to allow the Ag paste to set. Finally, the device was fully encapsulated using epoxy resin, carefully delineating a sensing window of 1 × 1 mm².
3.4. Hysteresis and drift characteristic measurement
In this experiment, the analysis model utilized the AD620 model amplifier and the power supply (GPS-3033). The amplifier was supplied with both positive and negative bias voltages (+5 V and −5 V) simultaneously. A semiconductor parameter analyzer was used to analyze the parameters. After connecting the circuits, different concentrations of pH solutions were tested, and the analysis was conducted in the order of pH7-pH5-pH7-pH9-pH7 cycles. Each group of probes was soaked in the respective pH concentration for five minutes, with recordings taken every minute, resulting in a total cycle duration of 25 min. The probes were immersed into samples for long-term stability detection, ensuring aseptic sealing with only a small hole for wire passage to prevent contamination. The same measurement device was then used to record data every ten minutes over a period of 10 h, allowing observation of the drift behavior.
3.5. Current–voltage measurement
A pH-EGFET (pH-sensitive electrolyte-gate field-effect transistor) was created by connecting the gate of a MOSFET to a sensor head. The current-voltage (I-V) characteristics curves of the pH-EGFET were measured using the Agilent 4156 C semiconductor parameter analyzer system at room temperature. The temperature of the system was controlled using a PID temperature controller. The sensor head and a reference electrode (Ag/AgCl) were immersed directly into buffer solutions and connected to the gate of a commercially available standard MOSFET device. To ensure consistent electrochemical characteristics of the ITO pH-EGFET, a pH meter probe and a temperature thermocouple probe were utilized to monitor and maintain a uniform buffer condition.
3.6. Cell line culture
The Hs68 cell line, derived from human foreskin fibroblasts, was obtained from American Type Culture Collection (ATCC, Manassas, VA, USA; ATCC Number: CRL-1635™). 4. Conclusions
In this study, our objective was to develop a device capable of simultaneously detecting changes in glucose concentration and pH value. Using the EGFET model, we utilized the sensing film fabricated via the HT method to create a sensing probe as a gate, which was connected to the transistor. This resulted in the development of an economical, highly sensitive, and long-lasting glucose detection device. Through comprehensive testing, we observed that the Ga2O3 film fabricated using the HT process exhibited enhanced stability and a superior response to hydrogen ion concentration in pH buffer solutions and culture media. Regarding glucose detection, the results demonstrated a highly linear correlation between glucose concentration and either current change or voltage change, with an coefficient of determination value exceeding 0.99. In conclusion, this research effectively engineered a biocompatible and cost-effective dual-function glucose sensor with promising prospects for future applications in studying cellular microenvironment metabolism-related phenomena.
