In this study, for the first time, we introduce a simple and low cost pH sensor based on a commercial junction field effect transistor. The transistor was mechanically treated, isolated and used as an ion sensitive field effect transistor for pH detection, after electrodeposition of the sensing membrane on its surface. Polypyrrole, as a suitable sensing membrane, was electrodeposited on the copper metal gate of transistor from a solution containing pyrrole monomer, hydroquinone monosulfonate as a proper ligand and sodium salicylate for avoiding the substrate oxidation, under a two-step deposition conditions. The prepared sensor showed a near-Nernstian response of 52.3 mV pH−1over a linear pH range of 2.75–12.20, an ultra low hysteresis of 0.56 mV, a very low drift of 0.14 mV h−1and a low response time of less than 8 s.

The ion sensitive field effect transistors (ISFET), as a new type of chemical sensing electrodes, was first introduced by Bergveld [1] in 1970. In the past decade, various types of these electrodes, including biomedical [2], biochemical [3], microphysiological and bioelectronical [4] and physical [5] sensors, have been fabricated for a wide variety of applications. Most of these sensors have been designed on the basis of metal oxide silicon field effect transistor (MOSFET) [6,7]. In ISFET devices, a reference electrode is used to provide necessary gate bias and, after immersing electrode in a proper solution, a potential difference is generated across the solution–membrane interfaces, which is proportional to the concentration of the ions exposed to the membrane [8].

Among different ISFET electrodes, the ISFET pH sensors have been of special interest of many researchers in various fields, including chemical, biological and medical analyses. The ISFET pH sensors possess distinct advantages over conventional pH measurement systems, including robustness, small size, and applicability to integrate into miniaturized systems [1,9]. Different pH sensitive gate insulator materials such as (SiO2) [1], Si3N4[10], Al2O3[11], Ta2O5[12], polyelectrolyte/nanoparticle multilayer film [13], poly(3-hexylthiophene) (P3HT) [14], indium tin oxide (ITO) [9,15], modified diamond [16] and polyurethane [17] have been employed in construction of ISFET pH sensors.

Meanwhile, a number of conductive polymers [14,18–23] and non-conductive polymers [15,24] have already been used as interesting materials for preparation of pH sensors based on field effect transistor (FET) devices. The deposition of these polymeric materials on ISFET electrodes has been carried out by a number of deposition techniques, including photolithography [25], chemical vapor deposition (CVD) [21], irradiation of UV light [15,22], sputtering [23], etching [21] and electrochemical deposition [23,26].

One of the most favorable conductive polymers is polypyrrole (PPy). This polymer is easily deposited from aqueous or nonaqueous media [27] and produces a high conductivity and quite stable polymeric membrane that is very adherent to many of substrates [28].

In this work, for the first time, we used a commercially available cheap junction field effect transistor (JFET) combined with a hydroquinone monosulfonate-doped PPy membrane for sensitive pH sensing. In fact, after mechanical removing of plastic cover on the copper metal gate of the transistor, a controlled layer of PPy membrane was electrodeposited on the copper gate from a solution containing pyrrole monomer, hydroquinone monosulfonate (HQS) as ligand [29] and sodium salicylate for avoiding the substrate oxidation [30]. The proposed method can efficiently afford the above-mentioned main problems associated with the preparation of many ISFET devices including poor isolation between the gate dielectrics and the chemical solutions [31], high fabrication costs and lack of long-term stability [8]. It is worth mentioning that, although there are some previous reports on electrodeposition of some metals [32–36] and also PPy [37] on copper substrate, mainly for corrosion protection, to the best of our knowledge there is no previous report on the use of electrodeposited PPy on copper surface for sensor applications.

Analytical reagent-grade potassium chloride, boric acid, phosphoric acid, salicylic acid, acetic acid, pyrrole, sodium hydroxide, HQS and universal buffer were purchased from Merck chemical company. Standard solutions were prepared with deionized water. Heat 1, 2, 4 and 5 mm shrinkage tubes and the Advantech USB-4716, 200 KS s−1, 16-bit Multifunction USB Module (China) were purchased from local markets. Throughout the work, universal buffer (0.04 M) was used to adjust pH of solutions from 2.0 to 12.5. A Metrohm pH meter (827 pH lab) with extensive range glass electrode was used for pH monitoring throughout the work.

In this work, low cost commercial JFETs (low noise, N-channel) were purchased from the local market. The cost of each transistor was less than 1 US$. It is worth mentioning that, although the cheap FET type transistors available from local markets were manufactured by different companies such as Siemens, Toshiba, Philips, in all cases the metal gate was made of copper metal.

The plastic coverage on the metal gate area was removed with a micro driller and the terminal pads and soldered wires were shielded with heat shrinkage tubes for avoiding contact with solution. Schematics of this electrode, before and after shielding, are shown in Fig.1a and b, respectively.

aSchematic of ISFET electrode before (a) and after (b) encapsulation, and pictures of the ISFET device before (c) and after polypyrrole membrane electrodeposition (d)

For fabrication of the sensing membrane, we used a three electrode cell including the metal gate of JFET as working electrode, a platinum disk as the counter electrode and a double junction Ag/AgCl (3 M KCl) electrode (Metrohm) as the reference electrode. Polypyrrole was deposited on metal gate in two steps. In the first step, a 5 mL solution containing sodium salicylate (1 M, pH = 6.0) and pyrrole (0.5 M) was prepared. Then, the PPy film was electrodeposited on the working electrode using the potentiostatic mode at 1.5 V for 1 min using a Metrohm Autolab instrument (PGSTAT302N). In the second step, the potentiostatic process under the above-mentioned condition was applied for 10 min for preparation of HQS-doped PPy film on the PPy substrate. The solution for this process contained 0.02 M HQS and 0.5 M pyrrole. Then, the ISFET electrode was dried at ambient temperature. It should be noted that pyrrole is stable over a wide pH range and PPy is electrodeposited from aqueous solution over a pH range 4.0–10.0 [38]. Due to the presence of the salicylate ion and copper substrate, the neutral, basic and highly acidic (below pH 4.7) media could not be used. At the neutral and basic media, copper oxide layers are formed that limit further electron transfer. Also at pHs below 4.7, the salicylate ion is precipitated out. Therefore, a pH of 6.0 was chosen for the pyrrole electrodeposition [30,39]. The pictures of the ISFET device before and after PPy membrane electrodeposition are also shown in Fig.1c and d, respectively.

An Advantech board (USB 4716, 200 KS s−1) was used for applying voltage and data acquisition. A homemade automated set up composed of an appropriate circuit and a visual basic programs was designed. The total electrical characteristic curves such as ISFET characteristics, transfer characteristics and sampling experiments were recorded in solutions of varying pH, at room temperature. Schematic representation of measurement set up is shown in Fig.2. The sampling experiments of the ISFET device have been performed at a fixed drain-source voltage (VDS= 0.2 V), and constant drain-source current (IDS= 150 μA), followed by recording the output voltage from the feedback mode circuit.

Schematic representation of the measurement set up.VDS= drain-to-source voltage;VGS= gate-to-source voltage

As mentioned in experimental section, the plastic coverage of a commercial N-JFET is removed carefully by a micro driller, after which the copper metal gate of transistor can be modified with PPy electrochemically. Treatment with micro driller is very important in this stage as it should satisfy two critical points. First, one should be careful that only the plastic cover of metal gate is removed and the source and drain parts are remained untreated. Otherwise short circuit will occur between the drain or source and the gate. Second, the tip of micro driller should not damage the copper metal gate surface.

To prove the required safety in this respect, the transistor was characterized before and after treatment and the results are shown in Fig.3. As is quite obvious from Fig.3, for all specifiedVGSandVDSvoltages tested, the amount of drain current is similar, indicating that the treated transistor is not damaged. After this successful test, all parts of the treated transistor, except for copper gate area, were shielded by a proper heat shrinkage tube.

For electrodeposition of appropriate membrane on metal gate of transistor, we should have knowledge about composition of metal. For this purpose, we did several regular qualitative/quantitative tests [40], and all observations declared the presence of copper as metal gate of the commercial N-JFET used. For verification of these qualitative observations, we studied the electrochemical behavior of the metal gate, as follows. For acquiring the cyclic voltammetry test, we used the gate connector as a working electrode and putted it in a solution containing 1.0 M sodium salicylate at pH 6.0. The resulting cyclic voltammogram, taken at a scan rate of 20 mV s−1is shown in Fig.4. As seen, there are two main peaks in voltages 0.06 and 0.94 V corresponding to the oxidation of copper and/or copper-salicylate complex formation [41] and subsequent formation of a salicylate based polymeric film, respectively, which is in accordance to the literature report [30]. Also for further confidence, the electrode was subjected to consecutive potential cycles and the results are shown in the inset of Fig.4. The results clearly show a continuous decrease in the current for the peak at 0.94 V without any significant variation of the other peak, brought about upon applying consecutive potential cycles, which emphasizes the continuous formation of the salicylate-based polymeric film at the surface of copper substrate.

Cyclic voltammetry of metal gate of ISFET electrode in 1.0 M sodium salicylate at pH 6.0

Since the gate of transistor is consisted of copper, as an oxidizable metal, it is not possible to directly deposit the HQS-doped PPy sensing membrane on the gate metal; it is well-known that, in this case, upon increasing of oxidation potential, the metal oxidation is occurred before the polymerization step [30]. Therefore, we decided to do the job under a two-step deposition conditions. First, before depositing of the sensing membrane composed of HQS and PPy, an intermediate PPy layer in the presence of salicylate ion was successfully deposited at the copper gate to cover its total area. Then, in the second step, the deposition of HQS-doped PPy sensing membrane was afforded, as explained in experimental section.

It is interesting to note that, if the deposition of sensing membrane was occurred in a single step (i.e., from a solution containing HQS, pyrrole and sodium salicylate), the resulting response voltage-pH calibration would show two separate parts of different slopes (see Fig.5, Line 1). However, by applying such a two-step procedure, a nice single slope near-Nernstian calibration curve with a slope 52.3 mV pH−1over a wide pH range of 2.75–12.20 was obtained (Fig.5, Line 2).

Calibration curves of electrodes coated under one-step (filled triangle) and two-steps (filled square) deposition condition

A homemade setup shown in Fig.2was used to obtain various characteristic curves of the prepared pH-ISFET sensor. For driving the characteristic curves, the prepared ISFET was immersed in a 0.04 M universal buffer of pH 2.65 and by addition of specific amounts of NaOH solution, the pH of solution was changed consecutively. The transfer characteristics measurements were carried out at a constant drain-to-source voltage of 0.2 V at room temperature, over a pH range of 2.65–12.26. The resulting transfer characteristics curves of drain-to-source current vs. reference voltage curves from solutions with varying pH over the range of 2.65–12.26 are shown in Fig.6. As seen, the H+ions in the buffer electrolyte solutions have an electrochemical interaction with the surface of sensing membrane and, consequently, the pinch off voltage (VP) of the ISFET device has been changed with change in pH of solution. It should be noted that the pinch off voltage is the amount of gate-to-source voltage at which the current flow between source and drain is blocked because of the depletion of the channel between these electrodes [42].

Since, in this work a treated N-JFET was used as ISFET, operation of the ISFETs should be very similar to that of the N-channel JFETs. When an ISFET and a reference electrode (i.e., Ag/AgCl) are placed in solutions of various pHs, a family of curves can be generated by applying a constant voltage of −0.5 V to the reference electrode and scanning the drain voltage from 0.0 to 1.5 V. Thus, the resulting drain-to-source current vs. drain-to-source voltage curves of solutions of different pHs, similar to Fig.6, were obtained (Fig.7). As can be seen from Fig.7, drain current was decreased by increasing pH of solution, which is similar to the case where the reference potential was moved to more negative values. From this observation, it can be concluded that the proposed electrode is responded to hydronium ion concentration in solution.

It is worth mentioning that, for driving any of various curves such as calibration curve, transfer characteristics, ISFET characteristics, the universal buffer was freshly prepared and various solution pHs were carefully checked with the reference glass electrode continuously. In addition, the total time necessary for driving various curves was only about 15 min, during which the buffer was remained quite stable.

Since a pH sensor for real applications in biochemical engineering needs to possess good selectivity to protons without any interference from other ions, especially Na+and K+, the selectivity of the proposed pH sensor over Na+and K+ions was investigated. The sensor was immersed in a buffer solution of pH 12.0 containing 0.2 M of Na+and K+as interfering ions and the transfer characteristics were measured. The results showed no measureable change in pinch off voltage obtained from the transfer characteristics in the absence and presence of the interfering ions, emphasizing that the electrode is insensitive to the presence of such high concentrations of interfering ions even in the presence of very low proton concentrations.

In this work, after each polymer coating and the construction of the proposed pH sensor, the sensor found to work quite well for at least a week, without any measurable change in response. It is interesting to note that, after safe use of each electrode for this period of time, it can be easily renewed as follows. First, the sensing membrane deposited on the metal gate is removed mechanically. Then, the copper metal gate is dipped into concentrated nitric acid, and thoroughly washed with water. Now the electrode is ready for re-coating of the sensing membrane by following the procedure given in experimental section.

For driving the pH sensitivity of the prepared ISFET electrode sensor, it was biased in the linear region of their electrical characteristics, as follows. The ISFET is biased at a fixedVDSandIDS(i.e., 0.2 V and 150 μA, respectively) so that the variation in solution concentration will result in the changes of the pinch off voltage (VP). The drain current is maintained constant by varying the gate voltage via a reference electrode in solution. The resultingVPis directly proportional to pH and, hence, is a direct indication of pH of solution. The corresponding sampling experiment curve at various pHs is shown in Fig.8. The calibration curve derived from sampling experiment results is shown in inset of Fig.8. As is obvious, the prepared sensor possesses a linear pH sensitivity of 52.3 mV per pH over a linear pH range of 2.75–12.20.

Output voltage–time curves derived from feedback mode circuit at constantVDS= 0.2 V andIDS= 150 μA at various

The average response time (i.e., the time required to complete 90 % of the change in potential after immersion in the new solution) of the prepared pH sensor can be obtained from different pH steps of the sampling experiment curves. The magnification of a single step corresponding to pH changes from 4.33 to pH 5.31 is shown in Fig.9, as an example. The results clearly revealed that the average response time of the proposed sensor over the pH range studied is below 8 s, which is much lower than a response time of 100 s reported by Prissanaroon-Quajai et al. [29], where the authors used HQS as a proton sensing ligand.

Magnified sample experimental output voltage–time curve for pH variation from 4.33 to 5.31

When the intrinsic response of the pH-ISFET is completed, the output voltage of the pH-ISFET still varies gradually and monotonically with time, the phenomenon being called as drift [43]. The drift rate (also called drift coefficient) is then evaluated after initial time of stabilization of ISFET as a linear change ofVoutper unit time [44].

In this study, the drift characteristics of FET-based pH sensor were evaluated as follows. The pH-ISFET electrode was immersed in a buffer solution of pH 3.0 and the output voltage was measured continuously with elapse of time for 26 h, under the conditions mentioned in experimental section. The experimental results are shown in Fig.10. As it is seen from Fig.10, the sensor response is decreased with time for 15 h, and then tended to level off linearly at longer time periods. Thus, the drift rate obtained from the slope of the linear part of the output voltage–time plot (Fig.10) was found to possess a low value of 0.14 mV h−1. It should be mentioned that this value is much lower than that previously reported for some ISFET pH sensors with drift values of 3.96 [16] and 2.2 mV h−1[45].

Drift in the output voltage vs. time for ISFET pH sensor, measured in a buffer solution of pH 3.0

The hysteresis, also called memory effect, is a result of the slow response of the pH-ISFET [9]. In fact, there are different output voltages when the pH-ISFET was measured many times at the same pH value. This voltage deviation is defined as the hysteresis width [43]. The hysteresis is a suitable marker for the evaluation of the reproducibility of the devices. In practice, hysteresis can be evaluated from titration first in acidic or basic direction and then in an opposite direction to close the pH loop [46].

To evaluate the hysteresis of the proposed pH-ISFET sensor, the output voltage of feedback circuit was measured for the following solution pH cycles: pH 7.0 → pH 4.1 → pH 7.0 → pH 10.4 → pH 7.0 → pH 4.1 → pH → 7.0 → pH 10.4 → pH 7.0, and the results are shown in Fig.11. As is obvious, the proposed sensor revealed very low hysteresis value of about 0.56 mV in the cycles studied. This result also confirms the film stability, as the voltage is recovered to the same pH value. It is worth mentioning that the hysteresis value of this sensor is much lower than that previously reported for some ISFET pH sensors with values of 9.8 [17] and 10 mV [45], which indicate the film stability of the proposed pH sensor at above-mentioned pH cycles.

For driving the temperature coefficient of pH sensor, the transfer characteristic curves of drain-to-source current vs. gate-to-source voltage for a buffer solution of pH 7.0, at a fixed drain-source voltage ofVDS= 0.2 V, were obtained over a relatively wide temperature range of 10–46 °C and the results are shown in Fig.12.

Transfer characteristics curve of ISFET pH sensor at various temperatures (from 10 to 46 °C) andVDS= 0.2 V.Insetshows the plot of gate-to-source voltage vs. temperature curve atVDS= 0.2 V andIDS= 150 μA

The inset of Fig.12shows the linear plot gate-to-source voltage vs. temperature at a fixed drain-to-source current of 150 μA. The temperature coefficient of the pH sensor, obtained from the slope of this graph, found to be 1.1 mV pH−1.

In Table1the response characteristics (i.e., sensitivity, pH range, drift, hysteresis and response time) of some previously reported ISFET pH sensors [9,14–16,18,19,43] are compared with those obtained in this work. As is obvious, the proposed sensor is superior to the previous ones in regard to drift, hysteresis and response time, although its pH range and sensitivity are more or less similar to those of the previously reported pH sensors. In addition, it should be noticed that the cost of the proposed sensor is much lower than that of the reported electrodes.

In this work for the first time we conveniently used a very low cost commercial JFET, after electrodeposition of hydroquinone monosulfonate-doped PPy modification at its copper metal gate, for ISFET application in aqueous media for sensitive pH determination over a wide range of 2.75–12.20 with a near-Nernstian slope of 52.3 mV pH−1. The drift (0.14 mV h−1) and hysteresis (0.56 mV) of the prepared sensor were found to be much lower than that of many previously reported ISFET pH sensors. The presence of Na+and K+ions revealed no measurable interfering effect in pH sensing of the proposed sensor. The proposed sensor efficiently afforded the common drawbacks of poor isolation between the gate dielectrics and the chemical solutions, lack of long-term stability and high fabrication costs, which are associated with the preparation of many ISFET devices.

In the past decade, various types of these electrodes, including biomedical [2], biochemical [3], microphysiological and bioelectronical [4] and physical [5] sensors, have been fabricated for a wide variety of applications. Most of these sensors have been designed on the basis of metal oxide silicon field effect transistor (MOSFET) [6,7]. In ISFET devices, a reference electrode is used to provide necessary gate bias and, after immersing electrode in a proper solution, a potential difference is generated across the solution–membrane interfaces, which is proportional to the concentration of the ions exposed to the membrane [8].Among different ISFET electrodes, the ISFET pH sensors have been of special interest of many researchers in various fields, including chemical, biological and medical analyses. The ISFET pH sensors possess distinct advantages over conventional pH measurement systems, including robustness, small size, and applicability to integrate into miniaturized systems [1,9]. Different pH sensitive gate insulator materials such as (SiO2) [1], Si3N4[10], Al2O3[11], Ta2O5[12], polyelectrolyte/nanoparticle multilayer film [13], poly(3-hexylthiophene) (P3HT) [14], indium tin oxide (ITO) [9,15], modified diamond [16] and polyurethane [17] have been employed in construction of ISFET pH sensors.Meanwhile, a number of conductive polymers [14,18–23] and non-conductive polymers [15,24] have already been used as interesting materials for preparation of pH sensors based on field effect transistor (FET) devices. The deposition of these polymeric materials on ISFET electrodes has been carried out by a number of deposition techniques, including photolithography [25], chemical vapor deposition (CVD) [21], irradiation of UV light [15,22], sputtering [23], etching [21] and electrochemical deposition [23,26].One of the most favorable conductive polymers is polypyrrole (PPy). This polymer is easily deposited from aqueous or nonaqueous media [27] and produces a high conductivity and quite stable polymeric membrane that is very adherent to many of substrates [28].In this work, for the first time, we used a commercially available cheap junction field effect transistor (JFET) combined with a hydroquinone monosulfonate-doped PPy membrane for sensitive pH sensing. In fact, after mechanical removing of plastic cover on the copper metal gate of the transistor, a controlled layer of PPy membrane was electrodeposited on the copper gate from a solution containing pyrrole monomer, hydroquinone monosulfonate (HQS) as ligand [29] and sodium salicylate for avoiding the substrate oxidation [30]. The proposed method can efficiently afford the above-mentioned main problems associated with the preparation of many ISFET devices including poor isolation between the gate dielectrics and the chemical solutions [31], high fabrication costs and lack of long-term stability [8]. It is worth mentioning that, although there are some previous reports on electrodeposition of some metals [32–36] and also PPy [37] on copper substrate, mainly for corrosion protection, to the best of our knowledge there is no previous report on the use of electrodeposited PPy on copper surface for sensor applications.ExperimentalMaterials and apparatusAnalytical reagent-grade potassium chloride, boric acid, phosphoric acid, salicylic acid, acetic acid, pyrrole, sodium hydroxide, HQS and universal buffer were purchased from Merck chemical company. Standard solutions were prepared with deionized water. Heat 1, 2, 4 and 5 mm shrinkage tubes and the Advantech USB-4716, 200 KS s−1, 16-bit Multifunction USB Module (China) were purchased from local markets. Throughout the work, universal buffer (0.04 M) was used to adjust pH of solutions from 2.0 to 12.5. A Metrohm pH meter (827 pH lab) with extensive range glass electrode was used for pH monitoring throughout the work.ISFET fabricationIn this work, low cost commercial JFETs (low noise, N-channel) were purchased from the local market. The cost of each transistor was less than 1 US$. It is worth mentioning that, although the cheap FET type transistors available from local markets were manufactured by different companies such as Siemens, Toshiba, Philips, in all cases the metal gate was made of copper metal.The plastic coverage on the metal gate area was removed with a micro driller and the terminal pads and soldered wires were shielded with heat shrinkage tubes for avoiding contact with solution. Schematics of this electrode, before and after shielding, are shown in Fig.1a and b, respectively.Fig. 1aSchematic of ISFET electrode before (a) and after (b) encapsulation, and pictures of the ISFET device before (c) and after polypyrrole membrane electrodeposition (d)Full size imagePreparation of ion sensitive membraneFor fabrication of the sensing membrane, we used a three electrode cell including the metal gate of JFET as working electrode, a platinum disk as the counter electrode and a double junction Ag/AgCl (3 M KCl) electrode (Metrohm) as the reference electrode. Polypyrrole was deposited on metal gate in two steps. In the first step, a 5 mL solution containing sodium salicylate (1 M, pH = 6.0) and pyrrole (0.5 M) was prepared. Then, the PPy film was electrodeposited on the working electrode using the potentiostatic mode at 1.5 V for 1 min using a Metrohm Autolab instrument (PGSTAT302N). In the second step, the potentiostatic process under the above-mentioned condition was applied for 10 min for preparation of HQS-doped PPy film on the PPy substrate. The solution for this process contained 0.02 M HQS and 0.5 M pyrrole. Then, the ISFET electrode was dried at ambient temperature. It should be noted that pyrrole is stable over a wide pH range and PPy is electrodeposited from aqueous solution over a pH range 4.0–10.0 [38]. Due to the presence of the salicylate ion and copper substrate, the neutral, basic and highly acidic (below pH 4.7) media could not be used. At the neutral and basic media, copper oxide layers are formed that limit further electron transfer. Also at pHs below 4.7, the salicylate ion is precipitated out. Therefore, a pH of 6.0 was chosen for the pyrrole electrodeposition [30,39]. For verification of these qualitative observations, we studied the electrochemical behavior of the metal gate, as follows. Therefore, we decided to do the job under a two-step deposition conditions. First, before depositing of the sensing membrane composed of HQS and PPy, an intermediate PPy layer in the presence of salicylate ion was successfully deposited at the copper gate to cover its total area. The sensor was immersed in a buffer solution of pH 12.0 containing 0.2 M of Na+and K+as interfering ions and the transfer characteristics were measured. First, the sensing membrane deposited on the metal gate is removed mechanically. Then, the copper metal gate is dipped into concentrated nitric acid, and thoroughly washed with water. The drift rate (also called drift coefficient) is then evaluated after initial time of stabilization of ISFET as a linear change ofVoutper unit time [44].In this study, the drift characteristics of FET-based pH sensor were evaluated as follows. The pH-ISFET electrode was immersed in a buffer solution of pH 3.0 and the output voltage was measured continuously with elapse of time for 26 h, under the conditions mentioned in experimental section. The experimental results are shown in Fig.10. As it is seen from Fig.10, the sensor response is decreased with time for 15 h, and then tended to level off linearly at longer time periods. Thus, the drift rate obtained from the slope of the linear part of the output voltage–time plot (Fig.10) was found to possess a low value of 0.14 mV h−1. It should be mentioned that this value is much lower than that previously reported for some ISFET pH sensors with drift values of 3.96 [16] and 2.2 mV h−1[45].Fig. 10Drift in the output voltage vs. time for ISFET pH sensor, measured in a buffer solution of pH 3.0Full size imageThe hysteresis, also called memory effect, is a result of the slow response of the pH-ISFET [9]. In fact, there are different output voltages when the pH-ISFET was measured many times at the same pH value. This voltage deviation is defined as the hysteresis width [43]. The hysteresis is a suitable marker for the evaluation of the reproducibility of the devices. In practice, hysteresis can be evaluated from titration first in acidic or basic direction and then in an opposite direction to close the pH loop [46].To evaluate the hysteresis of the proposed pH-ISFET sensor, the output voltage of feedback circuit was measured for the following solution pH cycles: pH 7.0 → pH 4.1 → pH 7.0 → pH 10.4 → pH 7.0 → pH 4.1 → pH → 7.0 → pH 10.4 → pH 7.0, and the results are shown in Fig.11. As is obvious, the proposed sensor revealed very low hysteresis value of about 0.56 mV in the cycles studied. This result also confirms the film stability, as the voltage is recovered to the same pH value. The drift (0.14 mV h−1) and hysteresis (0.56 mV) of the prepared sensor were found to be much lower than that of many previously reported ISFET pH sensors. The presence of Na+and K+ions revealed no measurable interfering effect in pH sensing of the proposed sensor.
