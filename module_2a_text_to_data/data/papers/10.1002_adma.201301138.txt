 Sensors can be categorized according to operational principles
such as electrical sensors, optical sensors, magnetic sensors,
and so on. An electrical sensor is simple in device confi guration because the detection of the electrical signal generally
does not need complicated detection equipments. Among
electrical sensors based on different device geometries, for
example, three-terminal transistors and two-terminal resistors, organic fi eld-effect transistor (OFET)-based sensors are
one of promising candidates for low-cost, portable, sensitive,
and selective applications in chemical, biological, and physical
monitoring, [1,2]
 Gas sensing is in general important for environment and health issues. Ammonia is one of the most harmful
environmental pollutants. Until now, ammonia detection based
on OFET sensors has been reported in a few cases. [2]
 We summarize the key performance parameters (sensitivity, response/
recovery time, selectivity, reversibility, stability, and detection
limit) of electrical ammonia sensors with transistor or resistor
geometries using organic semiconductors (OSCs), conducting
polymers, carbon, and inorganic compounds as the active layer
in Table S1, Supporting Information. [2,3]
 It can be seen that in
the reported sensors the sensitivity ( S 1, Igas-off / Igas-on , for detailed
defi nition, see Experimental Section) is basically smaller than
10. Furthermore, those devices respond and recover quite
slowly (generally in the range of minutes, Table S1, Supporting
Information). [2,3]
 Undoubtedly, higher sensitivity and faster response/recovery
rate will signifi cantly increase the credibility and fi delity of
sensors and thus expand their application fi eld. This is still a
challenging task because in an OFET, the conductive channel
locates at the interface between an OSC and a dielectric, [4]
 but
the thick and continuous OSC fi lms (tens of nm) commonly
used in OFETs create barriers for the interaction between the
conductive channel and the analyte, and therefore dramatically
lower the sensitivity and prolongs the response/recovery time.
Lateral scaling (microstructuring) and vertical scaling (thinning)
of OSC thin fi lms enable the conductive channel to be exposed
to analyte molecules effectively, [2b,5]
 and thus can accelerate the
interaction between the conductive channel and analytes. However, growth of ultrathin, micro-structured and continuous (at
least in one dimension) OSC fi lms through solution methods
is a challenging task due to the necessary compromise between
thickness, microstructuring and continuous fi lm formation.
Here, we report a versatile strategy for the growth of dendritic
microstripes of OSCs with 4–6 molecular layers (6.5–11 nm in
thickness) and one-dimensional continuity, which is a kind of
continuous, microstructured and ultrathin OSC fi lm, via a dipcoating process. Based on ultrathin microstripes, ammonia sensors with signifi cantly enhanced sensitivity and short response/
recovery time are realized. The key performance parameters are
listed in Table S1, Supporting Information, for an easy comparison with other electrical sensors reported previously.
 Selection of appropriate active OSCs is one of the prerequisites for high performance sensors, especially in view of sensitivity and selectivity, because the chemical nature of OSCs determines the type, strength, and specifi city of interactions between
OSCs and analytes. In our work, a solution-processable OSC,
namely a dialkyl tetrathiapentacene (DTBDT-C6) ( Figure 1 a) is
used. [6]
 A dip-coating technique is adopted to produce ultrathin
microstripes of this semiconductor compound, as we have
reported previously. [7]
 By optimizing the growth conditions, uniform and ultrathin dendritic microstripes are obtained over a
large area (centimeter size). The underlying growth mechanism
will be discussed separately elsewhere. Atomic force microscopy (AFM) measurements indicate that the thickness of these
microstripes is only 4–6 molecular layers (Figure 1 b; Figure S1,
Supporting Information). X-ray diffraction (XRD) measurements (Figure S2, Supporting Information) reveal that DTBDTC6 molecules adopt an edge-on arrangement on the substrates
(Figure 1 a, right part). Large-area scanning electron microscopy
(SEM) images (Figure 1 c) show that the main arms of the dendritic microstripes (trunks in Figure 1 b,c) are well aligned and
extend continuously over hundreds of micrometers. The continuity and alignment over large area facilitate the fabrication of
planar electronic devices.
 The ultrathin, continuous and well-aligned microstripes
allow the easy fabrication of OFET-based sensors. We fi rst

NH 3

 Generally, two kinds of defi nitions are used for sensitivity in
electrical sensors. The fi rst one is expressed as a percentage, that is
( Igas-off – Igas-on )/ Igas-off × 100%, or ΔI / Igas-off × 100% (referred as S 2).
The second one is the ratio of current between gas off and gas on
( Igas-off / Igas-on , referred as S 1). If the current does not change signifi cantly
(over one order of magnitude), the fi rst one is commonly used. If the
current change is over one order of magnitude, the second criterion
would be clearer than the fi rst one. These two defi nition can be easily
converted by the equation S 1 × (1 – S 2) = 1. The response time is
defi ned as the period in which the sensor output signal reaches 10% of
the steady value upon exposure to the analyte, while the recovery time is
defi ned as the period in which the sensor output signal reaches 90% of
the steady state after the analyte is removed.

 In conclusion, ammonia gas sensing with ultrathin microstripes of DTBDT-C6, which are grown in a controllable way
via dip-coating, was tested in an OFET geometry. Excellent
sensor performance including high sensitivity, fast response/
recovery rate, good selectivity, low concentration detection
ability, good reversibility, and stability were demonstrated. Such
a performance, especially the sensitivity and response/recovery
time, represents a great progress for OFET-based sensors. The
ultrathin microstripes provide direct and effi cient pathways for
the analytes’ activities (adsorption/diffusion/interaction/desorption) within the conductive channel, and therefore account
for the improved sensing performance. This study may pave an
avenue toward low-cost, fast, and portable electronic noses for
environmental monitoring in agricultural areas and chemical
processing plants, gas detection for counter-terrorism, and
sensing for disease diagnosis.

Figure S5 shows the typical sensing curve of ultrathin microstripes (4-6 ML) and continuous
film (20 nm) toward acetone (> 200 ppm). Clear sensor signal i.e., the change of drain-source
current can be observed in sensor with ultrathin microstripes, while continuous film yields
very weak sensing signal. This comparison definitely confirms that ultrathin microstripes can
enhance the sensor performance.
