Abstract
Distinguishing structural isomers is a critical and challenging task for biotechnology, chemical industry, and environmental monitoring. Approaches currently available are limited in terms of selectivity and simplicity. In this paper, a highly sensitive organic field-effect transistor (OFET) using the cyclopentadithiophene-benzothiadiazole (CDT-BTZ) copolymers as a semiconductor is presented for easy and selective detection of different families of structural isomers, as well as between different isomers within each family. High accuracy discrimination is achieved over a range of concentrations using only a single sensing parameter derived from the OFET characteristic transfer curve. As a reference, other homopolymer- and donor–acceptor copolymer-based OFET sensors are examined but do not have an equivalent sensing performance to that of the CDT-BTZ-based OFETs. Investigating the link between isomer absorption and swelling, supramolecular order and energy levels of the active layer reveals a unique effect of each isomer on the energy bands of the semiconducting polymer.

1. Introduction
Structural isomers of volatile organic compounds play an important role in biotechnology as well as pharmaceutical and chemical industries.1 For example, xylene has three isomers, namely, p-xylene, m-xylene, and o-xylene, and is widely utilized in the synthesis of important chemical products, including chemical intermediates of polyesters, rubber, and other polymers.2 Ketone and aldehyde isomers are used in a variety of applications, and are tightly connected to health issues.3 Indeed, volatile organic isomers have varying degrees of harmfulness at high concentrations; for example, the U.S. National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health (NIOSH) has recommended a time-weighted average permissible exposure limit of 100 ppm for xylenes, whereas for some ketones, such as 3-heptanone, it is only 50 ppm.4 Hence, accurate detection, monitoring, and discrimination of these compounds are essential in environmental monitoring and healthcare.

Gas chromatographic mass spectrometry has long been applied for the accurate detection and quantification of structural isomers.5 However, it requires expensive equipment and is of limited availability. Gas sensors can be more reliable and applicable in this field since they hold promise for cheap and fast isomer detection. So far, mass sensor arrays,6 metal oxide sensors,7, 8 chemiresistors,9-11 field-effect transistors (FETs),12, 13 and cataluminescent sensors14 have been introduced as practical discrimination tools. Haick and co-workers13 have reported a highly selective gas sensor based on an ambipolar diketopyrrolopyrrole FET for sensing xylene isomers. Successful detection and discrimination between isomers with <40 ppm concentrations were achieved only after applying machine learning on a combination of device parameters, including carrier mobility (µ), threshold voltage (Vth), and subthreshold swing. Cross-reactive chemiresistors array based on gold nanoparticles or carbon nanostructures have enabled detection and discriminative performances between ketone and aldehyde isomers, such as heptanal/3-heptanone and hexanal/2-hexanone.10, 11 The discriminatory power of most of the reported sensors was largely limited to high concentrations or extreme conditions. For example, high detection limits of 103 and 400 ppm were reported in refs. 6 and 1 respectively; 5 ppm p-xylene were detected using cobalt-doped zinc oxide nanowire-based sensors. However, such metal-oxide-semiconductor-based sensors work only at relatively high temperatures (>400 °C), and no selectivity with regard to the other isomers of xylenes could be achieved.7 Other methods have used multiple cross-reactive parameters and can only discriminate between one type of isomers at a specific concentration.10, 11, 13 Accordingly, a reliable selective sensor for detecting multiple and chemically diverse isomers over a wide range of concentrations is desirable from both fundamental and practical viewpoints.

2 Results and Discussion
2.1 Device Fabrication and Performance
Herein, we report on a highly selective OFET gas sensor based on high mobility donor–acceptor copolymer (CDT-BTZ) that can discriminate between multiple isomers (derivatives of xylenes, ketones, and aldehydes) over a wide range of concentrations using only one sensing parameter. OFETs based on conjugated polymers or small molecules are promising sensor platforms because they have various advantages, including high sensitivity, mechanical flexibility, and low-cost fabrication processes.15 CDT-BTZ was used as a semiconductor and an active sensing component in a typical OFET structure of bottom gate/bottom contact. The semiconductor material was drop-cast on the source–drain electrodes and hydrophobically modified SiO2 (Figure 1a,b). Figure 1c is a scanning electron microscope (SEM) image of the semiconductor film composed of a very dense polymer network. The ordered packing in the solid-state contributes to the high mobility of the polymer.16-18 Moreover, it increases the exposed surface area, improving interactions with analytes, and increasing the detection ability of sensors. Grazing-incidence scattering was used to measure the degree of supramolecular order of CDT films. It shows that the d-spacing between the chains of CDT-BTZ is ≈3.05 nm under ambient conditions (Figure S1, Supporting Information). Figure 1d,e presents output and transfer curves of the transistor and shows a typical behavior under ambient conditions. Several parameters, such as the carrier mobility (µ), threshold voltage (Vth), on-current (Ion) and the source–drain currents (Ids) at different gate voltages (Vg)—all of which are extracted from the OFET transfer curves—were monitored during exposure to isomers and used as sensing features. In most cases, discrimination was achieved using only one feature. However, by combining multiple sensing features derived from a single OFET and/or by using a pattern-recognition algorithm, chemical fingerprints for each isomer could be obtained with greater accuracy for a very wide range of concentrations.

2.2 Sensor Performance under Exposure to Organic Isomers
In this report, CDT-BTZ OFETs were used to detect seven important, but chemically different, volatile organic isomers at different concentrations (Table 1). These isomers were chosen for their significance in many aspects of life: xylenes, which are found in areas close to gas stations, for their relation to environmental monitoring and the necessity to ensure their concentration is under the time-weighted average permissible exposure limit of 100 ppm; and heptanal, 3-heptanone, hexanal, and 2-hexanone for their role as diagnostic biomarkers of diseases and health monitoring.3, 19

Based on the OFET transfer curve, changes in multiple sensing parameters, such as µ, Vth, Ion, or Ids at different values of Vg were extracted. The normalized parameters have different or cross-reactive responses toward the isomers, and the discrimination ability of each parameter was determined by the accuracy value, which is the proportion of true results (true positives and true negatives) of the total number of cases examined (see explanation in Section S1, Supporting Information). For simplicity, we focused on two parameters, µ and Vth, to analyze the responses and the discrimination ability. However, other parameters, such as current values, were also used. Figure 2a–f shows the response of the parameters to different concentrations of isomers in the range of 10–320 ppm for xylenes and 10–160 ppm for the hexanal, 2-hexanone, heptanal, and 3-heptanone. The huge difference in responses to aldehydes (heptanal and hexanal) and ketones (3-heptanone and 2-hexanone) is clear in Figure 2a–d—over almost the whole range of concentrations. For example, as shown in Figure 2a,b, the effect of hexanal on the sensor between 10 and 160 ppm is much smaller compared to that of 2-hexanone allowing the discrimination between both isomers. This can be clearly seen in the box-plot demonstration as shown in the Supporting Information, Figure S2. Regarding xylenes, p-xylene and o-xylene have a very similar effect on the mobility, but much different from the response of m-xylene. Vth responds differently to all the xylene isomers (Figure 2e), showing the weakest response to m-xylene. This means that p-xylene or o-xylene can be selectively discriminated from m-xylene—disregarding the concentration value—when these three compounds occur in a complex mixture. Moreover, the sensor can discriminate between p-xylene/m-xylene and o-xylene/m-xylene over the whole range of concentrations using only one parameter, either Vth or µ (Figure S3, Supporting Information). The fact that we can discriminate between two isomers over a wide range of concentrations is highly desirable in real applications for the ability to separate/distinguish different simultaneous stimuli. On the other hand, the discrimination between p-xylene and o-xylene is only limited to specific single concentrations. The ketones and aldehydes shown in this study have a negative effect on the mobility, which might be explained by the typical swelling of the polymer because of gas uptake, increasing the distance between polymer chains and decreasing conduction.

For heptanal and 3-heptanone, the difference in response is clear over the whole range of concentrations, where each compound has its own response–concentration curve. This could be due to different detection limits where aldehydes are detectable only in high concentrations compared to ketones (Figure 3a,b). We have also tested the evolution of responses of the sensors with time. In the previous experiments shown in Figure 1 the exposure time was only 5 min, but here we extended this period to 30 min to study the ability of discrimination when the normalized characteristics reach equilibrium. Figure 3e,f shows the change in µ and Vth of the sensor under exposure to 160 ppm of xylenes, and Figure 3g,h shows the change in µ and Vth of the sensor under exposure to 80 ppm of heptanal and 3-heptanone. The discrimination between isomers was preserved for the whole exposure step even though it is slightly more obvious immediately at the beginning of the exposure step. This might be explained by time delays originated from the different properties of the isomers. Noteworthily, the mobility response to xylenes is positive whereas it is negative for ketones/aldehydes. This feature is very useful for the selectivity between different families of isomers. In addition, one important advantage of our CDT-BTZ based sensors is the straightforward discrimination from the value of the drain current. This leads to a simple and direct read-out with no need for further data processing, such as pattern recognition algorithms, as shown in ref. 13. Figure 3i highlights the discrimination ability between xylenes at 40 ppm using the Ion of the transistor. Indeed, a very high accuracy value (90%) was obtained, which is quite unique for one-feature discrimination. To extend the study of the CDT-BZT based sensors, we have shown the responses of the sensor to four mixtures (three 1:1 binary mixtures and 1:1:1 tertiary mixture) of isomers with equal total concentration of 120 ppm. Figure 3j shows the responses of Ion to the different mixtures showing a good discrimination accuracy of 85%. These results are consistent with the trends obtained and shown before in Figures 2 and 3, where the ΔIon/|Ion| values to the different mixtures, from high to low, was: p/o, p/m/o, m/o, p/o. Despite the good discrimination between xylene mixtures shown in Figure 3i, it is difficult to use one single device feature of sensors for analyzing all concentrations and combination of xylenes. The ternary mixture is not easy to analyze but this approach still introduces a reliable detection for some binary mixtures, or ternary mixture at specific concentrations as shown in Figure 3i.

Inspired by the nature of olfactory systems, we have used arrays of cross-reactive features in conjunction with pattern recognition methods of multiple OFET parameters to obtain high selectivity toward different compounds.20 Using this approach, several parameters were combined to obtain optimal discriminations between isomers. In Figure 3k, µ and Vth were used as two cross-reactive parameters for the discrimination between all isomers over the range of concentrations of 20–160 for hexanal, 2-hexanone, heptanal, and 3-heptanone, and 20–320 ppm for xylenes. Discriminant function analysis (DFA) was used to examine the discriminatory ability using a combination of the two parameters. New parameters were defined by this method, CV1 and CV2, which are obtained from a linear combination of ΔIon/|Ion| and ΔVth/|Vth|. The combination of cross-reactive parameters was optimized to improve the discrimination. Figure 3k shows that the plot of CV2 versus CV1 gives a clear separation between all groups of isomers with an accuracy value of 80%. The only limiting part, as explained before, is the discrimination between o-xylene and p-xylene. However, this large-scale discrimination between all groups of isomers is still unprecedented.

As a summary of the key merits, the device has shown exceptional discrimination ability between different types of isomers. Namely, using only one parameter, for example, µ of the OFET, responses to xylenes were separated from one another at each specific concentration between 20 and 160 ppm. More importantly, responses to o-xylene and p-xylene were unprecedently separated from responses of m-xylene over the whole range (20–160 ppm). Ketones and aldehydes have also shown similar characteristics where sensors could discriminate between the two compounds over the range of 20–160 ppm using only one sensing parameter, µ or Vth. Furthermore, we found that the mobility response to xylenes is positive whereas for ketones/aldehydes it is negative under the range of 20–160 ppm, which makes for simple discrimination between all these families of isomers.

2.3 Investigation of the Discrimination Mechanism
The outstanding performance of CDT-BTZ in discriminating between multiple isomers raises a question regarding the sensing mechanism. We considered two types of responses: first, swelling of the active layer or/and change in the supramolecular order because of gas absorption. Second, electrical response caused by intermolecular interaction between the analytes and the CDT-BTZ. For testing the swelling response, ellipsometry measurements were used to check thickness changes following gas absorption. The change is correlated to the amount of gas absorbed by the active layer. This experiment showed no noticeable difference between the absorption of each isomer because the changes were very similar (Table S1, Supporting Information). From these results, we can safely infer that discrimination between isomers is mainly based on different electrical responses, but not on swelling behavior. For further examination of the effect of isomers on CDT-BTZ films, we took grazing-incidence scattering measurements to check if there was any change in the degree of supramolecular order after the absorption of gases. There were no noticeable changes in these measurements under the exposure to different isomers, which could be explained by the negligible effect of isomers on the crystalline domains because they diffuse mainly into the less compact amorphous regions. The d-spacing between the chains of CDT-BTZ was ≈3.05 nm (Figure S1, Supporting Information). In brief, we found no noticeable difference between the effects of isomers on the film swelling or the supramolecular order; therefore, we suggest that the different sensitivity might originate from unique intermolecular interactions between the isomers and the polymer. These interactions affect several characteristic physical parameters/properties of the sensing film, such as energy levels and work function.[13,20] Therefore, we proceeded to investigate the electrical interactions by calculating the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) and lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) levels under gas exposures using density functional theory (DFT). The calculations were derived from the CDT-BTZ monomer, which is supposed to provide a good approximation to the behavior of the whole polymer; however, the polymer is expected to have a more complicated response to the analytes. The HOMO and LUMO values obtained are summarized in Table 2. By comparing the effect of xylenes on the HOMO level, m-xylene has the most negative HOMO level, followed by p-xylenes and then o-xylene. Higher HOMO levels (more negative) lead to a higher hole injection barrier, which directly decreases the currents in the device. This is consistent with the Ion values obtained on exposure to different xylenes, where o-xylene has the highest Ion, followed by p-xylene and m-xylene; o > p > m. Heptanal has an outstanding effect on the energy levels of CDT-BTZ compared to the other isomers; however, this was not correlated to any corresponding responses of the sensing parameters. In addition, the optimized molecular structure of the CDT-BTZ and its complexes with the other isomers showed different electron density distribution (Figure 4). The optimized structures show that isomers preferably interact with the benzothiadiazole (BTZ) group, whereas the CDT group, which is shielded with long aliphatic chains, is less affected (Figure 4). The long aliphatic chains might interrupt the intermolecular interactions with isomers or prevent them from reaching the CDT group. In general, each isomer has its own electrical effect on the orbital energies and the electron density distribution of CDT-BTZ chemical structure, leading to a unique fingerprint for each compound.

2.4 Empirical Comparison with Other Polymers
To support our conclusions regarding the unique selectivity of CDT-BTZ, we compared its performance to other polymeric semiconductors including: poly(3-hexylthiophene) (P3HT), poly[2,5-bis(3-tetradecylthiophen-2-yl)thieno[3,2-b]thiophene] (PBTTT), poly[2,5-(2-octyldodecyl)-3,6-diketopyrrolopyrrole-alt-5,5-(2,5-di(thien-2-yl)thieno[3,2b]thiophene)] (PDPP-DTT), and poly[3,6-dithiophene-2-yl-2,5-di(2-octyldodecyl)-pyrrolo[3,4-c]pyrrole-1,4-dione-alt-naphthalene] (PDPP-TNT); Figure S4 of the Supporting Information gives more details. These polymers introduce different modes of conjugation ranging from homopolymers to donor–acceptor copolymers. Similar sensors were fabricated, using the same method and structure, and then exposed to the gas concentrations in the same experimental systems. The discrimination accuracy of the sensors was evaluated for comparison with those obtained with CDT-BTZ. Figure 3i,j showed that CDT-BTZ has a very high discrimination accuracy of 90% between xylenes at 40 ppm; however, the other polymers (P3HT, PBTTT, PDPP-DTT, and PDPP-TNT) failed to show any discrimination ability even after using the best combination of parameters for the DFA analysis (Figure S5, Supporting Information). In addition, the discrimination ability between isomers over the range of 10–160 ppm was examined; Table S2 of the Supporting Information summarizes the discrimination accuracy values of CDT-BTZ and the other polymers. As seen in the table, the other polymers reveal some kind of discrimination between ketones and aldehydes over the range of 10–160 ppm; however, the accuracy values obtained are much lower than those of the CDT-BTZ copolymer. For the xylenes, the other polymers, compared to CDT-BTZ, gave lower discrimination ability between the pairs of m-xylene/o-xylene and p-xylene/m-xylene over the range of 10–160 ppm, using µ as a sensing parameter. The highest accuracy was obtained with PBTTT; 85% and 80% for m-xylene/o-xylene and p-xylene/m-xylene, respectively. However, Vth responses to isomers were similar and gave very low accuracies of <60%. Similar to the performance of CDT-BTZ, discrimination between p-xylene and o-xylene was challenging. Unlike the other tested polymers, CDT-BTZ contains a functional benzothiadiazole group that is not shielded by aliphatic side chains, and contains two nitrogen sites that might have a significant role in interactions with isomers, leading to a different sensing behavior compared to normal thiophene groups found in the other polymers. Therefore, in order to test this assumption, we also examined the sensitivity of another TBT containing polymer, poly(diketopyrrolopyrrole-thiophene-benzothiadiazole-thiophene) (PDPP-TBT, see Figure S4, Supporting Information), toward the studied xylenes, ketones, and aldehydes. This polymer did not show any remarkable selectivity toward xylenes while the selectivity to hexanal, 2-hexanone, heptanal, and 3-heptanone was comparable to CDT-BTZ (Table S2, Supporting Information). The superior performance of CDT-BTZ might be originated from the specific combination between electron-deficient and electron-rich groups which introduce differently interacting sites along the polymer backbone. The role of the backbone interacting sites and functional groups needs further investigations to better understand the molecular origin of discrimination between isomers.

To summarize, we have introduced a new gas sensor based on an OFET fabricated from a donor–acceptor polymer semiconductor with high charge carrier mobility. This device allows selective discrimination between multiple isomers over a wide range of concentrations. The high discrimination ability could, in principle, originate from different electrical interactions, such as doping and trapping, between the isomers with the polymer backbone, especially the electron deficient benzothiadiazole, giving a unique sensing fingerprint for each isomer. These conclusions were justified by DFT calculations that pointed to distinct polymer–isomer interactions (mainly on the benzothiadiazole moiety). In addition, the performance of CDT-BTZ was compared to those of other semiconductors to support the role of the monomer structure in this high selectivity. This work is mainly focused on sensor performance and its discrimination ability between isomers. Further work is also needed to better understand the main interaction between isomers and CDT-BTZ. From the viewpoint of practical sensing, the superior selectivity obtained in this work, which is not just limited to only one type of isomers, is unprecedented compared to other reported devices. Therefore, this sensor will be a great alternative to current complicated or limited isomer discrimination methods, even more so because it can be applied for environmental monitoring and noninvasive diagnostic technologies.

3 Experimental Section
Materials: P3HT (regioregular, average Mn 54 000–75 000, electronic grade, 99.995% trace metals basis) was purchased from Sigma-Aldrich. CDT-BTZ (C16, Mn 28 000), PBTTT (C14, Mn > 50 000), (Mn 76 323), PDPP-TNT (Mn 63 750), and PDPP-TBT (Mn 60 200) were synthesized as reported previously in literature.16-18, 21-26

Fabrication of the FET Sensor: CDT-BTZ OFETs were prepared on silicon wafers capped with 300 nm thermal oxide. A Ti/Au (10/200 nm) bottom-gate electrode was deposited by electron beam evaporation. A total of 49 pairs of circular interdigitated source and drain Au (300 nm) electrodes were prepared using photolithography and electron-beam evaporation. The outer diameter of the circular electrode area was 3000 µm. The gap between the two adjacent electrodes (channel length) and the width of each electrode were 25 and 5 µm, respectively. The silicon wafers were treated with plasma and immersed in octadecyltrichlorosilane (ODTS)/toluene solution (60 × 10−3 m, 20 mL) for 10 h. Finally, CDT-BTZ films were deposited on the OTS-treated substrates by drop-casting using a CDT-BTZ solution (2 mg mL−1) in chloroform. The surface morphology of the CDT-BTZ film was characterized by SEM.

FET-Sensor Measurements: CDT-BTZ OFET sensor was loaded into a stainless-steel chamber of ≈300 cm3. For the exposure of pure vapors, a computer-controlled bubbler was filled with the corresponding liquid, and then N2 was bubbled through. The resulting gas mixture (N2 and organic vapor) was diluted to obtain the desired concentrations. For the preparation of mixtures, the outputs of several bubblers were mixed together and diluted as needed. Vacuum was used as a baseline between gas exposure cycles. The total flow rate was kept at 5 L min−1 during the experiment. Source–drain current Ids versus gate voltage Vg measurements (swept between 5 and −25 V with 0.5 V steps and −25 V constant source–drain voltage, Vds) were carried out using a Keithley 2636A system SourceMeter and a 3706 system Switch/Multimeter. Extraction of the sensing parameters following the FET measurements is given in detail in Section S1 (Supporting Information).

DFT Calculations: B3LYP/3-21G simulator was used to calculate the optimized molecular structure, and the HOMO and LUMO energies of complexes of CDT-BTZ with each isomer under vacuum.

Discriminant Function Analysis: DFA analysis used JMP 10 software (SAS Institute, Inc.). DFA finds linear combinations of sensing features that separate two or more classes of objects. In binary classification, DFA predicts membership in two categories based on known continuous sensing responses. For the discrimination between all isomer groups, CV1 and CV2, a linear combination of ΔIon/|Ion| and ΔVth/|Vth|, were used. CV1 = 0.631 × ΔIon/|Ion| − 2.073 × ΔVth/|Vth|, and CV2 = 0.502 × ΔIon/|Ion| + 0.995 × ΔVth/|Vth|.

Abstract
Distinguishing structural isomers is a critical and challenging task for biotechnology, chemical industry, and environmental monitoring. Approaches currently available are limited in terms of selectivity and simplicity. In this paper, a highly sensitive organic field-effect transistor (OFET) using the cyclopentadithiophene-benzothiadiazole (CDT-BTZ) copolymers as a semiconductor is presented for easy and selective detection of different families of structural isomers, as well as between different isomers within each family. High accuracy discrimination is achieved over a range of concentrations using only a single sensing parameter derived from the OFET characteristic transfer curve. As a reference, other homopolymer- and donor–acceptor copolymer-based OFET sensors are examined but do not have an equivalent sensing performance to that of the CDT-BTZ-based OFETs. 1. Introduction
Structural isomers of volatile organic compounds play an important role in biotechnology as well as pharmaceutical and chemical industries.1 For example, xylene has three isomers, namely, p-xylene, m-xylene, and o-xylene, and is widely utilized in the synthesis of important chemical products, including chemical intermediates of polyesters, rubber, and other polymers.2 Ketone and aldehyde isomers are used in a variety of applications, and are tightly connected to health issues.3 Indeed, volatile organic isomers have varying degrees of harmfulness at high concentrations; for example, the U.S. National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health (NIOSH) has recommended a time-weighted average permissible exposure limit of 100 ppm for xylenes, whereas for some ketones, such as 3-heptanone, it is only 50 ppm.4 Hence, accurate detection, monitoring, and discrimination of these compounds are essential in environmental monitoring and healthcare.

Gas chromatographic mass spectrometry has long been applied for the accurate detection and quantification of structural isomers.5 However, it requires expensive equipment and is of limited availability. Gas sensors can be more reliable and applicable in this field since they hold promise for cheap and fast isomer detection. So far, mass sensor arrays,6 metal oxide sensors,7, 8 chemiresistors,9-11 field-effect transistors (FETs),12, 13 and cataluminescent sensors14 have been introduced as practical discrimination tools. Haick and co-workers13 have reported a highly selective gas sensor based on an ambipolar diketopyrrolopyrrole FET for sensing xylene isomers. Successful detection and discrimination between isomers with <40 ppm concentrations were achieved only after applying machine learning on a combination of device parameters, including carrier mobility (µ), threshold voltage (Vth), and subthreshold swing. Cross-reactive chemiresistors array based on gold nanoparticles or carbon nanostructures have enabled detection and discriminative performances between ketone and aldehyde isomers, such as heptanal/3-heptanone and hexanal/2-hexanone.10, 11 The discriminatory power of most of the reported sensors was largely limited to high concentrations or extreme conditions. For example, high detection limits of 103 and 400 ppm were reported in refs. 6 and 1 respectively; 5 ppm p-xylene were detected using cobalt-doped zinc oxide nanowire-based sensors. However, such metal-oxide-semiconductor-based sensors work only at relatively high temperatures (>400 °C), and no selectivity with regard to the other isomers of xylenes could be achieved.7 Other methods have used multiple cross-reactive parameters and can only discriminate between one type of isomers at a specific concentration.10, 11, 13 Accordingly, a reliable selective sensor for detecting multiple and chemically diverse isomers over a wide range of concentrations is desirable from both fundamental and practical viewpoints.

2 Results and Discussion
2.1 Device Fabrication and Performance
Herein, we report on a highly selective OFET gas sensor based on high mobility donor–acceptor copolymer (CDT-BTZ) that can discriminate between multiple isomers (derivatives of xylenes, ketones, and aldehydes) over a wide range of concentrations using only one sensing parameter. OFETs based on conjugated polymers or small molecules are promising sensor platforms because they have various advantages, including high sensitivity, mechanical flexibility, and low-cost fabrication processes.15 CDT-BTZ was used as a semiconductor and an active sensing component in a typical OFET structure of bottom gate/bottom contact. The semiconductor material was drop-cast on the source–drain electrodes and hydrophobically modified SiO2 (Figure 1a,b). Figure 1c is a scanning electron microscope (SEM) image of the semiconductor film composed of a very dense polymer network. The ordered packing in the solid-state contributes to the high mobility of the polymer.16-18 Moreover, it increases the exposed surface area, improving interactions with analytes, and increasing the detection ability of sensors. Grazing-incidence scattering was used to measure the degree of supramolecular order of CDT films. It shows that the d-spacing between the chains of CDT-BTZ is ≈3.05 nm under ambient conditions (Figure S1, Supporting Information). Figure 1d,e presents output and transfer curves of the transistor and shows a typical behavior under ambient conditions. Several parameters, such as the carrier mobility (µ), threshold voltage (Vth), on-current (Ion) and the source–drain currents (Ids) at different gate voltages (Vg)—all of which are extracted from the OFET transfer curves—were monitored during exposure to isomers and used as sensing features. In most cases, discrimination was achieved using only one feature. However, by combining multiple sensing features derived from a single OFET and/or by using a pattern-recognition algorithm, chemical fingerprints for each isomer could be obtained with greater accuracy for a very wide range of concentrations.

2.2 Sensor Performance under Exposure to Organic Isomers
In this report, CDT-BTZ OFETs were used to detect seven important, but chemically different, volatile organic isomers at different concentrations (Table 1). These isomers were chosen for their significance in many aspects of life: xylenes, which are found in areas close to gas stations, for their relation to environmental monitoring and the necessity to ensure their concentration is under the time-weighted average permissible exposure limit of 100 ppm; and heptanal, 3-heptanone, hexanal, and 2-hexanone for their role as diagnostic biomarkers of diseases and health monitoring.3, 19

Based on the OFET transfer curve, changes in multiple sensing parameters, such as µ, Vth, Ion, or Ids at different values of Vg were extracted. The normalized parameters have different or cross-reactive responses toward the isomers, and the discrimination ability of each parameter was determined by the accuracy value, which is the proportion of true results (true positives and true negatives) of the total number of cases examined (see explanation in Section S1, Supporting Information). For simplicity, we focused on two parameters, µ and Vth, to analyze the responses and the discrimination ability. However, other parameters, such as current values, were also used. Figure 2a–f shows the response of the parameters to different concentrations of isomers in the range of 10–320 ppm for xylenes and 10–160 ppm for the hexanal, 2-hexanone, heptanal, and 3-heptanone. The huge difference in responses to aldehydes (heptanal and hexanal) and ketones (3-heptanone and 2-hexanone) is clear in Figure 2a–d—over almost the whole range of concentrations. For example, as shown in Figure 2a,b, the effect of hexanal on the sensor between 10 and 160 ppm is much smaller compared to that of 2-hexanone allowing the discrimination between both isomers. This can be clearly seen in the box-plot demonstration as shown in the Supporting Information, Figure S2. Regarding xylenes, p-xylene and o-xylene have a very similar effect on the mobility, but much different from the response of m-xylene. Vth responds differently to all the xylene isomers (Figure 2e), showing the weakest response to m-xylene. This means that p-xylene or o-xylene can be selectively discriminated from m-xylene—disregarding the concentration value—when these three compounds occur in a complex mixture. Moreover, the sensor can discriminate between p-xylene/m-xylene and o-xylene/m-xylene over the whole range of concentrations using only one parameter, either Vth or µ (Figure S3, Supporting Information). The fact that we can discriminate between two isomers over a wide range of concentrations is highly desirable in real applications for the ability to separate/distinguish different simultaneous stimuli. On the other hand, the discrimination between p-xylene and o-xylene is only limited to specific single concentrations. The ketones and aldehydes shown in this study have a negative effect on the mobility, which might be explained by the typical swelling of the polymer because of gas uptake, increasing the distance between polymer chains and decreasing conduction.

Figure 3a–d shows the normalized responses of the parameters to different isomers. For heptanal and 3-heptanone, the difference in response is clear over the whole range of concentrations, where each compound has its own response–concentration curve. This could be due to different detection limits where aldehydes are detectable only in high concentrations compared to ketones (Figure 3a,b). The same behavior is found with xylenes, as p-xylene and o-xylene were detected at lower concentrations compared to m-xylenes (Figure 3c,d). We have also tested the evolution of responses of the sensors with time. In the previous experiments shown in Figure 1 the exposure time was only 5 min, but here we extended this period to 30 min to study the ability of discrimination when the normalized characteristics reach equilibrium. Figure 3e,f shows the change in µ and Vth of the sensor under exposure to 160 ppm of xylenes, and Figure 3g,h shows the change in µ and Vth of the sensor under exposure to 80 ppm of heptanal and 3-heptanone. The discrimination between isomers was preserved for the whole exposure step even though it is slightly more obvious immediately at the beginning of the exposure step. This might be explained by time delays originated from the different properties of the isomers. Noteworthily, the mobility response to xylenes is positive whereas it is negative for ketones/aldehydes. This feature is very useful for the selectivity between different families of isomers. In addition, one important advantage of our CDT-BTZ based sensors is the straightforward discrimination from the value of the drain current. This leads to a simple and direct read-out with no need for further data processing, such as pattern recognition algorithms, as shown in ref. 13. Figure 3i highlights the discrimination ability between xylenes at 40 ppm using the Ion of the transistor. Indeed, a very high accuracy value (90%) was obtained, which is quite unique for one-feature discrimination. To extend the study of the CDT-BZT based sensors, we have shown the responses of the sensor to four mixtures (three 1:1 binary mixtures and 1:1:1 tertiary mixture) of isomers with equal total concentration of 120 ppm. Figure 3j shows the responses of Ion to the different mixtures showing a good discrimination accuracy of 85%. These results are consistent with the trends obtained and shown before in Figures 2 and 3, where the ΔIon/|Ion| values to the different mixtures, from high to low, was: p/o, p/m/o, m/o, p/o. Despite the good discrimination between xylene mixtures shown in Figure 3i, it is difficult to use one single device feature of sensors for analyzing all concentrations and combination of xylenes. The ternary mixture is not easy to analyze but this approach still introduces a reliable detection for some binary mixtures, or ternary mixture at specific concentrations as shown in Figure 3i.

Inspired by the nature of olfactory systems, we have used arrays of cross-reactive features in conjunction with pattern recognition methods of multiple OFET parameters to obtain high selectivity toward different compounds.20 Using this approach, several parameters were combined to obtain optimal discriminations between isomers. In Figure 3k, µ and Vth were used as two cross-reactive parameters for the discrimination between all isomers over the range of concentrations of 20–160 for hexanal, 2-hexanone, heptanal, and 3-heptanone, and 20–320 ppm for xylenes. Discriminant function analysis (DFA) was used to examine the discriminatory ability using a combination of the two parameters. New parameters were defined by this method, CV1 and CV2, which are obtained from a linear combination of ΔIon/|Ion| and ΔVth/|Vth|. The combination of cross-reactive parameters was optimized to improve the discrimination. Figure 3k shows that the plot of CV2 versus CV1 gives a clear separation between all groups of isomers with an accuracy value of 80%. The only limiting part, as explained before, is the discrimination between o-xylene and p-xylene. However, this large-scale discrimination between all groups of isomers is still unprecedented.

As a summary of the key merits, the device has shown exceptional discrimination ability between different types of isomers. Namely, using only one parameter, for example, µ of the OFET, responses to xylenes were separated from one another at each specific concentration between 20 and 160 ppm. More importantly, responses to o-xylene and p-xylene were unprecedently separated from responses of m-xylene over the whole range (20–160 ppm). Ketones and aldehydes have also shown similar characteristics where sensors could discriminate between the two compounds over the range of 20–160 ppm using only one sensing parameter, µ or Vth. Furthermore, we found that the mobility response to xylenes is positive whereas for ketones/aldehydes it is negative under the range of 20–160 ppm, which makes for simple discrimination between all these families of isomers.

2.3 Investigation of the Discrimination Mechanism
The outstanding performance of CDT-BTZ in discriminating between multiple isomers raises a question regarding the sensing mechanism. We considered two types of responses: first, swelling of the active layer or/and change in the supramolecular order because of gas absorption. Second, electrical response caused by intermolecular interaction between the analytes and the CDT-BTZ. For testing the swelling response, ellipsometry measurements were used to check thickness changes following gas absorption. The change is correlated to the amount of gas absorbed by the active layer. This experiment showed no noticeable difference between the absorption of each isomer because the changes were very similar (Table S1, Supporting Information). From these results, we can safely infer that discrimination between isomers is mainly based on different electrical responses, but not on swelling behavior. For further examination of the effect of isomers on CDT-BTZ films, we took grazing-incidence scattering measurements to check if there was any change in the degree of supramolecular order after the absorption of gases. There were no noticeable changes in these measurements under the exposure to different isomers, which could be explained by the negligible effect of isomers on the crystalline domains because they diffuse mainly into the less compact amorphous regions. The d-spacing between the chains of CDT-BTZ was ≈3.05 nm (Figure S1, Supporting Information). In brief, we found no noticeable difference between the effects of isomers on the film swelling or the supramolecular order; therefore, we suggest that the different sensitivity might originate from unique intermolecular interactions between the isomers and the polymer. These interactions affect several characteristic physical parameters/properties of the sensing film, such as energy levels and work function.[13,20] Therefore, we proceeded to investigate the electrical interactions by calculating the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) and lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) levels under gas exposures using density functional theory (DFT). The calculations were derived from the CDT-BTZ monomer, which is supposed to provide a good approximation to the behavior of the whole polymer; however, the polymer is expected to have a more complicated response to the analytes. The HOMO and LUMO values obtained are summarized in Table 2. By comparing the effect of xylenes on the HOMO level, m-xylene has the most negative HOMO level, followed by p-xylenes and then o-xylene. Higher HOMO levels (more negative) lead to a higher hole injection barrier, which directly decreases the currents in the device. This is consistent with the Ion values obtained on exposure to different xylenes, where o-xylene has the highest Ion, followed by p-xylene and m-xylene; o > p > m. Heptanal has an outstanding effect on the energy levels of CDT-BTZ compared to the other isomers; however, this was not correlated to any corresponding responses of the sensing parameters. In addition, the optimized molecular structure of the CDT-BTZ and its complexes with the other isomers showed different electron density distribution (Figure 4). The optimized structures show that isomers preferably interact with the benzothiadiazole (BTZ) group, whereas the CDT group, which is shielded with long aliphatic chains, is less affected (Figure 4). The long aliphatic chains might interrupt the intermolecular interactions with isomers or prevent them from reaching the CDT group. In general, each isomer has its own electrical effect on the orbital energies and the electron density distribution of CDT-BTZ chemical structure, leading to a unique fingerprint for each compound.

2.4 Empirical Comparison with Other Polymers
To support our conclusions regarding the unique selectivity of CDT-BTZ, we compared its performance to other polymeric semiconductors including: poly(3-hexylthiophene) (P3HT), poly[2,5-bis(3-tetradecylthiophen-2-yl)thieno[3,2-b]thiophene] (PBTTT), poly[2,5-(2-octyldodecyl)-3,6-diketopyrrolopyrrole-alt-5,5-(2,5-di(thien-2-yl)thieno[3,2b]thiophene)] (PDPP-DTT), and poly[3,6-dithiophene-2-yl-2,5-di(2-octyldodecyl)-pyrrolo[3,4-c]pyrrole-1,4-dione-alt-naphthalene] (PDPP-TNT); Figure S4 of the Supporting Information gives more details. These polymers introduce different modes of conjugation ranging from homopolymers to donor–acceptor copolymers. Similar sensors were fabricated, using the same method and structure, and then exposed to the gas concentrations in the same experimental systems. The discrimination accuracy of the sensors was evaluated for comparison with those obtained with CDT-BTZ. Figure 3i,j showed that CDT-BTZ has a very high discrimination accuracy of 90% between xylenes at 40 ppm; however, the other polymers (P3HT, PBTTT, PDPP-DTT, and PDPP-TNT) failed to show any discrimination ability even after using the best combination of parameters for the DFA analysis (Figure S5, Supporting Information). In addition, the discrimination ability between isomers over the range of 10–160 ppm was examined; Table S2 of the Supporting Information summarizes the discrimination accuracy values of CDT-BTZ and the other polymers. As seen in the table, the other polymers reveal some kind of discrimination between ketones and aldehydes over the range of 10–160 ppm; however, the accuracy values obtained are much lower than those of the CDT-BTZ copolymer. For the xylenes, the other polymers, compared to CDT-BTZ, gave lower discrimination ability between the pairs of m-xylene/o-xylene and p-xylene/m-xylene over the range of 10–160 ppm, using µ as a sensing parameter. The highest accuracy was obtained with PBTTT; 85% and 80% for m-xylene/o-xylene and p-xylene/m-xylene, respectively. However, Vth responses to isomers were similar and gave very low accuracies of <60%. Similar to the performance of CDT-BTZ, discrimination between p-xylene and o-xylene was challenging. Unlike the other tested polymers, CDT-BTZ contains a functional benzothiadiazole group that is not shielded by aliphatic side chains, and contains two nitrogen sites that might have a significant role in interactions with isomers, leading to a different sensing behavior compared to normal thiophene groups found in the other polymers. Therefore, in order to test this assumption, we also examined the sensitivity of another TBT containing polymer, poly(diketopyrrolopyrrole-thiophene-benzothiadiazole-thiophene) (PDPP-TBT, see Figure S4, Supporting Information), toward the studied xylenes, ketones, and aldehydes. This polymer did not show any remarkable selectivity toward xylenes while the selectivity to hexanal, 2-hexanone, heptanal, and 3-heptanone was comparable to CDT-BTZ (Table S2, Supporting Information). The superior performance of CDT-BTZ might be originated from the specific combination between electron-deficient and electron-rich groups which introduce differently interacting sites along the polymer backbone. The role of the backbone interacting sites and functional groups needs further investigations to better understand the molecular origin of discrimination between isomers.

To summarize, we have introduced a new gas sensor based on an OFET fabricated from a donor–acceptor polymer semiconductor with high charge carrier mobility. This device allows selective discrimination between multiple isomers over a wide range of concentrations. The high discrimination ability could, in principle, originate from different electrical interactions, such as doping and trapping, between the isomers with the polymer backbone, especially the electron deficient benzothiadiazole, giving a unique sensing fingerprint for each isomer. These conclusions were justified by DFT calculations that pointed to distinct polymer–isomer interactions (mainly on the benzothiadiazole moiety). In addition, the performance of CDT-BTZ was compared to those of other semiconductors to support the role of the monomer structure in this high selectivity. This work is mainly focused on sensor performance and its discrimination ability between isomers. Further work is also needed to better understand the main interaction between isomers and CDT-BTZ. From the viewpoint of practical sensing, the superior selectivity obtained in this work, which is not just limited to only one type of isomers, is unprecedented compared to other reported devices. Therefore, this sensor will be a great alternative to current complicated or limited isomer discrimination methods, even more so because it can be applied for environmental monitoring and noninvasive diagnostic technologies.

3 Experimental Section
Materials: P3HT (regioregular, average Mn 54 000–75 000, electronic grade, 99.995% trace metals basis) was purchased from Sigma-Aldrich. CDT-BTZ (C16, Mn 28 000), PBTTT (C14, Mn > 50 000), (Mn 76 323), PDPP-TNT (Mn 63 750), and PDPP-TBT (Mn 60 200) were synthesized as reported previously in literature.16-18, 21-26

Fabrication of the FET Sensor: CDT-BTZ OFETs were prepared on silicon wafers capped with 300 nm thermal oxide. A Ti/Au (10/200 nm) bottom-gate electrode was deposited by electron beam evaporation. A total of 49 pairs of circular interdigitated source and drain Au (300 nm) electrodes were prepared using photolithography and electron-beam evaporation. The outer diameter of the circular electrode area was 3000 µm. The gap between the two adjacent electrodes (channel length) and the width of each electrode were 25 and 5 µm, respectively. The silicon wafers were treated with plasma and immersed in octadecyltrichlorosilane (ODTS)/toluene solution (60 × 10−3 m, 20 mL) for 10 h. Finally, CDT-BTZ films were deposited on the OTS-treated substrates by drop-casting using a CDT-BTZ solution (2 mg mL−1) in chloroform. The surface morphology of the CDT-BTZ film was characterized by SEM.

FET-Sensor Measurements: CDT-BTZ OFET sensor was loaded into a stainless-steel chamber of ≈300 cm3. For the exposure of pure vapors, a computer-controlled bubbler was filled with the corresponding liquid, and then N2 was bubbled through. The resulting gas mixture (N2 and organic vapor) was diluted to obtain the desired concentrations. For the preparation of mixtures, the outputs of several bubblers were mixed together and diluted as needed. Vacuum was used as a baseline between gas exposure cycles. The total flow rate was kept at 5 L min−1 during the experiment. Source–drain current Ids versus gate voltage Vg measurements (swept between 5 and −25 V with 0.5 V steps and −25 V constant source–drain voltage, Vds) were carried out using a Keithley 2636A system SourceMeter and a 3706 system Switch/Multimeter. Extraction of the sensing parameters following the FET measurements is given in detail in Section S1 (Supporting Information).

DFT Calculations: B3LYP/3-21G simulator was used to calculate the optimized molecular structure, and the HOMO and LUMO energies of complexes of CDT-BTZ with each isomer under vacuum.

Discriminant Function Analysis: DFA analysis used JMP 10 software (SAS Institute, Inc.). DFA finds linear combinations of sensing features that separate two or more classes of objects. In binary classification, DFA predicts membership in two categories based on known continuous sensing responses. For the discrimination between all isomer groups, CV1 and CV2, a linear combination of ΔIon/|Ion| and ΔVth/|Vth|, were used. CV1 = 0.631 × ΔIon/|Ion| − 2.073 × ΔVth/|Vth|, and CV2 = 0.502 × ΔIon/|Ion| + 0.995 × ΔVth/|Vth|.

Accuracy evaluation
The accuracy value describes the ability to discriminate between groups of gases. After
plotting the responses of the sensor to different gases, if separate clusters (that correspond
to each group of gases) are obtained, this means that the sensor can discriminate between
the gases. By assigning a specific range of values to each group, the accuracy can be
calculated by the portion of points found in the correct corresponding range. The highest
accuracy is obtained when having completely separated clusters.
