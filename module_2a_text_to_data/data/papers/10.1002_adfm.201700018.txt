Abstract
The thin-film structures of chemical sensors based on conventional organic field-effect transistors (OFETs) can limit the sensitivity of the devices toward chemical vapors, because charge carriers in OFETs are usually concentrated within a few molecular layers at the bottom of the organic semiconductor (OSC) film near the dielectric/semiconductor interface. Chemical vapor molecules have to diffuse through the OSC films before they can interact with charge carriers in the OFET conduction channel. It has been demonstrated that OFET ammonia sensors with porous OSC films can be fabricated by a simple vacuum freeze-drying template method. The resulted devices can have ammonia sensitivity not only much higher than the pristine OFETs with thin-film structure but also better than any previously reported OFET sensors, to the best of our knowledge. The porous OFETs show a relative sensitivity as high as 340% ppm−1 upon exposure to 10 parts per billion (ppb) NH3. In addition, the devices also exhibit decent selectivity and stability. This general and simple strategy can be applied to a wide range of OFET chemical sensors to improve the device sensitivity.

1 Introduction
In the past decades, organic field-effect transistors (OFETs) have been extensively explored for various applications such as radio frequency identification tags, display drivers, and circuits of moderate complexity.1-8 OFET-based sensor is another compelling application for OFETs, such as pressure sensors, photo sensors, and chemical sensors.9-16 The organic semiconductor (OSC) layer is a key factor in achieving high-performance OFET-based chemical sensors.17, 18 To date, remarkable enhancements in the sensing performance of OFET-based chemical sensors have been accomplished by improvements in both materials and processing methods, such as designing OSC molecules with specific π-conjugated core structures and side chains, bonding or depositing receptors on OSCs, modifying OSC film thickness.19-23 In the previous studies, these optimizations were usually achieved based on continuous and compact OSC thin-films, which actually limited the sensor sensitivity. The sensing response of an OFET-based sensor is usually due to the interactions of analytes with the charge carriers in OSCs conduction channel and the electrodes, such as doping or quenching induced charge carrier density variation, dipole-induced trapping and retarding of charges, and change in charge injection energy barriers.24-28 These interactions lead to changes in the threshold voltage, charge mobility and output source-drain current. However, the conduction channel of an OFET is usually concentrated within a few molecular layers at the bottom of the semiconductor film, close to the dielectric/semiconductor interface,29-32 so that the device structure of conventional OFET-based sensors with continuous and compact OSC thin-films often restricts their sensing performance. Analyte molecules have to diffuse through the organic semiconductor films before they can interact with charge carriers in the conduction channel,33-35 which limits the sensor sensitivity and response speed. Although OFETs based on organic semiconductor nanowire structures have been developed,36-38 the fabrication of those devices requires rather complicated techniques, and the device performances of nanowire OFETs can be quite different from batches to batches, which is not desirable for sensing applications.

In this work, we have developed a general strategy with a simple and effective method to overcome this limitation. Our strategy involves incorporating ultrathin porous OSC films into OFET chemical sensors. The ultrathin micro-porous OSC films were fabricated by a versatile template method. The porous OSC structure provides additional and direct pathways for analytes to interact with charge carriers in conduction channel. As a demonstration of this strategy, OFET chemical sensors with ultrathin porous OSC films were tested upon exposure to diluted NH3 vapors, and showed much higher sensitivity than the pristine OFET with the same OSC thickness. The porous OFET also exhibited higher sensitivity to NH3 than any previously reported OFET sensors,39-45 to the best of our knowledge, along with decent selectivity and stability. This strategy is general and simple, which can be applied to nearly all OFET chemical sensors.

2 Results and Discussion
The OFETs were fabricated with a bottom-gate top contact configuration. Heavily doped silicon wafers with 300 nm SiO2 dielectric layers were used as substrates. Polystyrene microspheres were employed as templates to fabricate the micro porous OSC films. First, polystyrene microspheres were deposited on plasma-treated SiO2/Si substrates by vacuum freeze-drying method. OSC films of Dinaphtho [2,3-b:2′,3′-f] thieno [3,2-b] thiophene (DNTT) were then thermally evaporated on the substrates after that. To ensure high sensitivity, only 10 nm of DNTT was deposited, since reducing OSC film thickness can enhance sensor sensitivity.46, 47 After that, adhesive tapes were used to remove polystyrene microspheres, leaving porous OSC films on substrates. Finally, gold source and drain electrodes were thermally evaporated through shadow masks. Control samples without the porous structure were fabricated with similar process without the use of the polystyrene microspheres. The device structures and the molecule structure of DNTT are illustrated in Figure 1a. A homemade sensor testing chamber connected with Keithley 4200 semiconductor characterization system and mass-flow controller were used to perform the sensing test. N2 was used as the background vapor unless specifically noting that air was used.

As shown in Figure 1a, the porous structure provides efficient and direct pathways for analytes to reach the conduction channel, improves the direct interaction between the analytes and charge carriers in the conductive channel, and result in more effective sensing activity in the porous OFET than the traditional OFET with condensed OSC film. Accordingly, the sensitivity can be improved. Figure 1b,c shows optical images of the porous DNTT films with polystyrene microspheres and after removing polystyrene microspheres, respectively. From Figure 1c, 10 µm scale pores can be observed obviously which provide expedient diffusion of the analyte molecules. The film coverage of the porous film devices is about 55%. Further reducing the film coverage would result in devices which are not working, because the film will not be continued. Figure 1d shows the fabrication procedure of the porous OFET-based sensors. Details of the fabrication process are described in the Experimental Section.

The electrical characteristics of a porous DNTT OFET and a pristine DNTT OFET were investigated and compared, as shown in Figure 2. On the basis of the transfer characteristics and output I–V curves, both devices exhibited typical p-type OFET characteristics. The ID–VD curves of these two OFETs are shown in Figure 2a,b. Both devices exhibited clear linear and saturation regions. Figure 2c shows the transfer characteristics of the devices. The output current ID of the porous OFET is 5 times lower than that of the pristine OFET, due to the reduced organic semiconductor coverage in the conduction channel of the OFET. The porous OFET is equivalent to a pristine OFET with reduced channel width. Despite the reduced ID, the porous OFET show ample device performance for sensing applications, although the output current is lower than that of the pristine OFET devices.

To demonstrate the sensing performance of our porous OFETs, we tested and compared the sensing performance of bother devices upon exposure to NH3 vapor at various concentrations. The US Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) limit NH3 exposure to 50 ppm for 8 h per day, and 25 ppm for 10 h per day and 40 h per week in the workplace. Therefore, it is necessary for NH3 sensors to detect NH3 at very low concentration. In addition, NH3 detection is also important in medical applications. For example, when a patient has liver diseases or urea balance problem, the breath NH3 concentration can be a few hundred ppb higher than normal persons. NH3 sensors with high sensitivity and low detection limit can potentially be used in diagnostic breath analysis system.53 We tested the devices output currents in a homemade test chamber and injected NH3 vapors at various concentrations ranging from 0 to 10 ppm (v/v). Figure 3a,c shows the transfer characteristic curves of the pristine OFET and porous OFET upon exposure to NH3 at different concentrations, respectively. The ID of both devices decreased after the device was exposed to diluted NH3 vapor. The porous OFET exhibited more significant response than the pristine OFET at all NH3 concentrations. Figure 3b,d plots the normalized source-drain current (ID/ID,0) response of the two devices as a function of the gate voltage. Figure S1 (Supporting Information) shows the responses of the pristine OFET and the porous OFET to various NH3 concentrations, measured at low VG from −20 to 0 V. The normalized response is larger at lower gate voltage, and the devices exhibited highest sensitivity when the gate voltage was around −10 V. It can be noted that, after 0.1 ppm NH3 vapor was injected, the pristine OFET showed negligible response, but the porous OFET exhibited obvious response, with more than 10% current decrease under VG = −10 V. The pristine OFET started to show obvious response when the NH3 concentration was increased to 0.5 ppm. At high VG, the difference in sensor sensitivity of the two devices is more obvious when the analyte concentration is high. Upon exposure to 10 ppm NH3 vapor and under VG = −60 V, the porous OFET was almost completely quenched, with the ID decreased to less than 7% of its original value, while the ID of the pristine OFET only reduced to 46% of its original value.

The change of effective charge mobility (μE) and threshold voltage (VTH) of the devices was extracted from the transfer characteristic curves and plotted in Figure 3e,f as a function of NH3 concentration. When the injected NH3 vapor concentration was increased, charge density in the conduction channel was reduced, the μE of the devices was then reduced, and the VTH gradually shifted in the negative direction, due to charge trapping effect and the quenching effect of the NH3 molecules with the positive charge carriers (holes) in the p-type OFETs.43 Figure 3e,f compares the change of μE and VTH of both devices as a function of NH3 concentration. At all NH3 concentrations, the changes of both the μE and the VTH were more significant for the porous OFET than that of the pristine OFET. The porous structure provides more direct paths for the NH3 molecules to interact with the charge carriers in the conduction channel near the bottom layer of the semiconductor film, so that more charges can be trapped and quenched in the porous OFET than those in the pristine device, in which the NH3 molecules have to diffuse through the semiconductor film to interact with charges. When 0.5 ppm NH3 was injected, the VTH of the porous OFET changed by 2.2 V, while the VTH of the pristine device showed almost no change (0.18 V). At high NH3 concentration of 10 ppm, the porous OFET showed μE decreased by 70%, which is again much larger than the 30% mobility reduction of the pristine device. The sensing mechanism of our porous OFET sensors is the same as most p-type OFET chemical sensors, which is the quenching and trapping of the positive charge carriers (holes) in the OSC by NH3. The free hole densities were reduced, the threshold voltage became more negative, and the effective charge mobility was decreased upon exposure to NH3. The porous structure simply provides additional and fast pathways for efficient hole/NH3 interactions in the conduction-channel around the pores. We did not change the sensing mechanism of the OFET sensors, but simply enhanced the interactions. That makes our strategy a general approach, which can be applied to many other OFET chemical sensors.

To further compare the sensitivity of the devices, the current changes of the devices at fixed VG were monitored with time during sensing tests. Air with a certain relative humidity (RH) was used as background vapor when testing recovery properties of the devices. As shown in Figure 4a, when VG = −10 V, the porous OFET exhibited 13% current decrease rapidly right after 0.1 ppm NH3 was injected, while the pristine OFET only showed 3.5% current decrease, which is consistent with the results shown in Figure 3b,d. The response speed of the porous device was also faster than that of the pristine device. When 1 ppm NH3 was injected, the porous OFET exhibited 73% ID decrease, and t90 = 95 s, which means 90% of the changes of ID happened in 95 s and then reach a relatively steady value, which is again a more significant and faster response than the 32% current decrease of the pristine OFET with t90 = 200 s. When the NH3 vapor was removed, all devices showed fast recovery. Figure 4c shows the response and recovery of the porous OFET over time, with the injection and vent of NH3 vapors at various concentrations. The VG was fixed at −10 V. Air was used as the background vapor. 0.5, 1, 3, 5 and 10 ppm NH3 vapors were injected and vented in sequence. The porous OFET exhibited response with high sensitivity and fast response/recovery speed.

Figure 5d plots the relative sensitivity (RS) of the two devices at different NH3 concentrations. Here the RS was defined as the percentage of current change per ppm analyte concentration. In this work, RS = (ΔI/ID,0 × 100)/C(NH3), where C(NH3) is the concentration of NH3 in the unit of ppm. The dashed red column shows the RS of the porous OFET, while the grey solid column represents the RS of the pristine OFET. As shown in the figure, the porous OFET shows much higher RS than that of the pristine OFET at all NH3 concentrations. Especially, the superiority of the porous OFET becomes more obvious at lower NH3 concentration. At ultralow NH3 concentration of 10 ppb, the porous OFET exhibited RS as high as 340% ppm−1. As a comparison, the pristine OFET only showed RS of 20% ppm−1, since the device only has negligible response, less than 0.15% current decrease, upon exposure to 10 ppb NH3. The extremely high sensitivity of the porous OFET sensor can be attributed to the porous organic semiconductor layer structure, which provides additional and direct pathways for NH3 molecules to interact with holes in the OFET conduction channel.

Device stability is another important factor for sensor devices.48, 49 The organic semiconductor DNTT used here is a relatively air-stable compound. We found that the porous OFET sensors have decent air stability. The porous structure did not make the device unstable in air. Figure 6a shows the transfer characteristics of a porous OFET tested as a fresh device, and the same device stored in air for 15 and 30 d. The transfer characteristic curves showed negligible changes over the period of one month. Figure 6b compares the sensing responses of the porous OFET at various VG upon exposure to 1 ppm NH3 vapor, tested as fresh device, and stored in air for 15 and 30 d. The device exhibited reproducible sensing performances. The comparative study on sensing properties of devices under different RH was also carried out in this work. In Figure 6c, we can see that there is only a little change in the transfer characteristics of the porous OFET tested under different RH percentages. Figure 6d shows the sensing responses of the porous OFET upon exposure to 1 ppm NH3 vapor, tested under different RH ranging from 31% to 68%. The changes in sensing responses were not significant.

We have also investigated the selectivity50, 51 of the porous OFET sensors. A porous OFET was tested upon exposure to different chemical vapors, ranging from 1 ppm NH3 vapor to 500 ppm of various vapors including hydrogen chloride (HCl), hydrogen peroxide (H2O2), hexane, dimethyl methylphosphonate (DMMP), dimethyl carbonate (DMC), chloroform (CHCl3), acetone, ethanol (EtOH), deionized water (H2O), and acetonitrile (CH3CN). As shown in Figure 7, the device exhibited decent selectivity. The error bars show the deviation of the sensing responses. When the device was exposed to HCl and H2O2, the semiconductor was doped and the device showed obvious current increase. When nonpolar vapor hexane was injected, the device exhibited negligible response. When vapors with higher polarity and stronger hole quenching effect were injected, the device exhibited more significant current decrease, due to charge trapping and quenching effects. The device exhibited much stronger response to NH3 vapors than all other vapors, even when the NH3 concentration used was 500 times lower than all other vapors. This result suggests that the porous OFET sensors can have decent selectivity over a wide range of vapors. The significantly enhanced sensitivity, decent stability and selectivity of the porous OFET demonstrate the great promise for applications in low cost and portable chemical sensors.

3 Conclusion
In conclusion, we have demonstrated a promising strategy to enhance the sensing performance of OFET-based chemical sensors with a versatile and low-cost template method. OFET-based chemical sensors with porous film structure and pristine organic semiconductor film structure were fabricated and their sensing performances were compared. OFET chemical sensors with porous structure exhibited faster response, much higher sensitivity and better recoverability than that of the pristine one, due to its increased analyte adsorption sites, and more efficient and direct pathway for analyte/charge interaction in the conduction-channel around the pores. Porous OFETs exhibited obvious and reproducible response to 10 ppb NH3, with an RS up to 340% ppm−1, which is more sensitive than all other OFET sensors previously reported, to the best of our knowledge. The devices also showed decent stability and sensing selectivity. This strategy is general and simple, with great potential to be applied to nearly all OFET chemical sensors.

4 Experimental Section
Materials: Polystyrene microspheres with diameter of 10 µm (2.5 wt%) were purchased from Afla Aesar. Organic semiconductor DNTT was synthesized according to literature reported previously52 followed by vacuum sublimation purification.

OFET Devices Fabrication: Heavily n-type doped Si wafers with 300 nm of thermally grown SiO2 layers were used as substrates. They were first cleaned by sonication in acetone and isopropanol alternatively for 1 h, and then rinsed with deionized water and ethanol, dried by nitrogen flow. The original polystyrene microspheres dispersion (2.5 wt%) was diluted to 1.5 wt% and dispersed by ultrasonication for 10 min. The substrates were treated by oxygen plasma (Harrick Plasma, PDC-32G) for 5 min and then coated with diluted polystyrene microspheres dispersion by drop casting method. After that, the samples were vacuum freeze dried (Ningbo Scientz Biotechnology) to allow the dispersion of polystyrene microspheres on substrates, which can be utilized as templates to fabricate porous OSC layers.53 10 nm DNTT semiconductor layers were thermally evaporated on these samples at a rate of 0.08 Å s−1 and a pressure of 1 × 10−4 Pa. Next, adhesive tapes were used to remove polystyrene microspheres on the samples. Finally, 80 nm Au electrodes were thermally evaporated on the templates through shadow masks with a channel length of 50 µm and a channel width of 14 mm. The porous OFETs and the pristine OFETs were prepared in the same batch, but the fabrication of porous OFETs had additional processes to add and remove PS spheres. Other than that, both devices were fabricated together, including the deposition of electrodes and organic semiconductors.

Device Characterization: The surface morphologies of the porous DNTT films were investigated by an optical microscope (Shanghai Optical Instrument Factory, 6XB-PC) and a field emission scanning electron microscope (FEI, Nova Nano SEM 450). All electrical characterizations of OFETs were measured by using a Keithley 4200 (Keithley Instrument) semiconductor characterization system connected to a homemade vapor testing chamber.
