Electrolyte-gated SrCoOx FET sensor for highly sensitive detecting pH in extreme alkalinity solution
Han Zhou, Gaocan Qi (✉), Wenbin Li, Yuhang Wang, and Zhihao Yuan (✉)
School of Materials Science and Engineering, Tianjin Key Lab of Photoelectric Materials & Devices, and Key Laboratory of Display Materials and Photoelectric Devices (Ministry of Education), Tianjin University of Technology, Tianjin 300384, China
© Tsinghua University Press
Received: 16 August 2023 / Revised: 30 August 2023 / Accepted: 30 August 2023
ABSTRACT
The pH monitoring is significantly important in chemical industry, biological process, and pollution treatment. However, it remains a great challenge to measure pH in extreme alkalinity conditions. Herein, we employ an electrolyte-gated field-effect-transistor (FET) strategy using non-stoichiometric SrCoOx with rich oxygen-vacancy defects as channel materials for detecting extreme alkalinity. The corresponding channel can provide effective oxygen-ion-migration sites for reversible transformation of OH− ↔ O2− + H+ driven by electric field. The resultant electrolyte-gated FET sensor exhibits a sensitive linear response to high concentrations of alkaline solution, 1–20 M. Significantly, the sensor has the ability to directly indicate the pH values ranging from 14.0 to 17.0 in consideration of ion-activity coefficient data. This work offers a great possibility for directly detecting base concentration as well as pH values in extreme alkaline solutions.
KEYWORDS
field-effect-transistor (FET), pH sensor, extreme alkaline solution, SrCoOx, oxygen-ion migration
1 Introduction
pH is a fundamental and useful concept in the field of chemistry due to its important role in chemical reactions, cellular activities, environmental monitoring, as well as many industrial processes including ferrous metallurgy, semiconductors, food, drugs, cement, paper making and textile, etc. [1–7]. The glass electrode is nowadays known as the gold standard for pH measurement, but it suffers from intrinsic fragility and requires frequent recalibration due to signal instability or drift. In the past decades, researchers have developed several alternative pH-sensing materials and devices to detect pH, such as optical, electrochemical, and transistor-based sensors [8–12]. However, most of the developed sensors are not suitable for extreme alkalinity (pH > 14).It is necessary to measure and monitor the pH value under the extreme alkaline conditions for many industrial processes, such as metal smelting, leather working, papermaking, nuclear fuel reprocessing, chemosynthesis, and waste treatment [13–19]. In the past few years, some scientific efforts have been made towards pH sensing to extreme alkalinity. Ma and co-workers synthesized H-imidazo[4,5-f][1,10] phenanthroline derivative as fluorescent probe to steadily detect extreme alkalinity [19], but only with NaOH concentrations of 3–6 M. Li et al. constructed a polybisphenol-based pH electrode [20], featuring a good potentiometric response in the pH range from 0 to 15. Significantly, Kang et al. designed a pH-dependent luminescent sensor based on an ultra-stable discrete cluster (Hf13(μ4-O)8(OCH3)36) used in both concentrated acid and alkali [21], which can recognize concentrated alkali (1–18 M NaOH) with good selectivity and regenerability. Although these advances have been made in detecting pH, the vulnerability of the pH-sensing materials or complex operation process limited their practical application for extreme alkalinity, which called for developing of new pH-sensing materials or devices.
In the past decades, the electrolyte-gated field effect transistor (FET) sensors for the detection of proteins/DNA, uric acid, and ions like H+/OH−, etc. have aroused intense research interests owing to their advantages of sensitive response, label-free detection, miniaturized sensor size, and compatibility with electronic instruments [22–31]. Among them, there is an increasing interest in the pH sensor based on the FET because of its wide potential of applications ranging from chemical industries to laboratories, and to the body fluid pH [27–31]. The pH sensor is a type of resistance device, and in principle, is similar to the conventional FET, but the channel is replaced with H+/OH− sensitive materials in direct contact with electrolyte solution [32–35]. The response to pH variation is benefited from the remarkable conductance variation of FET channel for the formation of electrical double layer near its surface upon adsorption of H+/OH− ions under a gate voltage. In an FET sensor, it should be currently highlighted that the construction of highly stable and sensitive channel material is the key to application as pH sensor. Some efforts have been made in exploring sensitive materials including metal oxides and sulfides, conducting polymers, graphene, etc. For example, Feng et al. fabricated MoS2-based FET sensor for sensitively detecting pH values with high resistance gradient within operating pH range of 8.0–12.0 [33]. Neubert and coworkers reported a graphene-based FET pH sensor with one-dimensional graphene edge as channel materials [35], and the pH sensor delivered a sensitive resistance-response in a pH range from 3.0 to 7.5 under a gate voltage of −0.07 V. The progress is pushing the FET sensors toward a more sensitive and convenient pH detection. However, the pH sensors were designed to only function well in weak acidity or alkalinity whose pH was generally between 2 and 12. While the strong alkalinity or acidity resulted in the failure of FET sensors to detect pH because the presented sensitive materials as channel suffer from corrosion and losing sensitive in strong alkali or acid condition.
It is known that the nonstoichiometry in SrCoOx series is associated with the presence of oxygen vacancies. Oxygen-vacancy nonstoichiometry can enrich material functionality tailoring in transparency, magnetism, ionic conductivity, and catalytic activity through the control of oxygen ion transfer. Recently, researches have demonstrated electric-field control of dual-ion (O2− and H+) phase transformations, with associated electrochromic and magnetoelectric effects [36]. Hence the nonstoichiometric SrCoOx could, in principle, be used as channel materials of electrolyte-gated FET for the detection of H+ or OH− concentrations. Here, we demonstrated a SrCoOx-based electrolyte-gated FET sensor for detecting base concentration and pH in extreme alkalinity conditions based on a reversible oxygen-ion transfer driven by electric field, schematically illustrated in Fig. 1. In the pH sensor, the SrCoOx nanoparticles with rich oxygen vacancies employ an oxygen-vacancy channel for reversible oxygen-ion migration driven by electric field. Different from the formation of electrical double layer near channel surface of traditional FET devices, injection and extraction of active ions occurred in our FET channel material, which will endow the FET sensor with an extended detection range and enhanced sensitivity. Our pH prototype sensor delivers a good linear relation between alkali concentration and log value of resistance under ultra-high concentration condition ranging from 1 to 20 M OH−. Significantly, the prototype sensor could directly indicate the pH values ranging from 14.0 to 17.0 as well. Our work has advanced the electrolyte-gated FET sensor towards directly pH detection in extreme alkaline solutions.
2 Material and methods
2.1 Synthesis of SrCoOx powder
All chemicals were analytical grade and purchased from Shanghai Macklin Biochemical Technology Co., Ltd without further refinement. The SrCoOx powder was prepared via a sol-gel approach followed by two-step calcination. Typically, Co(NO3)2 (0.01 mol), Sr(NO3)2 (0.01 mol), and citric acid (0.06 mol) were dissolved in 80 mL deionized water. 15 mL of ethylene glycol was added into the solution after all solute has been completely dissolved through vigorously stirring; the solution was then stirred for 24 h to form a magenta homogeneous colloid. The colloid was dried in a 60 °C oven to form a purple xerogel, which was subsequently calcined in air at 400 °C for 4 h to form a black precursor. The precursor was then ground and milled, and calcined for a second time in air at 900 °C for 10 h. After cooling, the resulting powder was then ball-milled for an additional 6 h to obtain the final SrCoOx powder. 2.2 Construction of FET pH sensor
The substrate circuit of the electrolyte-gated FET sensor was made made of fluorine-doped tin oxide (FTO) glass with conductive layer of ~ 800 nm by chemical etching. Typically, source and drain electrode regions on FTO were first protected using SU-8 photoresist via photolithography. Then, the exposed conductive layer of FTO between protected electrode regions was etching using zinc power and 1 M HCl solution. FET electrodes were finally obtained via washing off SU-8. A SrCoOx paste can be prepared via mixture of 0.8 g SrCoOx nanocrystals with 0.2 g polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF) in 4 mL N-methylpyrrolidone through 24 h of vigorous stirring. The paste was spin-coated onto the FET electrode and then incubated in a vacuum and aged at 240 °C in ambient air for 4 h to fabricate a pH-sensitive SrCoOx film. In addition, a piece of graphite plate was used as gate electrode. A custom-made container was fabricated with Teflon plastic to construct SrCoOx-based FET sensor, which consisted of a cubic chamber for placing test solution and two slots for fixing SrCoOx-based electrodes and gate electrode. The FET electrodes (source, drain, and gate) were connected into circuit through copper wires with silver-paste immobilization. The bias was applied by an electrochemical station (CHI660E, Chenhua, China) while the real-time resistance was measured by a Source Meter (2600B, Keithley, America). The measurement results have been repeatedly verified and validated to ensure their accuracy and reliability.
2.3 pH sample preparation
Unless otherwise noted, 1 M NaCl solutions were used as base electrolyte solutions, and pH was adjusted with NaOH solution. The pH values in the range from 7.0 to 14.0 were calibrated using a commercial pH sensor (FE28, METTLER TOLEDO). For solutions with pH values greater than 14, the test solutions were prepared and calibrated using appropriate amounts of NaOH solutions based on reported activity coefficients [37], and the relevant activity coefficients as well as calculated pH values are provided in Table S1 in the Electronic Supplementary Material (ESM). The high concentration NaOH solutions were prepared with boiled deionized water, and their concentrations were determined by chemical titration. 2.4 X-ray diffraction (XRD) and X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) characterization
XRD data were collected with a Rigaku Miniflex II diffractometer by using Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.54056 Å) in the 2θ range from 3° to 60°. The composition and chemical valence analyses were investigated by XPS (Thermo ESCALAB250Xi) with monochromatic Al Kα (hν = 1486.6 eV) radiation. All the binding energies in the XPS analysis were corrected for specimen charging by referencing them to the C 1s peak set at 284.8 eV. The tested samples were prepared by socking the samples into corresponding solutions for 24 h and dried through centrifuge and torrefied then measured straightforward.
2.5 Cyclic voltammetry of symmetrical battery
SrCoOx powder, acetylene black, and PVDF were mixed in an 8:1:1 weight ratio. The mixture was dissolved in N-methylpyrrolidone and stirred for 24 h. The mixed slurry was coated on carbon cloth and dried in a 60 °C oven. Then it was transferred to a 110 °C vacuum drying oven and dried for 12 h. It was cut into 12 mm circular pieces to obtain the SrCoOx electrodes. The SrCoOx electrodes were used as both the positive and negative electrodes in the symmetrical button cell, and 1 M NaOH solution was used as electrolyte between the electrodes. The cyclic voltammetry test was conducted with an electrochemical workstation (Thales, Zahner) in a range of −1.2 to +1.2 V and at a testing rate of 0.1 mV/s.
2.6 Density functional theory (DFT) calculation
The electronic structure calculations of SrCoOx (x = 2.50, 2.625, 2.750, 2.875, and 3.00) were performed within DFT employing the Vienna ab initio simulation package (VASP) code. The projector augmented wave (PAW) pseudopotentials were used. Strong correlations between Co d electrons were treated within the local spin density approximations of DFT with Hubbard U corrections (LSDA + U). The U value was set to be 7.5 eV, which has been optimized by Mitra et al. [38]. The valence electrons included for Sr, Co, and O are 4s24p65s2, 3s23p63d7, and 2s22p4, respectively, and a plane-wave cutoff energy of 600 eV was used. A Monkhorst–Pack special k-point grid of 4 × 6 × 6 was chosen to integrate over the Brillouin zone. The energies converged to within 10−6 eV/cell. All forces converged within 0.01 eV/Å. During geometry optimization of SrCoOx crystal structures, different magnetic configurations were considered referring to the spin directions [38, 39]. 3 Results and discussion
Nonstoichiometric SrCoOx was prepared using a sol-gel approach followed by a two-step calcination. The variation in O deficiency of the SrCoOx film in different alkaline solutions was conveniently studied using X-ray diffraction analysis. The as-prepared SrCoOx film mainly consisted of SrCoO2.5 crystals in brownmillerite phase (Fig. S1 in the ESM shows the SEM image of the SrCoOx film), and this phase structure was found to be stable when immersed in a neutral solution of 1 M NaCl solutions as shown by the black and blue patterns in Fig. 2(a). When the SrCoOx film was exposed to strong alkaline solutions, the characteristic diffraction peaks of the brownmillerite phase (101) and (201) at 28.06° and 44.02° were suppressed. Instead, a new peak at 2θ position of 40.56° emerged, which is consistent with the characteristic peak (111) of the perovskite phase. Additionally, the "fingerprint" (002) peak of brownmillerite SrCoO2.5 at 10.8° [40], marked with BM-(002), gradually disappeared with the increase of NaOH concentration from 1 to 10 M. Meanwhile, the "fingerprint" (002) peak of perovskite SrCoO3−δ at 48.1°, marked with P(002), was correspondingly enhanced, as shown in the detailed spectra in Fig. 2(a). This result indicates that the SrCoOx film underwent a gradual transformation from SrCoO2.5 to SrCoO3−δ under high concentrations of OH− ions. This transformation can also be confirmed by the shift of the brownmillerite SrCoO2.5 (008) diffraction peak, marked with BM(008), to higher angles following immersion in 1 M NaOH, and the disappearance of the BM(008) peak in 10 M NaOH. This is due to the fact that perovskite SrCoO3−δ has a smaller lattice parameter compared to brownmillerite SrCoO2.5. An electrolyte-gated FET device was developed for the detection of extreme alkalinity. The FET device configuration was based on SrCoOx and is shown in Fig. 1(b). Real-time resistance measurements were taken as the NaOH concentration increased from 1 to 10 M with 0 V applied bias voltage. The resistance variation of the SrCoOx channel over time is shown in Fig. 2(b) by the black curve. The resistance of the SrCoOx channel decreased from approximately 1000 to ~ 12 kΩ in about 5000 s, which implied a sensitive detection of SrCoOx films to strongly or extremely alkaline solutions. However, when the NaOH concentration was reduced back to 1 M, the resistance only partially recovered to approximately 700 MΩ after about 7000 s. Therefore, the response time and recoverability of the device require further improvement.
To improve the response speed and recoverability of the SrCoOx pH sensor, an appropriate gate voltage needs to be applied to induce electric field-driven O2− insertion and extraction. To understand this behavior, cyclic voltametric (CV) testing was performed on a SrCoOx electrode with 1 M NaOH as the electrolyte within a voltage range of −1.2 to +1.2 V with a sweep speed of 0.1 mV/s. As shown in Fig. 2(c), the CV curves of the SrCoOx electrode displayed repeatable electrochemical behavior during several cycles. The approximate symmetry of the CV curves suggested the reversible redox reaction of SrCoOx in alkaline solutions. Under a positive sweep voltage, two oxidation peaks could be observed at 0.32 and 0.90 V, corresponding to the oxidation processes of Co2+/Co3+ and Co3+/Co4+, respectively [41]. The redox reaction involves the transfer of electrons between different oxidation states of Co ions accompanied by O2− insertion. The experimental results also showed that water decomposition becomes significant when the sweep voltage exceeds ±1 V due to the resulting high electrochemical current. Taking these results into account, gate voltages of −1 and +1 V were applied to the SrCoOx channel to accelerate the redox of Co ions and O2− insertion/extraction process. This process helped to enhance the sensor's sensitivity and response time. The response and recovery times of the SrCoOx channel were measured during the variation of OH− concentration between 1 and 10 M under gate voltages, as shown by the red line in Fig. 2(b). The response time was about 900 s and the recovery time was less than 1000 s. In addition to the decrease of response time, a notable increase in resistance variation was also observed. Hence, in the following test −1 and +1 V were used in response and recover process, correspondingly, for alkalinity detection of the SrCoOx-based FET sensor. The SrCoOx-based FET sensor was firstly used to detect conventional alkaline solution with pH ranging from 7 to 14. Figure 2(d) illustrates a linear relationship between response resistance [35,42] (the average resistance obtained when the resistance response reached a steady state whose resistance fluctuation lower than 1%) and pH values in this range. This linear response suggests the potential application of the SrCoOx-based FET sensor in accurate pH indication. Their linear relationship could be given by Eq. (1) with an R-square value of 0.98:
R = (30.40 − 2.14 pH) × 106 (1)
Equation (1) also indicates the sensitivity of FET sensor is about 2.14 MΩ/pH in the pH range of 7–14. To verify the reliability of this pH sensor, it was used to measure two standard basic solutions, a 0.01 M Na2B4O7·10H2O solution with a pH of 9.18 and saturated Ca(OH)2 solution with a pH of 12.64. Red spheres in Fig. 2(d) show resulting resistances corresponding to the two standard solutions. It can be seen that their pH-resistance relationships are consistent well with the fitting curve, indicating a precise pH indication of this FET pH sensor.
The SrCoOx-based FET sensor was then used to detect extreme alkaline solutions. Figure 3(a) shows transient response to some typical concentrated NaOH aqueous solutions. It can be seen that our FET sensor manifests excellent recoverability in detecting different concentration of NaOH, even to 20 M NaOH which has the highest −OH concentration at room temperature [21]. Given that the vertical axis in Fig. 3(a) is exponential form, the response resistances decreased in the order of magnitude level with the increase of NaOH concentration, indicating a sensitive response behavior. The response resistances to a series of NaOH concentrations changed from 1 to 20 M with step size of 1 M were collected and plotted in Fig. 3(b). It can be seen a good linearity between response resistances (R) and NaOH concentrations (CNaOH) in exponential coordinates. Their linear relationship between lg(R) and lg(CNaOH) is present in Eq. (2), with an R-square value of 0.998
lg(R) = −2.22 · lg(CNaOH) + 6.05 (2)
Accordingly, the NaOH concentration with respect to response resistances can be given in Eq. (3)
CNaOH = 531.16 · R−0.45 (3)
The stability of the SrCoOx based FET sensor was evaluated with repetition and anti-interference tests in response to extreme alkalinity. Figure 3(c) shows the cycling response of the SrCoOx-based FET sensor to 1 and 10 M NaOH. It can be seen that the response resistances to 1 or 10 M NaOH were basically consistent with each other during repeated detection process for several times. The influence of some potentially interfering ions on the response to 1 M −OH is displayed in Fig. 3(d). The result shows that the presence of K+ (1 M), Na+ (1, 2, or 3 M), Cl− (1 M), NO3− (1 M), and SO42− (1 M) did not obviously affect the response resistance to 1 M −OH, which mainly stabilized at ~ 1030 kΩ. These results demonstrate that the SrCoOx based FET sensor possessed excellent response stability in the detection of extreme alkalinity. It is commonly understood that the concentration of hydrogen ions [H+] in highly concentrated alkaline solutions is determined by the activity of hydroxyl ions, a[OH−]. Therefore, pH values of concentrated NaOH solutions can be obtained according to the activity coefficients of NaOH solutions reported in Ref. [37]. After transformation, the relationship between response resistance and pH of concentrated NaOH is plotted in Fig. 4(a). lg(R) and pH show a monotonic decreasing relationship, indicating that in the pH range of 14.0–17.0, pH values correspond one-to-one with response resistance. A polynomial fitting of this data was conducted to determine the relationship between lg(R) and pH in Eq. (4).
lg(R) = 50.12 − 5.27 pH + 0.15pH2 (4)
The fitting of the function used for the SrCoOx-based FET pH sensor shows a high R-square value of 0.994, which suggests that this function is extremely well fitted to the relationship between R and pH. On the base of the fitting result, the pH response as a function of response resistance conforms to Eq. (5)
pH = √(lg(R) − 3.82 + 6.80)/0.39 (5)
This finding is of great significance, as it indicates that this sensor is capable of providing direct pH readings for measuring extreme alkalinity solutions with pH ranging from 14.0 to 17.0. In Fig. 4(b), the pH sensor's stability across various pH levels is depicted. It can be seen that the response resistances are relatively stable in detecting the same pH values at different times. Given that the pH values were correlated with logarithm of response resistances, a slight resistance fluctuation hardly affected the pH indication of our FET pH sensor. Additionally, the detection of various pH values did not impact the sensor's response behavior during subsequent measurements, indicating a high accuracy and a satisfied reliability of the pH sensor in handling different extreme alkalinity. These results highlight the potential of SrCoOx based FET sensor as a valuable and practical technology for pH sensing in extreme alkaline solutions.
To investigate the variation of Vo during the field effect process, the chemical state variation of SrCoOx film in extreme alkaline solutions was investigated using XPS. The O 1s spectra of SrCoOx film immersed in neutral NaCl solution show an asymmetry peak ranged from 528.0 to 534.0 eV with a shoulder peak centered at ~ 529.3 eV, see Fig. 5(a). With concentration increase of NaOH solutions from 1 to 10 M, the shoulder peak of O 1s gradually vanished and the broad main peak centered at 531.2 eV became more and more sharp. In addition, a new peak centered at 533.5 eV emerged and was gradually strengthened. In view of that the SrCoOx material is a non-stoichiometric compound with ability to accommodate wide range of O deficiency, the O species and Co valences in SrCoOx crystal are very complicated. The crystal of perovskite SrCoO3−δ can be described as a stacked Co(IV)-O6 octahedra with Sr(II) atom inserted in their interstice (see Fig. 5(b)). Its O 1s XPS spectra generally characterized as a broad peak ranged from 530.0 to 533.0 eV, which was considered to contain lattice O located at ~ 530.3 eV and vacancy O located at ~ 531.7 eV caused by little O deficiency [43]. With further reduction of O atoms, perovskite SrCoO3−δ changed into brownmillerite SrCoO2.5. Different with randomly distributed Vos in common oxides, Vos arranges regularly as 1D channels in SrCoO2.5 crystal, making the originally stacked Co(IV)-O6 octahedra structure change into alternatively arranged Co(IV)-O6 octahedra and Co(II)-O4 tetrahedra sub-layers along the c-axis (see Fig. 1(a)) [44, 45]. The O 1s XPS spectra of brownmillerite SrCoO2.5 always displayed a low-energy peak centered at ~ 529.3 eV near the broad peak (530.0–533.0 eV) [40, 46]. In addition, it has been reported that O 1s peak of CoO located at 529.5 eV [47]. Hence, the O 1s peak at 529.3 eV of SrCoO2.5 should be assigned to displace O in Co(II)-O4 tetrahedra around Co2+ near Vo channel. The broad peak ranged from 530.0–533.0 eV was similar with the O peak in perovskite SrCoO3, which can be assigned to the lattice O and defective O in Co(IV)-O6 octahedra. Based on these understanding, the O 1s spectra of SrCoOx materials were deconvoluted into three peaks centered at 529.3, 530.3, and 531.7 eV, which corresponded to displaced O in Co(II)-O4 tetrahedra, lattice O in Co(IV)-O6 octahedra, and vacancy O in Co(IV)-O6 octahedra. In addition, the peak centered at 533.5 eV was generally ascribed to adsorbed OH− on material surface. From the spectra variation of O 1s in different pH values, we can conclude that the as-prepared SrCoOx crystal was mainly brownmillerite SrCoO2.5 with plenty of Vos. During field effect process for detecting extreme alkaline solution, OH− aggregated on SrCoO2.5 surface, and partial O atom of OH− entered the O vacancy channel of SrCoO2.5 near the Co(II)-O4 tetrahedral layer. The Vo in SrCoOx gradually vanished with increacement of OH− concentration, resulting the transformation from brownmillerite SrCoO2.5 to perovskite SrCoO3. This phase transformation can also be confirmed by the p orbital XPS spectra of Co atoms. As is shown in Fig. S2 in the ESM, the Co2+ and Co3+ peaks gradually enhance along with the reduction of Co4+ peak [48].
To further study the mechanism of Vo-variation induced resistance change of SrCoOx, we performed DFT calculation to map out the electronic structure evolution employing the VASP code. We have included the strong correlation effects to deal with the Co d electrons by means of the LDA + U scheme [38]. Figure 5(b) shows total density of states (DOS) of SrCoOx, calculated using U value of 7.5 eV and going from SrCoO2.5 to SrCoO3−δ (their band structures were collected in Fig. S3 in the ESM). From Fig. 5(b), we can see that the brownmillerite SrCoO2.5 with plenty of Vo is a semiconductor with a wide band gap of ~ 1.9 eV, indicating a large resistance of their crystal materials (this calculated band gap was close to the experimental value obtained via optical absorption spectra [36]). When one O atom is inserted in the oxygen vacancy channel of SrCoO2.5, two bands of Co 3d orbital cross its Fermi level, see Fig. S3(b) in the ESM, which means an increase of conductivity. With further reduction of Vo as O insertion, more and more bands cross the Fermi level, and density of electronic states rapidly increases near the Fermi level. When a stoichiometric SrCoO3 is reached, the band structure displays a typical metallic state. It can be seen that the gradually reduction of Vo made the SrCoOx film change from a wide band gap semiconductor to a metallic material. This dramatic electronic structure variation is capable of explaining the remarkable resistance variation of SrCoOx channel in extreme-alkalinity detection.
Based on above experimental results and theoretical calculation, a Vo-concentration related sensing mechanism was proposed to interpret the response behavior of SrCoOx based FET pH sensor. In extreme alkaline solutions where the pH was greater than 14.0, the high concentration of OH− promoted the O2− insertion reaction in SrCoOx, Vo + OH− → O2− + H+. The presence of gate voltage further improved this process. With the decrease of Vo, the conducting behavior of SrCoOx changed from a semiconductive state to a metallic state, resulting in a remarkable change in resistance by several orders of magnitude. Therefore, the SrCoOx-based FET pH sensor exhibited an exponential decrease of resistance to increase in −OH concentration during extreme alkalinity detection. In normal alkaline solution where the pH was between 7 and 14.0, little O2− was inserted into SrCoOx for the low OH− concentration. Vo concentration in SrCoOx had a minor change, and the conducting behavior of SrCoOx remained in a semiconductive state. The resistance variation is mainly caused by the electrical double layer formed near the channel surface upon adsorption of OH− ion. Hence, the SrCoOx-based FET pH sensor showed a linear relationship between resistance and pH values in normal alkalinity detection.
4 Conclusions
In summary, an electrolyte-gated FET pH sensor based on Vo-rich SrCoOx has been designed and fabricated for detecting extreme alkaline solutions. This FET pH sensor displayed a sensitive response to high-concentration alkaline solution, and was capable of directly indicating pH value ranging from 14.0 to 17.0. Our FET pH sensor also exhibited good repeatability and strong anti-interference capacity. A Vo-concentration related sensing mechanism was proposed to explain the sensitive response of the SrCoOx-based FET pH sensor. Unlike the traditional electrical double layer mode, the O2−-insertion into Vo of SrCoOx induced by high-concentration OH− makes this FET pH sensor suited for detecting extreme alkalinity. The reduction of Vo gave rise to the semiconductor-metallicity transformation of SrCoOx, which results in the unprecedented sensing property to extreme alkalinity. This SrCoOx based FET pH sensor has great potential for industrial application and environmental monitoring. Furthermore, the defect induced electronic-state transformation paves a new way for exploiting ion sensing materials with desired sensitivity, selectivity, and stability.
