A universal approach to a structured polymer substrate for manufacturing
a printed polymer gas sensor based on a fi eld eff ect transistor
V. P. Chekusova,a A. A. Trul,a E. V. Agina,a and S. A. Ponomarenkoa,b
aN. S. Enikolopov Institute of Synthetic Polymeric Materials, Russian Academy of Sciences,
70 ul. Profsoyuznaya, 117393 Moscow, Russian Federation
bChemistry Department, Moscow State University,
1/3 Leninskie Gory, 119991 Moscow, Russian Federation.
E-mail: ponomarenko@ispm.ru
Printed polymer fi eld-eff ect transistors are promising devices for manufacturing
highly sensitive gas and liquid sensors based on them. However, their practical application
as sensors imposes special requirements on structured substrates for the deposition of
active semiconductor and receptor layers. A simple approach to the production of a structured polymer substrate using exclusively additive methods for manufacturing from it
a printed polymer fi eld-eff ect transistor suitable for the use as a gas/liquid sensor is
presented.
Key words: inkjet printing, screen printing, silver inks, polymer dielectric, organic
fi eld-eff ect transistor.
Interest of researchers in gas sensors produced by
printing technologies has increased considerably in the
recent years, since these technologies make it possible
to manufacture high-quality thin, light, fl exible, cheap,
and environmentally friendly electronic devices.1,2
Printing technologies are much simpler in accomplishment than other modern technologies for producing
silicon and organic electronics that consist of many
stages including repeated steps of deposition, lithography, and etching.3 In addition, they allow one to
fl exibly tune the architecture of the devices and their
nanosized functions and provide low-temperature
vacuum-free integration of numerous materials and
functions on one substrate.4,5
Many printing methods with diverse possibilities of
printing resolution and velocity have been developed
at present. The choice of the printing method is primarily based on the size of the printed unit, equipment
capacity, and type of the used substrates along with the
target application of the printing material.6 Among
them inkjet printing is the most frequently used method
for studying and developing functional devices based
on polymeric materials due to simplicity of manufacturing a prototype and requirements of a small amount
of ink (1—2 mL) with a low concentration of a functional material (<0.1 wt.%). When inkjet printing is
used, droplets are ejected from the ink reservoir onto
the substrate to directly apply the desired image.7
Source and drain electrodes for sensors based on
fi eld-eff ect transistors (FET) are usually prepared using
standard photolithography combined with physical or
chemical deposition in vacuo.8 Chemiresistive sensors
are less demanding to device complexity and function
resolution and, hence, counter electrodes can directly
be printed on these sensors in addition to usual processes
of nano/microtreatment, for example ink based on
silver or gold can be used.9 Gas-sensitive materials can
also be applied using an inkjet printer between source
and drain electrodes of a FET-based sensor.10 Depending on the functional of materials and structure of the
sensor, the rheology of ink composition and dynamics
of its drying need thorough optimization for specifi c
printing types and processes.11
As for sensor structures and principles of their operation, the most part of printed gas sensors are
chemiresistive; i.e., they change resistance in the presence of an analyzed gas,12,13 or fi eld-eff ect transistor
devices reacting to the analyzed gas by changing conductivity of the active channel.14—17 Their sensitivity,
selectivity, and detection limit are tightly related to the
internal properties of nanomaterials (e.g., electrical
conductivity, doping levels. crystal defects, and surface
chemistry/morphology).18 The physicochemical properties, structure, and morphology of deposited nanomaterials, including their thickness, porosity, and
possible interaction with substrates and electrodes also

play an important role in the operation of the device.19,20
Conductive polymers or oligomers are especially
attractive as functional materials for detecting volatile
organic compounds,21 and their fl exibility and compatibility with various printing processes are important
factors of choice.22 A homogeneous solution of a polymer can be printed as continuous smooth structures
that further solidify as the solvent evaporates, which
makes it possible to exactly control the topology of the
printed fi lm surface. Polymers are often used as matrix
materials for manufacturing composites with functional
fi llers, such as metal oxides, graphene, and carbon
nanotubes, to additionally enhance sensitivity and selectivity and/or to shorten response time of the sensor.23
Possibilities of molecular design of the main and side
chains and modifi cation of the terminal groups allow
one to widely vary their electrical properties and
chemical composition of the surface tuning to diff erent
types of analytes,24,25 thus providing rational design
and choice of polymers for the development of sensor
matrices.26 To the moment, the majority of published
works about printed sensors are based on the materials
related to graphene,27,28 whereas other 2D materials,
for instance, monolayer fi lms of organic semiconductors
remain unstudied, although they are much less expensive and highly biosafe, which makes it possible to use
them as biological liquid sensors that directly contact
with the living organism.29
It is shown in our earlier works that organic fi eldeff ect transistors (OFED) with the active layer based
on the benzothienobenzothiophene derivatives on
silicon substrates are highly stable and reproducible and
demonstrate an ultrahigh sensitivity (tens of ppb) to
various volatile polar gases, such as ammonia, hydrogen
sulfi de, and nitrogen oxide in the presence of water
vapors, which makes their use promising in real gas
analytical systems.30—33 It should be mentioned that
the transition from the gas sensor based on monolayer
OFET on the silicon substrate with sputtered gold
contacts to the completely printed sensors on the polymer substrate is fairly diffi cult. The consecutive printing of functional layers (electrodes, dielectric, and
semiconductor) from organic solvents requires the
stabilization of each layer and optimization of the printing processes. The use of printed transistors as gas
sensors in an atmosphere with a high moisture content
implies the resistance of all its layers to water vapors.
The requirements imposed on the response time and
recovery time of real sensors lead to restraints related
to preventing the chemical interaction of the detected
gases with any sensor layer.
The purpose of this work is to develop a universal
approach to the preparation of a structured polymer
substrate by exclusively printing methods without using
photolithography and sputtering in order to manufacture from it a printed polymer fi eld-eff ect transistor
(PPFET) suitable for the use as a gas/liquid sensor.
Experimental
Polystyrene (PS) (Sigma—Aldrich) with a molecular
weight of 280 000 was chosen as the polymeric dielectric.
Inkjet printing was performed with a PixDro LP50 jet
printer using a Spectra SM-128 AA piezoelectric printing head
in the drop-on-demand (IPDOD) mode.
Commercial silver ink representing nanosized silver particles in a solvent were used as the ink: the CRSN2442 silver
paste (Sun Chemical) for screen printing (SP) and the Silverjet
DGP-40LT-15C inkjet silver ink (Sigma—Aldrich) for inkjet
printing (IP).
The voltampere characteristics (VAC) (dependences of
the current on the voltage) of the printed structures were
measured using the PS-100 probe station (Printeltech LLC,
Russia) to estimate conductivity.
The capacity of dielectric layers was estimated on an E7-20
digital emittance meter (MNIPI, Russia) with a sinusoidal
signal with an amplitude of 1 V at the frequency from 25 Hz
to 1 MHz. An additional bias voltage up to 10 V was supplied
to the sample in order to study the dependence of the capacity on the frequency. The leakage current in the dielectric
layers was measured using the probe station.
Results and Discussion
The architecture with the consequently deposited
functional layers by additive methods was chosen for
manufacturing devices based on PPFET: self-organized
interface layer, bottom gate, dielectric, and conducting
contacts (Fig. 1). This architecture was chosen due to
the need of direct contact of the analyzed medium (gas
or liquid) with the active semiconductor layer when
using PPFET as the sensor unit.
Screen printing, doctor blade (DB) method, and
inkjet 2D printing were used as additive methods for
the deposition of functional layers. A number of devices
with a specifi ed architecture was produced using these
layers. Indium tin oxide (ITO) conductive glass, nonconductive glass, and poly(ethylene naphthalate) (PEN)
150 m thick were chosen as substrates. The conditions
were initially selected on the glass with ITO to avoid a
necessity to print the gate electrode for the subsequent
measurement of electrical characteristics of the dielectric layer.
The dielectric polymeric layer was deposited on the
substrates by the DB method. The deposited substance

was distributed over the blade edge in such a way that
a meniscus would be formed between the blade and
substrate. When the blade moves, the deposited substance is distributed over the substrate to form a uniform
fi lm. During motion the blade is arranged at a fi xed
distance from the substrate. To prepare the layer, optimum conditions of dielectric deposition were selected
by subsequent changing the blade movement velocity,
the height of the gap between the blade and substrate,
and the heating temperature of the stage. The ink composition (polymer type, solvent, and polymer concentration in solution) was also optimized taking into account prospects of its further use in inkjet printing.
The chosen PS is a dielectric frequently applied in
organic electronics and potentially well suitable for
using in the composition of gas sensors due to hydrophobicity, which should provide sensor resistance to
water vapors. Among the other dielectrics used, PS has
the lowest polarity, which should decrease the probability of interacting with polar gas-analytes and to provide
fast response and recovery. In addition, PS is appropriate for use in solution processing compatible with the
roll-to-roll approach, unlike many self-organized dielectrics. Toluene is the closest solvent to PS by
chemical structure, but it is relatively volatile even at
room temperature and, hence, its use in the pure form
for IP can result in fast and irreversible nozzle clogging
of the printing head of the printer. High-boiling 1,2-dimethylbenzene (o-xylene) in an amount of 5 and 10%
was added to the solvent in order to decrease the ink
volatility.

formation of a single round-shape droplet per each
supplied impulse, the physical properties of the ink for
IP, such as viscosity and surface tension, should lie in
a certain range depending, to a high extent, on the
printing head used. The properties of ink and its behavior during IP can theoretically be determined using the

The Reynolds number is the ratio of inertia forces
to viscosity forces and is expressed by Eq. (1). The
Weber number (We) makes it possible to compare the
inertia forces of the liquid with surface forces and is
applied for an analysis of bubble or droplet formation34:
the We value should be higher than 4, otherwise no
droplet will be formed. The Ohnesorge number (Oh) is
highly signifi cant in liquid mechanics and relates viscous, inertia, and surface forces as indicated in Eq. (3).
In inkjet technology, the Oh number refl ects the dimensional scale of the jet or droplet and physical
properties of the liquid. Unlike the Re and We numbers,
the velocity and, hence, the dependence on the motion
conditions are excluded from the mathematical description.35 The Oh numbers suitable for stable IPDOD
printing are expected to range from 0.1 to 1. Too high
viscosity will impede droplet separation for high Oh
values, while too low Oh values can result in the appearance of many satellites.35,36 Some authors prefer
to use the so-called Z number equal to the inverse
Ohnesorge number (1/Oh) for the description of an
admissible range of stable printing possibility.36,37
In our experiments, the measured surface tension
of toluene at 20 C was 28.5 mN m–1, the addition of
5 and 10 wt.% o-xylene was found to make no signifi cant
contribution to a change in the surface tension of the
solvent, and the measured value suits the parameters
of the printing head used (Spectra SM-128 AA). To
achieve the necessary value of kinetic viscosity of the
ink caused by requirements imposed on the Oh number,
the concentration of the polymer solution was changed
and the viscosity was measured by capillary viscosimetry. As a result, the PS concentration optimum for IP
was determined to be 60 mg mL–1.

the stage (30, 60, and 85 C). The quality of the dielectric layers obtained was evaluated from the surface
morphology using polarization optical and atomic force
microscopies and interferometry. An analysis of changes

The optimized printing technology of the dielectric
layer made it possible to transit from conductive glass
substrates with ITO to non-conductive glass and poly-
(ethylene naphthalate). The use of these materials as
substrates with the specifi ed architecture of the device
(see Fig. 1) demands an additional deposition of the
bottom silver electrode (gate electrode). To provide
a suffi cient adhesion of silver ink to the glass substrate,
the substrate surface was modifi ed to form on it an
interface self-organized layer based on 3-aminopropyltriethoxysilane (APTS) from the gas phase38 of oligomer
of 3-aminopropyltriethoxysilane (o-APTS) by the
spincoating method (Fig. 3).39,40
It was found that the interface layers both deposited
from the gas phase and obtained by the spincoating
method provide a suffi cient adhesion of silver ink to the
glass substrate. However, when the subsequent printing
of the dielectric layer leads to the situation where toluene (present in the solution) partially dissolves the
o-APTS-based layer, which impedes its use as the
modifying basis (see Fig. 3, а, b). At the same time, the
APTS-based interface layer has a suffi cient degree of
crosslinking to retain its morphology upon printing and,
therefore, this layer was further used (see Fig. 3, c). It
should be mentioned that the printed gate electrode
also required thermal annealing in a vacuum oven at
150 C to provide good sintering of silver. Annealing
at lower temperatures or without vacuum does not
provide a suffi cient stability of the conductive layer,
which damages the contacts upon the subsequent deposition of the dielectric layer: the dielectric is mixed
with silver particles resulting in the shorted electrodes
upon voltage supply.
The completing layers in the developed architecture
of the device are conductive contacts (source and drain
electrodes), which were printed using a similar procedure of inkjet or screen printing (Fig. 4).
The conducting properties of the printed silver layers
were measured prior to measurements of the dielectric
properties of the PS layers in order to reliably determine
that the used contacts are ohmic. The voltampere
characteristics (VAC) of the printed structures were
measured to estimate the conductivity. All printed
structures demonstrated the classical resistive VAC
(Fig. 5, а), and their total (R) and surface (RS) (or sheet
resistance, viz., specifi c value usually used for comparison of the conductivities of thin fi lms) resistances
are given in Table 1.
The data in Table 1 show that all printed structures
demonstrate rather low resistances (R), but an interesting experimental fact was observed: the surface resistances (RS) of the structures printed by SP on PEN are
7 times lower than those on glass, whereas the IP structures on PEN are 3 times lower than those on glass.

Several types of samples were studied in order to
estimate the capacity of dielectric layers: the samples
prepared on the glass with ITO with the top electrode
based on IP and SP and those prepared on glass or PEN
with the top and bottom electrodes based on IP and SP.
Note that the SP-based electrodes resulted in shorting
upon deposition on the PS layer, most likely, due to
the presence of the solvent and partial dissolution of
the PS layer. Therefore, the further data will be given
only for capacitors with IP electrodes. The typical
dependences of the specifi c capacity on the frequency
and voltage and of the leakage current density on the
voltage for the studied samples are shown in Fig. 6.
The primary characteristic for comparison is the
specifi c capacity at low frequencies, since this parameter would determine the density of charge carriers in
the transistor channel. The operating range is defi ned
as the maximum voltage at which the capacity does not
change yet upon voltage supply and as the leakage current density. An appropriate leakage current is a value
of 10–8 A, which gives a critical leakage current density
of 10–6 A cm–2 at the typical surface area of the transistor contacts equal to 0.01 cm2. Thus, the dependences
obtained clearly show that the highest specifi c capacity
equal to 27 nF cm–2 is achieved on the glass with ITO
and coincides with the published data.41 However, the
operating voltage range of this layer is only 0.5 V. The
lowest capacity is demonstrated by the sample on the
glass with the IP electrodes. The sample on PEN shows
a capacity higher by an order of magnitude than that
of the sample on glass and lower by an order of magnitude than that on the glass with ITO. At the same time,
its operating range is the largest of all the studied layers
being about 5 V. On the whole, the measured electrical
characteristics make it possible to use the printed substrates as a promising basis for the subsequent deposition
of the semiconductor active layer and testing them as
gas sensors.

It should be mentioned that the deposition of the
subsequent functional layers from solutions containing
toluene can result in the deformation of the printed
dielectric layer. Therefore, if the application of these
solutions is necessary, one can use an alternative procedure developed by us previously and described in
detail.42 As for manufacturing the printed device prepared in this work, its confi guration makes it possible
to deposit water-soluble semiconductor compounds,
for example, PEDOT  :  PSS, above the dielectric to
produce PPFET. In addition, these substrates can be
used for the deposition of thin (including monolayer)
semiconductor fi lms from the water surface by the
Langmuir—Blodgett and Langmuir—Schaefer methods
being laboratory analogs of roll-to-roll printing.
To conclude, the universal approach making possible to produce structured substrates on both glass and
fl exible polymeric substrates, which were prepared for
the further deposition of semiconductor and/or receptor layers and using as sensitive units of gas sensors,
using exclusively additive technologies.
