Abstract
This study introduces a straightforward method for depositing InZnSnO films onto flexible polyimide substrates at room temperature, enabling their application in electrochemical pH sensing and the detection of epinephrine. A comprehensive analysis of these sensing films, spanning structural, morphological, compositional, and profiling characteristics, was conducted using diverse techniques, including X-ray diffraction, atomic force microscopy, X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy, and secondary ion mass spectroscopy. The investigation into the influence of oxygen flow rates on the performance of InZnSnO sensitive films revealed a significant correlation between their structural properties and sensing capabilities. Notably, exposure to an oxygen flow rate of 30/2 (Ar/O2) the ratio of resulted in the InZnSnO sensitive film demonstrating outstanding pH sensitivity at 59.58 mV/pH within a broad pH range of 2–12, surpassing the performance observed with other oxygen flow rates. Moreover, under this specific condition, the film exhibited excellent stability, with a minimal drift rate of 0.14 mV/h at pH 7 and a low hysteresis voltage of 1.8 mV during a pH cycle of 7 → 4→7 → 10→7. Given the critical role of epinephrine in mammalian central nervous and hormone systems, monitoring its levels is essential for assessing human health. To facilitate the detection of epinephrine, we utilized the carboxyl group of 4-formylphenylboronic acid to enable a reaction with the amino group of the 3-aminopropyltriethoxysilane-coated InZnSnO film. Through optimization, the resulting InZnSnO-based flexible sensor displayed a broad and well-defined linear relationship within the concentration range of 10−7 to 0.1 μM. In practical applications, this sensor proved effective in analyzing epinephrine in human serum, showcasing notable selectivity, stability, and reproducibility. The promising outcomes of this study underscore the potential for future applications, leveraging the advantages of electrochemical sensors, including affordability, rapid response, and user-friendly operation.

1. Introduction
Epinephrine, a neurotransmitter prevalent in mammals, particularly within the central nervous and hormonal systems [1,2], is synthesized by the adrenal glands, serving as a hormone with significant impacts on various physiological processes, such as glycogen metabolism and the sympathetic nervous system. The concentration of epinephrine in the bloodstream plays a crucial role in regulating smooth muscle contractions, heart rate, blood pressure, glycogen breakdown in the muscles and liver, and fat breakdown in adipose tissue [3,4]. Accurate measurement of epinephrine levels in living organisms is pivotal for both diagnosing conditions and exploring new drug therapies for various diseases.
Over the long term, elevated levels of catecholamines in extracellular and circulating forms can lead to harmful conditions, resulting in cardiotoxicity and neurotoxicity [5] due to the formation of various oxidative byproducts. Disturbances in epinephrine levels in bodily fluids have been associated with serious health issues, including Parkinson's disease [6], schizophrenia [7], and Alzheimer's disease [8]. Furthermore, epinephrine exhibits the remarkable capability to influence mood and mental clarity, contributing to conditions like negative thinking, weight gain, fatigue, anxiety, digestive problems, insomnia, and hypomania, among others [5]. When employing epinephrine as a therapeutic agent, meticulous oversight of its concentration, both in the manufacturing phase and during treatment, is paramount to avert the detrimental consequences linked to excessive dosage. Consequently, the management of epinephrine levels becomes crucial, and sensors facilitating rapid and effective monitoring are highly preferred. Various techniques have been explored for epinephrine detection, including high-performance liquid chromatography [9], spectrophotometry [10], capillary electrophoresis [11], colorimetric assays [12], and fluorescence-based approaches [13]. However, these methods often come with notable drawbacks, such as high costs, prolonged analysis times, and the requirement for specialized expertise, posing certain challenges. In response to these limitations, electrochemistry has gained considerable attention, thanks to its outstanding attributes, including high selectivity, rapid response times, user-friendly operation, environmental friendliness, and cost-effectiveness. Consequently, electrochemical methods have been increasingly employed for the detection of epinephrine [5].
In the domain of electrochemistry, the ion-sensitive field-effect transistor (ISFET) has commanded attention since its inception in 1970 [14]. Offering numerous advantages for monitoring electrochemical reactions, ISFET is based on the well-established complementary metal-oxide-semiconductor (CMOS) technique, enabling large-scale production and seamless integration into diverse sensing systems. Unlike the three-electrode setup, ISFET requires only a single reference electrode for conducting biochemical measurements, presenting a more streamlined and efficient solution. Moreover, as an all-solid-state sensor, ISFET eliminates the use of delicate materials, making it resistant to acid, alkali, water, and physical wear and tear in practical applications [15]. The ISFET mechanism revolves around the influence of hydrogen ions on the potential of the oxide surface, resulting in a corresponding change in the channel current. As the charge density on the surface of sensing layer increases, the electron concentration in the channel follows suit. In 1983, J. Spiegel introduced an extended-gate field-effect transistor (EGFET) structure [16]. The EGFET design preserves the metal gate of the metal-oxide-semiconductor field-effect transistor (MOSFET) while isolating the sensing region from the MOSFET gate. The extended gate includes a substrate and a sensing window, serving as the gate of a MOSFET device within the analyte solutions. Furthermore, EGFET presents several advantages, including a dedicated chemical sensing area, distinct electrical properties, and easier packaging compared to ISFET. EGFET comes with inherent benefits such as a simpler manufacturing process, reduced susceptibility to optical illumination and operating temperature variations, a one-time gate configuration, immunity to chemical contamination, and excellent noise resistance. These advantages position EGFET as a promising device structure in the field of biotechnology, with the sensitive film material playing a crucial role where both its structure and the active sites on its surface significantly influence the overall performance of the sensor.
To enhance the efficacy of the sensing film, various metal oxide materials, including SnO2, TiO2, V2O5, ZnO, InSnO, InGaZnO, AlGaZnO, PdO, Al-doped ZnO, Nb2O5, Ta2O5, Bi2O3, Ga2O3 [[17], [18], [19], [20], [21]], have been utilized as sensing films for pH sensors and biosensors. Notably, InSnO sensing films exhibit remarkable sensitivity to pH levels. Additionally, InZnSnO films, recognized as transparent conductive oxides, play a crucial role in optoelectronic devices due to their exceptional ability to transmit visible light and efficient electrical conductivity. This makes them invaluable components for diverse applications, including solar cells, phototransistors, photodetectors, thin-film transistors (TFTs), and photodiodes [[22], [23], [24], [25]]. In InZnSnO-based TFTs, the presence of oxygen vacancies within the film functions as donor sites, producing free electrons. Moreover, modulation of the oxygen content allows for control over the energy bandgap of the InZnSnO film. The quantity of oxygen vacancies and the overall oxygen content play pivotal roles in determining the electrical characteristics and bias stability of TFTs [25,26]. However, the composition of InZnSnO films through the sputtering method has not been thoroughly explored for pH sensing.
In this study, we employed the RF cosputtering technique to deposit an InZnSnO film on a flexible polyimide substrate at room temperature. This film served as a sensitive membrane for the development of an EGFET solid-state sensor. We investigated the impact of the oxygen flow rate on both the sensing performance and structural features of the InZnSnO films. Additionally, a comprehensive analysis of the surface morphologies, chemical compositions, element profiles, and film structures of the InZnSnO films under different oxygen flow rates was conducted. Techniques such as atomic force microscopy (AFM), X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), secondary ion mass spectroscopy (SIMS), and X-ray diffraction (XRD) were employed for detailed film characterization. Furthermore, the sensitivity of the InZnSnO-based EGFET sensors was assessed while measuring various pH buffer solutions. The reliability of these EGFET sensors, considering parameters like drift rate and hysteresis voltage, was also examined. The resulting InZnSnO-based flexible sensor exhibited high sensitivity and selectivity, demonstrated long-term stability, and showed good repeatability in detecting epinephrine. Finally, the fabricated InZnSnO-based flexible sensor not only effectively analyzed epinephrine in human serum but also achieved real-time detection of epinephrine.
2. Experimental Section
In this investigation, a 5 × 5 cm2 flexible polyimide substrate was employed for depositing the InZnSnO film, which had a thickness of approximately 100 nm. The thin InZnSnO film was grown on the flexible polyimide using an RF cosputtering system with InZnO and ZnSnO targets under various oxygen flow rates (Ar/O2 ratios of 30/0, 30/1, 30/2, and 30/3) at room temperature. The cathode powers for InZnO and ZnSnO were set at 50 W and 20 W, respectively, while the sputtering pressure for the deposited InZnSnO film was maintained at ∼10−3 Torr. Following this, the delineation of sensitive areas with a 1.5 mm radius was carried out using a robotic dispensing system with an adhesive silicone gel (S181). For the assembly of the EGFET sensor, the backside of the InZnSnO film on the flexible polyimide was adhered to the Cu lines on a printed circuit board using silver gel. To prevent electrolyte leakage, epoxy resin was then used to encapsulate both the Cu lines and the InZnSnO film. A Rigaku TTRAX III Diffractometer, utilizing Cu Kα radiation, was employed to conduct the crystallographic analysis on the InZnSnO films at four different oxygen flow rates. A Thermo VG Scientific Microlab 350 system, equipped with a monochromatic Al Kα source and operated at 300 W, was used to analyze the chemical compositions of these films. The acceptance area measured 5 × 5 mm2. XPS spectra were obtained using a passing energy of 20 eV, incrementing by 0.1 eV at each sweep step. The X-ray beam had a photoelectron take-off angle of 45°. To compensate charge-up effects, a dual-beam charge neutralizer was employed. All XPS spectra were calibrated by aligning the C 1s peak at 285 eV. Background lines were determined using a Shirley-type shape, and peaks were fitted using a Lorentzian-Gaussian function. To investigate depth profiles, a ToF-SIMS IV/Ion-Tof system with a Cs+ erosion beam at an energy of 500 eV was employed for the InZnSnO films. An NT-MDT Solver P47 AFM, operating in tapping mode, was utilized to investigate the surface morphological characteristics of the InZnSnO films in 3 × 3 μm2 regions. The resonance frequency and spring constant of the cantilever were 280∼320 kHz and 20–40 N/m, respectively.
To evaluate the sensing performance of the InZnSnO sensitive films prepared under varying oxygen flow rates, we dropped 20 μL of standard pH buffer solutions (Merck) onto the InZnSnO sensitive films. These films, in conjunction with an Ag/AgCl reference electrode, were employed to record the drain current–reference voltage (IDS–VREF) transfer curves using a Keysight B1500A Semiconductor Device Analyzer, as depicted in Scheme 1. Buffer solutions with pH values ranging from 2 to 12 were used to assess the sensitivity of the InZnSnO-sensitive films in conjunction with the gate electrode of a commercial N-MOSFET (CD4007UBE) device. In the triode region we evaluated pH sensitivity and stability, considering factors like hysteresis and drift, through an analysis of the transfer curves. The correlation between IDS–VREF and pH in each buffer solution allowed us to calculate the sensitivity based on the slope of this relationship. Furthermore, a commercial pH meter was used to validate the pH values of each buffer solution.
To modify the surface of the InZnSnO sensing film for epinephrine detection, a series of steps were executed. Initially, a 2 % solution of 3-aminopropyltriethoxysilane (APTES) was applied to the InZnSnO sensing surface for 12 h to introduce amino groups to the surface. Subsequently, In a 5 % CO2 incubator, a solution was created by combining 5 μL of 1 mM 4-formylphenylboronic acid (4-FPBA) with 0.01 × PBS buffer and incubating it for 30 min. As shown in Scheme 1, a chemical reaction was employed to immobilize the carboxyl groups of 4-FPBA onto the amino groups of the APTES-coated InZnSnO-based flexible sensor device. This functionalized InZnSnO-based flexible EGFET sensor allowed for the detection of epinephrine and subsequent electrical measurements. To prevent nonspecific adsorption or binding of proteins on the sensor surface, the samples were treated with 5 μL of 2 % bovine serum albumin (BSA) and incubated at room temperature for 1 h. Following BSA adsorption, the samples were rinsed three times with PBS. Epinephrine solutions were prepared in a concentration range from 10−7 to 0.1 μM, using epinephrine dissolved in a 0.01 × PBS buffer solution and human serum.
3. Results and discussion
3.1. Structural properties of InZnSnO sensitive films
To investigate the crystallographic structure of InZnSnO films prepared under varying oxygen flow rates (Ar/O2 ratios of 30/0, 30/1, 30/2, and 30/3), XRD measurements were conducted in 2θ scan mode. Fig. S1 presents the XRD pattern for InZnSnO films processed under different oxygen flow rates. The pattern displayed a single weak peak at 2θ ≈ 22° and a strong peak at 2θ ≈ 26°, corresponding to the amorphous polyimide substrate [27]. Notably, the InZnSnO films processed at these oxygen flow rates did not exhibit clear peaks associated with In2O3, ZnO, or SnO2. The absence of well-defined peaks suggests that the InZnSnO film is likely amorphous, a characteristic attributed to the utilization of a room temperature fabrication process.
To delve deeper into the surface topography of the InZnSnO films, fabricated under various oxygen flow rates, we conducted an AFM examination, as illustrated in Fig. 1. The AFM image clearly reveals a consistent and even distribution of the InZnSnO film on the flexible polyimide substrate. The surface morphology and roughness of the InZnSnO film exhibited slight alterations with an increase in oxygen flow rate. A comparison between Fig. 1(a) and (b) readily demonstrates that augmenting the oxygen flow rate in the InZnSnO films results in only minor changes in surface morphology and roughness (Rrms = 1.35 and 1.42 nm, respectively). The surface profiles of the examined samples depicted in Fig. 1(c) and (d) indicate that the size of the crystalline grains increases from 1.51 to 1.63 nm with an increase in oxygen flow rate. This change in surface roughness, from 1.51 nm to 1.63 nm, becomes evident when transitioning the O2 flow rate from 30/2 to 30/3, corresponding to the increased oxygen content in the film.

To further explore the impact of varying oxygen flow rates on the sputtering process, we conducted XPS tests to analyze the chemical bonding states of InZnSnO films deposited under different oxygen flow rate conditions. In Fig. 3(a)-(b), distinct In 3d3/2 and 3d5/2 peaks, as well as Zn 2p1/2 and 2p3/2 peaks, were observed at approximately 452.2 and 444.7 eV, and around 1044.4 and 1021.3 eV, respectively, corresponding to In–O and Zn–O bonds [28,29]. Additionally, Fig. 3(c) displays the Sn 3d3/2 spectra, fitted with two subpeaks at 498.5 (SnI) and 495.2 (SnII) eV, indicating Sn–O bonds [30]. Notably, with an increase in the oxygen flow rate, there was a diminishing trend in the intensity of the SnII peak. At an oxygen flow rate of 30/3, the intensity of the SnI peak significantly increased, suggesting the formation of more metal–oxygen bonds, particularly Sn–O bonds. For an in-depth analysis of changes in oxygen vacancies and metal–oxygen bonds in the InZnSnO films, depicted in Fig. 3(d), the O peaks were deconvoluted into three distinct sub-peaks: loosely bound oxygen (–OH) at 532.2 eV (OI), oxygen vacancies (Vo) at 531.3 eV (OII), and metal–oxygen bonds (M − O) at approximately 530 eV (OIII) [29]. The ratio of oxygen vacancies can be expressed by the relative area of OII (OII/OI + OII + OIII), while the ratio of metal–oxygen bonds can be calculated using the relative area of OIII (OIII/OI + OII + OIII). The relative areas of oxygen vacancies for the InZnSnO films treated at 30/0, 30/1, 30/2, and 30/3 O2 flow rates were estimated to be 33.88 %, 34.22 %, 29.08 %, and 17.71 %, respectively. The corresponding relative areas of metal–oxygen bonds were determined to be 58.14 %, 57.88 %, 55.27 %, and 53.65 %. As shown in Fig. 3(d), with an increase in the oxygen flow rate, the relative area of OII peaks gradually decreased from 33.88 % to 17.71 %, while that of OIII peaks monotonically decreased from 58.14 % to 53.65 %. These results indicate that the introduction of oxygen during the sputtering process effectively reduces the presence of oxygen vacancies and enhances the oxygen lattices in InZnSnO films.

3.2. Sensing performance of InZnSnO-based EGFET pH sensors
The structure of an ISFET sensor closely resembles that of the MOSFET, with the distinction being the replacement of the metal gate by a reference electrode, electrolyte, and insulating oxide as a unified component. Consequently, the signal transmission mechanism is analogous, and the fundamental principle can be summarized as follows. When the ISFET is engaged in ion detection, a gate voltage (VGS) is applied via a reference electrode through an electrolyte. As VGS is progressively increased from a small to a larger value, it exerts a downward force on the holes within the ISFET substrate, triggering the accumulation of electrons and the eventual formation of a channel connecting the source and drain electrodes. Once VGS surpasses a specific threshold level (threshold voltage, VTH), the electron density within the channel becomes sufficient to enable drain current flow and generate drain voltage (VDS) between the source and drain electrodes. Consequently, the concentration of ions in the analytes can be directly quantified through VTH, as described by Eq. (1) [31].
(1)
In this equation, EREF represents the reference electrode potential; χsol denotes the surface dipole potential of the solvent; Ψ0, Qss, Qox, Cox, and QB signify the surface potential, density of interface state, charge density of gate, capacitance per unit area, and depletion charge density, respectively; φSi and φF are the work function of the silicon substrate and the Fermi potential, respectively. As a result, VTH is solely influenced by Ψ0, which is determined by the ion concentration. VTH plays a dual role, serving to control the on-off state of the ISFET and determine the ion concentration to facilitate the conversion of chemical signals into electrical ones. The equipment comprises a high input impedance FET, a signal line, and the gate-connected sensitive region. EGFET permits the incorporation of a standard commercial discrete MOSFET device linked to the gate extension. In contrast, the sensitive layer in an EGFET is recognized for its low impedance, resulting in increased conductivity and sensitivity. In practice, VTH cannot be directly measured and is instead inferred from other parameters such as VGS, VDS, or IDS, all of which are interrelated according to Eq. (2) [32].
(2)
In this equation, μn represents the electron mobility of the ISFET at room temperature and W/L denotes the width-to-length ratio of channel. Consequently, by designating certain parameters from Eq. (2) as constants, VTH can be indirectly derived from other variables, such as IDS, VGS, and VDS, characterizing the associated sensor as a potentiometric sensor. In the potentiometric principle, while maintaining IDS and VDS as constant parameters, alterations in VTH due to changes in ion concentration within the electrolyte are reflected through the VGS output. Conversely, when IDS and VGS are held steady, VTH is indicated by the VDS output. It is worth noting that the VGS output is the more commonly employed method in comparison to the VDS output.
Fig. 4(a)-(d) gives the IDS-VREF curves for the InZnSnO-based EGFET flexible sensors, which were subjected to treatment at four different O2 flow rates: 30/0, 30/1, 30/2, and 30/3. As the pH level increases, the IDS value decreases, as higher pH corresponds to a greater accumulation of OH− ions on the sensitive film in the alkaline solution, resulting in a negative voltage on the oxide surface. Conversely, lower pH levels lead to an increased accumulation of H+ ions in the acidic solution, associated with a positive voltage on the MOSFET device. To determine the pH sensitivity in relation to VREF, we evaluated the sensing response of the InZnSnO-based flexible sensor in the triode region.

The pH sensitivity of EGFET flexible sensors, within the pH range of 2–12, was determined by establishing a linear correlation between pH and VREF while keeping VDS at 0.1 V and IDS at 100 μA. The insets in Fig. 4(a)–(d) provide VREF values corresponding to pH levels for InZnSnO-based flexible sensors created under four different O2 flow rate conditions: 30/0, 30/1, 30/2, and 30/3. The pH sensitivity was quantified as follows: 54.54 mV/pH for the 30/0-treated sensor, 55.36 mV/pH for the 30/1-treated sensor, 59.58 mV/pH for the 30/2-treated sensor, and 53.23 mV/pH for the 30/3-treated sensor. Additionally, the linearity of these EGFET flexible sensors was excellent, with values of 99.758 %, 99.982 %, 99.983 %, and 99.919 %, respectively. Remarkably, the InZnSnO-based EGFET flexible sensor treated with an oxygen flow rate of 30/2 demonstrated the highest pH sensitivity, approaching a nearly Nernstian response of 59.4 mV/pH, and exhibited the most excellent linearity compared to all other conditions. This observation can be explained by the InZnSnO sensitive film possessing a rougher surface texture and enhancing the presence of oxygen lattices within the film. This not only ensures low resistivity but also leads to the creation of additional surface binding sites [31]. This, in turn, enhances the accumulation of H+ and OH− ions on the InZnSnO surface.
In addition to evaluating the pH sensitivity of an InZnSnO-sensitive film, we also examined the stability concerning hysteresis voltage and drift rate in the context of EGFET sensors. Fig. 5(a) presents the hysteresis curves for InZnSnO-based EGFET flexible sensors that were manufactured under different oxygen flow rates. These hysteresis measurements were conducted while cycling the pH from 7 to 4, then back to 7, followed by 10, and finally returning to 7 (7 → 4→7 → 10→7). The hysteresis effect is explained by the chemical interaction between surface oxide sites (e.g., chemical defects and oxygen vacancies) and electrolyte ions (e.g., H+ and OH−) present on the sensing film [33]. The hysteresis voltages measured were as follows: 17.1 mV for the 30/0-treated sensor, 10.7 mV for the 30/1-treated sensor, 1.8 mV for the 30/2-treated sensor, and 5.11 mV for the 30/3-treated sensor. Notably, the 30/2 oxygen flow rate resulted in the lowest hysteresis voltage, suggesting that an optimal oxygen flow rate may diminish both chemical defects and oxygen vacancies in the InZnSnO film. Beyond hysteresis voltage, the drift characteristic functions as an indicator of the stability of InZnSnO-based EGFET flexible sensors. These sensors find utility in monitoring diverse H+ ions or biological molecules in electrolyte solutions. However, during prolonged operation, chemical modifications may gradually occur on the surface of an oxide film or within the film itself. This leads to a gradual and temporary modulation in VTH in an EGFET or ISFET sensor, arising from variations in chemical composition that manifest on the film surface and cause a change in the overall capacitance of the oxide film [34]. Fig, 5(b) depicts the drift characteristics of InZnSnO-based EGFET flexible sensors under four different O2 flow rates (30/0, 30/1, 30/2, and 30/3) during testing in a buffer solution at pH 7. It is noteworthy that the 30/2 flow rate condition exhibited the lowest drift rate (0.14 mV/h), while the 30/0 flow rate condition showed the highest drift rate (2.22 mV/h). This heightened instability in the latter case could be attributed to the InZnSnO film having more defects or oxygen vacancies.

Moreover, to evaluate the enduring mechanical durability of the sensing properties under bending stress, the InZnSnO-based flexible sensor underwent multiple bending cycles. In Fig. 5(c), the pH sensing performance of the InZnSnO-based flexible sensor is depicted after a series of bending cycles. Notably, even after undergoing up to 500 repeated bending cycles, the pH sensitivity and linearity of this EGFET flexible sensor remained virtually unchanged. However, a sudden decline in performance became apparent after 700 repeated bending cycles, suggesting the onset of microcracks and alterations in the microstructure of the sensing film. In addition to high sensitivity, stability is a crucial factor for the practicality of sensors. To ascertain the robustness of an EGFET sensor, the sensing performance of the InZnSnO-based flexible sensor was evaluated after 180 days of continuous operation. In Fig. 5(d), it is demonstrated that there was no noticeable degradation in sensitivity for the InZnSnO-based flexible sensor, highlighting its high reliability and stability over at least 180 days. After this extended period of operation, the pH sensitivity of the InZnSnO-based flexible sensor remained at ∼58.5 mV/pH, identical to that of a fresh device. This observation indicates that the InZnSnO-sensitive film possesses remarkable long-term stability.
The InZnSnO-sensitive film utilized in the EGFET flexible sensor exhibited outstanding performance, achieving the highest sensitivity value of 59.58 mV/pH, the lowest drift rate of 0.14 mV/h, and the smallest hysteresis voltage of 1.8 mV. This performance surpasses that of previously reported works utilizing different fabrication methods and various sensitive film materials, including SnO2 [19], V2O5 [19], InSnO [19], InGaZnO [19], AlGaZnO [17], Al-doped ZnO [19], Nb2O5 [19], Bi2O3 [20], Ga2O3, [21]. Clearly, the EGFET sensor incorporating the InZnSnO-sensitive film demonstrates notably high pH sensitivity and remarkable electrochemical stability. The InZnSnO-sensitive film emerges as an innovative and promising material for a wide range of pH sensors.
3.3. Detection of epinephrine in sera using InZnSnO-based EGFET sensors
In Fig. 6(a), you can see the transfer characteristics of the InZnSnO-based EGFET flexible sensor for different concentrations of epinephrine. The graph depicts that as the epinephrine concentration increases, the VREF value shifts towards a higher VGS voltage, indicating the presence of negative charge on the InZnSnO surface. Additionally, Fig. 6(a) demonstrates the shift in the IDS curves, which are plotted against the epinephrine concentration ranging from 10−7 to 0.1 μM. As the epinephrine concentration increases, the VREF value shifts in the positive direction, indicating that the interaction between epinephrine and the boronic acid of 4-FPBA results in the formation of a boronate-epinephrine ester complex, which carries a negative charge in its anionic form. This alteration induces modulation in the carrier concentration of the MOSFET channel. In Fig. 6(b), the dynamic response curve is shown when the InZnSnO-based EGFET flexible sensor is exposed to different epinephrine concentrations. This sensor exhibited remarkable stability in sensing epinephrine. Additionally, Fig. 6(c) presents the extracted VREF value of the InZnSnO-based EGFET flexible sensor plotted against epinephrine concentration, ranging from 10−7 to 0.1 μM. The dose response is linear across a wide range, spanning six decades of epinephrine concentration. The sensitivity of our InZnSnO-based EGFET flexible sensor to epinephrine is 8.44 mV/pCepinephrine, calculated as the slope of the dose response, with an excellent linearity of 99.962 % and a low limit of detection (LOD) of 5 × 10−8 μM. This sensor demonstrates a broad dynamic range, accommodating epinephrine concentrations from 10−7 to 0.1 μM.

These performance attributes are superior to other FET sensors that employ Nafion + SWNTs/Pt [35], Pt [36], Al2O3 [37], BiVO4 nanofiber [38], and molecular imprinting polymer (MIP) [39]. The InZnSnO-based flexible sensor, being rapid and label-free, offers advantages such as a simple structure, ease of production, cost-effectiveness, and reliability. Consequently, it serves as a practical tool for human health monitoring.
To assess non-specific binding of neurotransmitters to the functionalized InZnSnO-based EGFET flexible sensor surface, a selectivity experiment was conducted. Five different neurotransmitters, including histamine, serotonin, phenethylamine, N-methylphenethylamine, and tyramine, were applied to the InZnSnO-based EGFET flexible sensor to investigate its selectivity. Fig. 6(d) gives the changes in VREF for the InZnSnO-based EGFET sensor during the detection of epinephrine, histamine, serotonin, phenethylamine, N-methylphenethylamine, and tyramine in standard serum. The concentration of these neurotransmitters is 0.1 μM. Notably, the ΔVREF values for histamine, serotonin, phenethylamine, N-methylphenethylamine, and tyramine were significantly reduced by more than ∼80 % compared to epinephrine, which retained the highest ΔVREF voltage. The findings indicate that our InZnSnO-based EGFET flexible sensor is primarily designed for detecting epinephrine, as opposed to the other tested neurotransmitters. This suggests that the InZnSnO-based EGFET flexible sensor possesses a strong selectivity for detecting epinephrine.
4. Conclusion
This study investigates how the oxygen flow rate affects the sensing performance and structural properties of InZnSnO-sensitive films cultivated on a flexible polyimide substrate for solid-state pH sensors. The XRD data indicates that InZnSnO films processed at four distinct oxygen flow rates demonstrated an amorphous structure. The AFM morphologies revealed that the surface roughness gradually increased as the oxygen flow rate increased. Additionally, SIMS analyses demonstrated a decline in Sn ion content with increasing oxygen flow rate. Moreover, under the 30/2 flow rate condition, XPS examination revealed optimal metal-oxygen bonds and reduced oxygen vacancies in the InZnSnO film. The InZnSnO sensitive film prepared under the 30/2 flow rate condition displayed superior performance, characterized by higher sensitivity (59.58 mV/pH), lower drift rate (0.14 mV/h), and a smaller hysteresis voltage (1.8 mV) compared to other O2 flow rate conditions. This enhanced performance can be attributed to the optimal oxygen flow rate, which reduces oxygen vacancies, enhances metal-oxygen bonds, and promotes the formation of an InZnSnO layer on the film. Moreover, the InZnSnO-based flexible sensor exhibited remarkable stability, maintaining a pH sensitivity of ∼58.5 mV/pH after 180 days of operation. To enable rapid and label-free detection of epinephrine in human serum, 4-FPBA was immobilized on an APTES-functionalized InZnSnO-based flexible sensor. The fabricated InZnSnO-based flexible sensor demonstrated the capability to detect epinephrine with high sensitivity and linearity, measuring 8.44 mV/pCepinephrine and 99.962 %, respectively, within the epinephrine concentration range of 10−7 to 0.1 μM. Consequently, this disposable InZnSnO-based flexible sensor enables rapid and label-free detection with exceptionally high sensitivity, reliability, and selectivity, eliminating the need for additional instruments to produce results. Our InZnSnO-based flexible sensor, characterized by its rapid, easy fabrication, high sensitivity, and good selectivity, holds the potential to play a significant role in evaluating human health.
