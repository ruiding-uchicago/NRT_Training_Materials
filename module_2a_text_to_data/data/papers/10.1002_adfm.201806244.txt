Abstract
The development of van der Waals heterostructures in 2D materials systems has attracted considerable interests for exploring new insights of (opto-) electrical characteristics, device physics, and novel functional applications. Utilizing organic molecular material with strong electron withdrawing ability, charge transfer van der Waals interfaces are formed between 2,3,5,6-tetrafluoro-7,7,8,8-tetracyanoquinodimethane (F4TCNQ) and MoS2, via which the modulation of the onset voltages and optimization of subthreshold swing values in MoS2-based field effect transistors are realized. Charge transfer process and its functionality mechanisms are further verified and investigated with first-principles calculation, scanning Kelvin probe microscope characterization, and temperature-dependent electrical characterization. With the charge transfer effect between reducing gas molecules and F4TCNQ, NH3 gas sensor is proposed and fabricated with the sensitivity reaching higher than 1000% at 100 ppm, much more outstanding performance than those of any reported MoS2-based NH3 gas sensors. The F4TCNQ-MoS2 hybrid strategy might open up a pathway for tuning and optimizing the electrical properties, in addition to novel functional units designing and fabrications in electric devices based on low-dimensional semiconducting systems.

1 Introduction
Thin-layered transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDCs) have drew much attention in recent years due to their unique electric, optoelectric, mechanical, and magnetic properties, meanwhile, they offer a new platform for exploring new fundamental physics in low-dimensional systems together with pathways for breakthrough in technologies.1-7 Among them, molybdenum disulfide (MoS2) has been most intensively investigated.8-14 When thinning MoS2 from bulk flake to an atomic monolayer, the bandgap would increase from 1.2 to 1.8 eV, along with a crossover from indirect to direct bandgap.15 The ideal electronic band structure brings promising features such as field effect conductivity, relative higher mobility comparing to their flexible counterpart organic semiconductor, excellent photoresponse, and so on.16-18 These lead to great potential applications in various functional devices, among which, MoS2 based field-effect transistors (FETs) attracted most interest.

In FETs applications, few-layered MoS2 could break through the scaling limit of bulk semiconducting materials, in addition to maintaining ideal electric performance.19-21 Whereas, one drawback that might be a bottleneck to the applications is that the existence of sulfur vacancies brings great uncertainty into the electric properties.22 Due to the volatility of chalcogen atoms, sulfur vacancies universally exist in both chemical vapor deposition (CVD)-grown and mechanical exfoliated MoS2 flakes, which would introduce gap states just below the conduction band edge, leading to localization and undesirable electrons doping.23-26 For instance, MoS2-based FETs are generally in on-states without applying any gate voltage because of the sulfur vacancies related donor states. Extra negative bias would be needed to shut down the devices, which would inevitably introduce power consumption even when devices are in nonoperating states. In addition, poor subthreshold swing (SS) and low reliability are always correlated to the sulfur vacancy states.

In recent years, van der Waals heterostructures, obtained by artificially stacking 2D materials layers or organic van der Waals crystalline materials upon each other, have exhibited great potential in optoelectrical properties improvement and functional structures constructions.27-31 In this work, we propose a strategy of organic charge transfer molecules treatment for MoS2 surface, with the formed organic–inorganic van der Waals interfaces (vdWIs), on one hand to address the incidental doping of sulfur vacancies, on the other hand to explore the novel microscopic interaction mechanisms within the organic–inorganic vdWIs, in addition to the possible functional applications. 2,3,5,6-Tetrafluoro-7,7,8,8-tetracyanoquinodimethane (F4TCNQ) with strong electron-withdrawing property is employed, whose lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) locates at around ELUMO = −5.3 eV.32-34 Charge transfer vdWIs are formed by vacuum depositing F4TCNQ onto the surface of MoS2, at which electron transfer takes place from MoS2 layer to F4TCNQ nanoparticles. With the depletion of residual electrons from sulfur vacancies, the onset voltages (Von's) of MoS2-based FETs are modulated from minus dozens volts to around zero, together with more ideal SS values. The existence of effective charge transfer and its functionality mechanisms are further verified and investigated with first-principles calculation, scanning Kelvin probe microscope (SKPM) characterization, and temperature-dependent electrical characterization. In addition, potential applications of this novel charge transfer vdWIs are demonstrated with reducing gas molecules sensing test. Based on the mechanisms of charge transfer between the interface and the gas analyte, ultrasensitive ammonia (NH3) gas sensor is fabricated. The sensitivity could exceed 1000%, 2–3 orders larger than that of bared MoS2-based FET sensor in this work, and higher than those of any reported MoS2-based devices. The proposed organic–inorganic vdWIs in tuning the electronic properties of MoS2-based FETs might open up a pathway for optimization of TMDCs-based electric, optoelectric devices, and mining their potentials in functional applications.

2 Results and Discussion
Device structure is shown in Figure 1a, silicon wafers of 300 nm thermally oxidized SiO2 are employed as substrates, onto which FETs channels are formed with mechanical exfoliated MoS2 flakes of various thickness ranging from monolayer to around 5 nm; Chromium/gold electrodes are fabricated with electrons beam lithography (EBL); F4TCNQ is vacuum evaporated onto the surface of MoS2, forming disconnected nanoparticles. Compared with solvent method for coating F4TCNQ, vacuum evaporation could more precisely monitor and control the nominal thickness of organic molecules' deposition.35 Optic image of one selected F4TCNQ-decorated MoS2 FET is shown in Figure 1b, together with the atomic force microscopic (AFM) image, the organic nanoparticles could be apparently identified on the surface of MoS2 flake. What's interesting that should be mentioned is, on fresh exfoliated MoS2 surface large single crystal-like F4TCNQ plates could form as shown in Figure S1a (Supporting Information), while on FET's MoS2 flakes, denser distributed nanoparticles are shown in Figure S1b (Supporting Information), this might be attributed to the EBL process's influence on the MoS2 surface. Figure 1c is the schematic diagram for the anticipated charge transfer process at the vdWIs between MoS2 and F4TCNQ.36 Due to the ultralow F4TCNQ's LUMO at about ELUMO = −5.3 eV, electrons residual in MoS2's donors or extended band states would tend to be extracted by the oxidizing organic particles. Space charge region (SCR) would form at the interface with transferred charge accumulating and band bending. Electrons become immobile in the disconnected organic particles, while simultaneously, holes at the donor states in MoS2 would be strongly localized.

Electric characterizations are performed on MoS2 FETs with different active layer thickness from monolayer to 5 nm. Figure 1d shows the transfer curves of linear region at drain–source voltage Vds = 2 V, for FETs with/without F4TCNQ's coating. FETs of bare MoS2 layer show onset voltages around Von = −30 V, while after coating of F4TCNQ, positive shift of the transfer curves takes place, and the positions of Von's are more concentrated distributed within the range from −10 to 0 V. This should be attributed to the charge transfer occurring at the vdWIs, in which process, the mobile electrons (could transport via multiple trapping and release process between the donor states and the extended band) distributed in the MoS2 layer are captured by the F4TCNQ's particles, and become highly localized.37, 38 Unlike reported methods for modulating,39 the formation of the vdWIs has not induce any degradation of FETs mobility, in addition, the subthreshold swings (SS) are universally improved, as shown in Figure 1e, one selected device shows improvement in SS value from 1.9 to 0.8 V dec−1. Modulation in Von's and SSs in eight MoS2 FETs are shown in Figure S2 (Supporting Information), average value of 30 V shift in Von's is obtained, and the SSs values are generally optimized with the deposition of F4TCNQ. For avoiding excessive depletion of the electrons in the MoS2 layer, to probe an appropriate nominal thickness of F4TCNQ is necessary (detailed content could be obtained in Figure S3, Supporting Information).

First-principles calculations are employed to analyze the charge transfer and electronic properties' modulation of MoS2 decorated by F4TCNQ, with considering the effect of sulfur vacancies. Low coverage absorption of F4TCNQ molecules on MoS2 is calculated to identify the lowest energy configuration, as shown in Figure 2a. Density of states (DOS) for intrinsic MoS2, MoS2 with sulfur vacancy, and F4TCNQ-coated MoS2 with sulfur vacancy are obtained. As shown in Figure 2b, compared with intrinsic bare MoS2, the sulfur vacancy could introduce gap states within 0.5 eV below the conduction band edge, whose doping effect should be responsible for the residual conductance of MoS2 FETs at Vgs = 0. Figure 2c shows the influence of F4TCNQ's absorption. The red line represents the organic molecules contribution to the total DOS of the hybrid system, F4TCNQs' LUMOs form gap states located near the MoS2's valence band edge, which would act as acceptor-like states. Dedoping process would occur with electrons transferring from the sulfur vacancies related donor states to the F4TCNQs' LUMOs related acceptor states. Due to the discontinuity of the F4TCNQ layer, the electrons become strongly localized at the acceptor states, and would not contribute to the conductance (MTR is highly suppressed owing to the depth of the location of acceptor states).

To further verify and investigate the charge transfer at the organic–inorganic vdWIs, AFM and SKPM are employed to characterize the surface of the F4TCNQ-decorated MoS2.40 Figure 3a shows the surface topology morphology of 5 µm × 5 µm area, uniformly distributed island-like F4TCNQ particles of hundreds nanometers could be identified, average height is around 20 nm. Surface potential distribution is illustrated in Figure 3b, low potential centers could evidently be observed at the site where the F4TCNQ islands locate, while other region with no F4TCNQ's coverage show several tens higher potential. This suggests that electrons accumulate within the F4TCNQ's particles making the potentials lower than other region, indicating the effective charge transfer taking place at the interface. To gain a deeper insight, further annealing is performed to make the F4TCNQ islands larger and more accumulated, an islands cluster as high as 60 nm is selected for AFM/SKPM measurements. The topology morphology and surface potential distribution are shown in Figure 3c,d. It is noted that the potential at the F4TCNQ islands region are not that uniformly low, at the edge of the island with lower height, the potential seems higher than the center. This could be clearly identified via profiles extraction with cross lines shown in Figure 3e, and the topology morphology shows wave packet-like profile, the edge of the packet denoted as position 1 and 2 exhibit potential around 80–90 mV lower than the F4TCNQ-free region, while the potential of the peak region is about −60 mV, not that low compared with the edge region. This phenomenon should be corresponded with the finite thickness of the space charge region. The transferred electrons in F4TCNQ particles actually distribute in a finite range with respect to the vdWIs, which is known as the space charge region in p-n junction, as shown in schematic diagram of Figure 3f. When the SKPM tip interacts with the space charge layer at the edge part, the measured potential would be lower due to higher electrons concentration, compared with the peak region away from the space charge region. The nonuniformity potential distribution in a F4TCNQ particle provides another argument for verifying the existence of interface charge transfer.

Besides the nondegraded mobility and the maintained bandlike temperature-dependent conductance with the coated organic molecules (Figure S4, Supporting Information), temperature dependence of the charge transfer process is investigated with electric characterization at temperature ranging from 100 K to room temperature. With the coating of F4TCNQ, Von's of MoS2 FET above 160 K distribute within 0 to −10 V, while Von's shift back to around −50 V at 100 K and below, similar to the electrical performance before F4TCNQ's deposition. This indicates the suppression of the effective charge transfer process at lower temperature, leading to the functional failure of F4TCNQ. As shown in Figure 4b, the decreasing of the temperature results in the weakening of the diffusion process at the vdWIs, shrinking the space charge region, followed by the back-shifting of the onset voltage. Between temperature T = 160 and 100 K, the anomalously fast switching on appeared at T = 120 and 140 K, meantime noted with SS values as small as 0.15–0.2 V dec−1. We attribute the sudden opening of the device to the vertical electric field's modulation on the charge transfer. Besides the build-in electric field within the vdWIs, the gate applied voltage could influence the charge transfer efficiency by modulating the built-in voltage and the polaron barrier between two localized states.41, 42 Between temperature T = 160 and 100 K, when negative gate voltage becomes larger (absolute value), the polaron barriers shown in Figure 4b are smaller, and charge transfer is easier to take place, F4TCNQ molecules regularly functioning, therefore FET would be at off state; with gate voltage sweeping forward and the electric field becoming weaker, the polaron barrier height would increase to hinder the charge transfer, which would lead to the defunctionalization of the organic molecules, and FET sudden switches on. Actually, as shown in Figure 4c, the transfer curves at T = 120 and 140 K are hybrid ones, which contains sudden transition of transistors from F4TCNQ's regularly working states to the organic molecules'' functional failing states. The anomalously fast switching on properties might be utilized in fabrication of novel ultrafast open transistors with selecting proper charge transfer materials in future works.

To explore the potential applications of the organic–inorganic vdWIs, by taking advantages of the charge transfer effect, we carried out ammonia gas sensing tests with the F4TCNQ-modified MoS2 FET together with bared MoS2 FET as comparison. We propose the FET gas sensor for NH3 detection based on the following mechanisms, as shown in Figure 5a. There is no analyte molecule, and electrons in MoS2 layer would be depleted by the F4TCNQ molecules via the charge transfer process we discussed above. Once we introduce gas molecules with reducibility such as NH3 to the surrounding atmosphere of the FET, charge transfer might take place between F4TCNQ and gas molecules. This process would consequently suppress the charge transfer effect, and electrons captured by F4TCNQ would be released back to the channel, bringing variation to the FET's conductance, which acts as the response signal to the reducing gas molecules. Figure 5b displays the NH3 sensing performance of the device, with transfer curves of FET at different NH3 concentration. Consistent with the anticipation, when exposed to NH3 molecules, the FET's drain current drastically increases, while the bared MoS2-based FET (inset of Figure 5b) shows conductance decrease under NH3 exposure, which is similar in behavior to the previous reported MoS2-based NH3 sensors.43, 44 This suggests different sensing mechanisms between MoS2 FETs with and without F4TCNQ. As we have proposed above, the NH3-induced current increase in F4TCNQ-MoS2 system stems from the charge transfer process, that is, the defunctionalization of the dedoping effect; while in bared MoS2 device, the polar NH3 molecules absorbed on the surface of MoS2 channel would introduce dipole-disorder into the electronic structure, leading to widening of the band tail DOS with the charge carriers more influenced by the localized states (illustrated in Figure S5, Supporting Information). Although the F4TCNQ-MoS2 system would be influenced by the dipole interaction, apparently the charge transfer effect domains the gas sensing response. The transfer curve could almost recover to the original one after purging N2 for 300 s, as shown in Figure 5b, revealing the ideal repeatability in gas sensing application with reversible charge transfer process.

The sensitivity that is a key factor to identify the gas sensing performance of the sensor could be defined as . At transistor's on states with drain–source voltage Vds = 1 V, gate–source voltage Vgs = 5 V, S values are extracted at different NH3 concentration pNH3 = 100, 500, 1000, and 2000 ppm. And for sensing behavior at transistor's off state, we note the ultrasensitive response for the F4TCNQ-modified MoS2 channel, which fast switches from off state to subthreshold region or on states at the exposure to NH3. We define response ratio , to depict the several orders change in currents of the FET. As S values displayed in Figure 5c, the sensitivity of F4TCNQ-MoS2 hybrid device is as high as 1460% at 100 ppm, 2–3 orders larger than the comparison of bared MoS2 device, and much more sensitive than previously reported MoS2-based NH3 gas sensors,43-48 as shown in Table S1 (Supporting Information). For sensing at transistor's off states, the RR values could reach as high as 1.5 × 107 at pNH3 = 100 ppm, and 4 × 108 for higher concentration, comparable with the on/off ratio value of a typical thin film transistor, that is, the exposure of NH3 could make the transistor switch from off to on states, these superb properties make it possible to integrate the sensor in digital circuit sensing networks. Figure 5d shows the continuous cycles of NH3 sensing, the response time is around several tens seconds scale, and the reproducibility of the sensing is confirmed. To quantify the density of electrons transfer from NH3 to F4TCNQ, we analyze the threshold voltage shift in the sensing process, the variation in mobile charge density in channel could be obtained with , as shown in Figure 5e, which the charge transfer density is around the 1011 cm−2 during the interaction of analyte molecules with sensor. Besides, the detection limit down to 100 ppm was investigated experimentally, in addition to the selectivity tests on several gas including both reducing and oxidizing ones, as shown in Figure S6 (Supporting Information).

3 Conclusion
In summary, we proposed a strategy to tune the MoS2-based FETs' electrical behavior via organic–inorganic vdWIs. Utilizing organic molecular material F4TCNQ with strong electron-withdrawing ability, the tens negative onset voltages could be tuned to near zero together with the improvement of subthreshold swing values and with no degradation of the field effect mobility. The employed first-principles calculation enabled us to gain insight into the charge transfer process between sulfur vacancies contributed donor states and F4TCNQs' LUMO related acceptor states. We further verified and investigated the charge transfer process at the vdWIs with SKPM characterization, and formation of space charge region was observed. With the temperature-dependent electric characterization, an anomalous fast onset of the FET was corresponded with the modulation of the temperature and electric field on the charge transfer efficiency. Based on the charge transfer mechanisms of F4TCNQ-MoS2 hybrid system, NH3 gas sensor was proposed and fabricated. By utilizing the charge transfer effect between NH3 and F4TCNQ, the sensitivity could reach more than 1000% at 100 ppm together with response ratio (
) as high as 107, much more outstanding performance than those of any reported MoS2-based NH3 gas sensors.

4 Experimental Section
Devices Fabrication: The SiO2/Si (300 nm thick thermal oxide) was used as substrate, onto which the MoS2 flakes were mechanically exfoliated. The substrates were then coated with poly(methyl methacrylate) (PMMA) followed by electron beam exposure, to define the source–drain electrodes. 5 nm chromium and 40 nm gold were, respectively, deposited as metal contacts with electron beam evaporation technique. Then the PMMA was lifted off, and the devices were annealed at T = 150 °C in glove box filled with Ar. F4TCNQ deposition was performed with thermal vacuum evaporation in Edwards AUTO 306. 5 nm initial layer of F4TCNQ was deposited followed by 70 °C annealing for 30 min in glove box.

Material Characterization and Electrical Measurements: AFM/SKPM measurements were carried out with a Bruke multimode AFM system in ambient atmosphere at tapping mode (morphology) and lift-up mode (potential), with a scanning rate of 0.3 Hz. Electric characterizations were carried out with Keithley 4200 semiconductor measurement system. The devices were vacuumed in a Lake shore made four-probe system. The temperature range is 100–300 K in the temperature dependence characterization.

Gas Sensing: To test the sensing property of NH3 gas, the FET was biased at a drain voltage of −1 V while keeping the gate voltage sweeping from +5 to −60 V. Before the gas sensing, the chamber was pumped to less than 1 Torr, and then N2 was purged into the chamber up to 1 atm. The device was tested for original data, then pumped down again. Different concentrations of NH3 from 100 to 2000 ppm were obtained by mixing NH3 with N2, then the mix gas with 1 atm pressure was introduced into the chamber. The exposure time of NH3 gas into the chamber was 60 s.
