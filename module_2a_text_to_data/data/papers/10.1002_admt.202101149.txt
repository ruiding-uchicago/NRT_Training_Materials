Abstract
Hygroscopic insulator field effect transistors (HIFETs) are a class of solid-state, low-voltage organic thin film transistors with promising sensitivity to hydrogen peroxide (H2O2). While work has progressed on the development and understanding of HIFET-based sensors, important questions regarding the fundamental H2O2 sensing mechanisms need to be fully investigated. In this work, a detailed study of the effects of H2O2 on the transient drain currents and transfer characteristics of HIFETs, while varying analyte concentrations and device voltages, is presented. The sensing mechanism is found to be consistent with the direct oxidation of the organic semiconductor channel, poly(3-hexylthiophene-2,5-diyl), by H2O2 diffused into the device through the permeable top gate electrode and hygroscopic insulator. Further, the importance of having a transistor structure in sensing H2O2, as compared to a two-terminal device, is demonstrated. These results represent important progress in the understanding of HIFET-based sensors, revealing paths for future applications and optimization.

1 Introduction
Organic thin film transistors (OTFTs) have been developed for sensing a wide array of chemical substances and physical phenomena as diverse as DNA,[1] pressure,[2] humidity,[3] glucose,[4] dopamine,[5] pH,[6] and hydrogen peroxide (H2O2).[7] H2O2 is a small, unstable molecule with strong oxidizing properties, and its significance in different fields has made it a target for OTFT-based sensors.[8] Industrial applications of H2O2 include rocket propellant,[9] textile bleaching,[10] and sanitization of machinery for food preparation and packaging.[11] In medicine and biology, H2O2 is relevant for its cytotoxicity, hence its frequent use as a disinfectant.[12] Within organisms, it is generated by immune cells to aid the destruction of foreign microbes, and is used as a cellular signaling molecule.[8] Significantly, H2O2 is produced in enzyme-driven oxidation of biologically relevant molecules, including glucose by glucose oxidase and uric acid by urate oxidase.[13] As a result, devices sensitive to H2O2 can form the basis of a variety of medically relevant biosensors with the addition of a suitable enzyme as a recognition element.

The hygroscopic insulator field effect transistor (HIFET) is a class of OTFT which employs a hygroscopic insulating layer that can behave as a solid-state electrolyte upon absorbing moisture from the environment. The result is a simple, low-voltage, solution processable, and solid-state device, with intriguing potential for low-cost biosensing and biointerfacing applications. In oxidative enzymatic biosensors based on HIFETs, selectivity is achieved through the enzyme whereas sensitivity is achieved through sensing H2O2.15 HIFETs with promising sensitivity to H2O2 have been demonstrated.[7, 14-16] While a wide variety of H2O2 sensors exist,[17] the biocompatibility of organic materials, and the ability to create very thin, light, and flexible devices, makes OTFTs particularly suitable for biosensing applications. Due to their simple, solid-state architecture, without the need for separate reference electrodes or electrolyte containment, HIFETs are an appealing option. To realize the full potential of HIFETs in oxidative enzymatic biosensors, understanding and optimizing the H2O2 detection mechanism in HIFETs is necessary.

For HIFETs, the earliest proposed sensing mechanism was that H2O2 is oxidized at the gate electrode due to the applied voltage (above ≈0.7 V) as per the oxidation half-reaction given in equation 1, and the liberated protons (H+) then diffuse through the device and dope the poly(3-hexylthiophene) (P3HT) channel, which in turn modulates the current in the channel.[15] This mechanism fully explained our earlier investigations and reports on HIFET sensors[16]

In our recent investigation of H2O2 sensing mechanisms using confocal fluorescence microscopy, we found evidence for two other processes of doping the P3HT channel, which cannot be explained by protons doping the P3HT channel.[18] One process occurred uniformly throughout the P3HT layer in the absence of applied voltages. When a potential was applied between drain and source (Vds = −1 V), doping also occurred throughout the film, but was strengthened over the source electrode and weakened over the drain electrode. We hypothesized that the first process was direct oxidation of the P3HT layer by the concentrated H2O2 analyte, which had diffused into the device through the permeable gate electrode. The second, voltage-dependent process, was consistent with anodic decomposition of H2O2 generating charge carriers over the source electrode, and standard cation-driven de-doping over the drain electrode.[18] Our results suggest that direct oxidation of the active semiconductor layer by hydrogen peroxide plays a significant, and likely primary, role in our experiments.[18] However, this hypothesis requires further investigation and confirmation, and the exact role and contribution of the electrochemical decomposition of H2O2 needs to be explored.

In this report, we present a detailed analysis of the effect of H2O2 on HIFET characteristics, to gain deeper insight into the sensing mechanisms and evaluate our earlier proposal. Building upon previous work, we study the effect of H2O2 concentration, across four orders of magnitude, on the Id modulation and device figures of merit. We also examine the effect of different Vg and Vds values on the Id sensing modulation, to understand how electric fields in the device influence the sensing response. Considering these observations, together with insights from previous reports, we make an important advance toward understanding H2O2 sensing mechanisms in HIFETs.

2 Results and Discussion
2.1 Transistor Characteristics
The device structure for HIFETs used in this study is shown in Figure 1a and representative output and transfer characteristics for the HIFETs used in this study are given in Figure S1 (Supporting Information). A HIFET functions analogously to an electrolyte-gated OFET (EGOFET). The solid-state poly(4-vinylphenol) (PVP) insulating layer is weakly acidic and its phenol groups ionize when exposed to moisture. The aqueous mobile cations (H+) and fixed phenol anions facilitate the high capacitance of the device, and hence its low-voltage operation.[19] A negative gate voltage causes holes to accumulate in the P3HT channel. Figure 1a shows holes accumulated in the channel in the saturation regime, where the large Vds causes the formation of a depletion region near the drain electrode. As evident in Figure S1b (Supporting Information), HIFETs in this configuration exhibit a positive threshold voltage, owing to a p-type doping effect of PVP at the interface with the P3HT channel material. A positive voltage is therefore applied to accumulate cations at the interface, depleting the P3HT channel and achieving an ‘OFF’ state (Figure 1b). In HIFETs, the OFF current is generally larger than would be expected of equivalent OFETs, which may be attributed to gate-to-drain leakage current.

Figure 1c plots the gate-source voltage (Vg), drain current, (Id) and gate current (Ig) with time. The transient response of device currents to a change of gate voltage provides useful insight into fundamental HIFET behavior and functions as a useful “case study” to inform the analysis of the H2O2 sensing response that follows later in this report. In Figure 1c, the Vds is held at −1 V, but at t = 0 s Vg is stepped from 0 to +0.3, −0.3, −0.6 or −1.0 V. As expected, the device is already ‘ON’ at Vg = 0 V, and a low, negative Id is observed (holes entering drain). At the same time, there is a small, positive Ig. This indicates a leakage current through the PVP layer. In this steady-state condition, the measured current at all three device electrodes sum close to zero (Id + Is + Ig = 0), indicating that all current flowing in flows out, or is balanced. At Vg = 0 V, the current measured at the source electrode (Is) (not shown) is nearly identical to Id, but of opposite polarity. In magnitude, Id is larger than Is by approximately the size of Ig, indicating that the difference is due to the gate leakage current flowing from gate to drain.

When gate voltage is changed, Id and Ig respond rapidly, and eventually find new steady states. The long duration needed to reach a steady state, particularly for larger gate voltages, reflects the relatively slow ionic diffusion mechanism in the PVP layer that underlies the doping and de-doping of the channel. The Ig response is a sharp spike that decays, resembling the charging current of a capacitor. If a negative gate voltage is applied, cations in the PVP move toward the gate, holes are injected into the channel (Figure 1a), and correspondingly, holes are removed from the gate. The negative gate current spike thus likely reflects holes exiting the gate electrode through the gate terminal. The opposite occurs when a positive gate voltage is applied (Figure 1b). Bernards and Malliaras have described similar behavior with a transient model for OECTs.[20] They note that spike-and-recovery behavior in the gate current occurs when hole transport is slow compared with ion transport.[20] Interestingly, we observe that the sum Id + Is + Ig deviates significantly from 0 for a few seconds immediately following the voltage step. The cause of this is uncertain, but may point toward a phenomenon like charge trapping, where charge enters the device but does not exit.

2.2 H2O2 Sensitivity
To characterize the sensitivity of HIFETs to H2O2, we measured the transient Id response to 5 µL of H2O2 solution across a range of concentrations, from 30 wt% to 0.03 wt% (9.8 to 9.8 × 10−3 m). Electrode voltages, Vg and Vds, were held at −0.3 V and −1 V, respectively. Figure 2a shows representative Id response curves for each concentration, including the response to deionized water as a baseline or ‘control’ trial and Figure 2b shows the effect on Ig. The Id response is given as the percent change in Id: 100 × ΔId(t)/Id(t = 0). To quantitatively compare these responses, we plot the averages for maximum Id modulation (Figure 2c), as well as a parameter β, the ratio of total charge gained within 200 s of analyte deposition (Figure 2d) (see supporting information for details). Device response times, defined as the time taken to reach 95% of the maximum Id reached, are given in Figure 2e. The overall trend in Figure 2 indicates that the Id response is strongly dependent on concentration. At low concentrations, maximum Id modulations are poorly differentiated (Figure 2c), but the β parameter proves more sensitive (Figure 2d) as it captures the reduced overall charge due to the slower response time.

In Figure 2, Id modulations are larger and faster with increasing concentration. The increase in the magnitude of Id (in the negative direction) reflects an increase in hole current through the drain electrode, which superficially resembles the effect of applying a negative gate voltage step (Figure 1c). The increase in Id is not simply an effect of increased gate-to-drain leakage current, as a modulation of nearly identical magnitude is observed in Is (indicating the modulated Id is originating from the source and flowing through the channel) and the measured gate current is orders of magnitude smaller. Therefore, the modulation can be considered a result of the doping of the P3HT channel, arising from the presence of H2O2. The variation in response times is indicative of a diffusion-dominated doping process. This behavior in HIFET sensors has been reported before,[16] and is consistent with Fick's laws of diffusion, which describe increased diffusion rate with larger concentration gradients.[21]

The behavior of the gate current, shown in Figure 2b, is also worth considering. Before introducing the analyte, Ig is usually very small and positive, though in a few cases a larger, negative Ig is recorded. The total gate leakage current is the net sum of a negative source-to-gate leakage and a positive gate-to-drain leakage. Gate leakage is often modeled as resulting from defects in the dielectric layer.[22] In that case, random variation in the distribution of such defects may alter the magnitude of either leakage component, and hence the direction of the net flow of charge. Regardless of its starting value, once the analyte is deposited, Ig consistently shifts in the negative direction. Negative gate current indicates a net flow of holes out of (or electrons into) the HIFET through the gate terminal. Initially, this may reflect a capacitive effect to maintain charge neutrality, where holes exit the gate to compensate the increase in holes in the channel, resembling the behavior seen in Figure 2 upon stepping to a negative gate voltage. However, the continuous, elevated Ig in the sensing response indicates an additional effect. The trajectory of Ig following sensing is erratic and varies considerably between individual devices. A similar behavior is seen with water and all concentrations of H2O2, suggesting that the elevated Ig is, in large part, an effect of the hydration of the device and an increase in PVP conductivity.

2.3 Effect of H2O2 on Figures of Merit
HIFET characteristics change appreciably after deposition of the analyte. This is illustrated in Figure 3, where we show representative transfer characteristics before and after deposition of deionized water, 0.3 and 30 wt% H2O2. Deionized water usually decreases the magnitude of Id throughout the Vg sweep (Figure 3a), whereas H2O2 typically results in an increase in Id (Figure 3a,b). We gain further insight into the effect of H2O2 on HIFET device properties by extracting key figures of merit (Figure 4) from these transfer curves (Figure 3). All devices were fabricated and tested by the same means at the same time, so variations in the figures of merit of each group ‘before’ the analyte are a result of random differences between devices.

Figure 4a,b shows the ‘ON’ current (or maximum Id) and the ‘OFF’ current (minimum Id), before and after deposition of the analyte. The ON current after deposition of the analyte is increased, on average, but the increase is often small compared to the standard deviations. OFF current increases more significantly and is strongly dependent on H2O2 concentration. The extracted threshold voltage (Figure 4c) also increases significantly upon deposition of H2O2, shifting in the positive direction. This reflects the increased doping of the channel, and hence the need for larger, positive gate voltages to reach a minimum Id (the OFF state).

Figure 4d shows the effect of H2O2 concentration on the product of saturation regime hole mobility (μsat) and capacitance (C). Where it is impractical to distinguish the two parameters by additional experimentation, the product can be a useful indicator of device properties and overall device performance. Significantly, in our experiment, μsat × C does not change appreciably at any H2O2 concentration. It seems unlikely that both parameters would change but happen to cancel out, so it is probable that neither μsat nor C change significantly. In the literature, different dopants for P3HT are variably reported to increase[23] or decrease[24] hole mobility. In our case, any such effect appears to be small. The mechanism of H2O2 sensing is also unlikely to be related to a change in device capacitance. Increased capacitance would be expected if the mechanism depended on some means to enhance the field effect between gate and channel. This could occur by an increase in ion concentration, or a modification of the gate electrode or dielectric that impacted the formation of electric double layers at the layer interfaces. Such mechanisms are therefore unlikely to play a significant role in the H2O2 sensing response in HIFETs. This supports the hypothesis that the P3HT layer is itself changing due to a chemical process, such as direct oxidation by H2O2.

Finally, Figure 4e,f shows the effect of H2O2 concentration on Ig, when in the ON state and OFF state, respectively. In the ON state, the negative Ig significantly increases in magnitude, and does so similarly across all analyte concentrations tested, including 0 wt% (deionized water). This reflects an increase in source-to-gate leakage current due to increased hydration of the PVP layer. Increased hydration may liberate more ions from the PVP and permit greater charge transfer between the channel and gate. By contrast, Ig recorded in the OFF state (Vg ≈ +0.8 V) increases significantly with increasing H2O2 concentration. In this state, gate-to-drain leakage is likely to predominate, along with a smaller contribution from gate-to-source leakage. Given the similar trend and magnitude, this gate leakage likely contributes significantly to the OFF current (Figure 4b). The source of this gate-to-drain leakage current is related to the H2O2 concentration. It is possible that this is a faradaic current resulting from the oxidation of H2O2 at the gate. At ≈0.8 V, the gate is the anode with respect to both source and drain, and thus electrochemical oxidation of H2O2 would likely occur. The electrons imparted to the gate electrode (see equation 1) would pass out of the device through the gate terminal and register as a positive current. The cations (H+) liberated from H2O2 at the same time may be responsible for conducting current to the drain. It should be noted that this would not be occurring to a significant extent under normal sensing conditions (Vg = −0.3 V) and so would not contribute to the standard Id response.

2.4 Effect of Gate and Drain voltages
Voltages applied to the HIFET have a significant role in determining the Id response to H2O2. Figure 5 shows representative transient responses to 30 wt% H2O2 under different gate voltages (while Vds = −1 V). There is a clear trend of decreasing Id modulation (Figure 5a) as Vg moves from positive (Vg = +0.3 V) to negative (Vg = −0.6 V). The largest modulations are achieved where the P3HT layer is initially less doped, and initial Id is lowest (Figure 5b).

Vg also impacts the Ig response to the analyte (Figure 5c). The net direction of the leakage current changes, from negative at −0.6 V to positive at +0.3 V. As Vg approaches Vds (−1 V), the component of Ig due to gate-to-drain leakage reduces, and the leakage becomes dominated by source-to-gate leakage. In theory, this may be ideal if the aim is to use Id to probe the doping of the channel, as it will minimize the contribution of Ig to Id. However, the much smaller modulation is a disadvantage, and may result in poorer device sensitivity and lower signal-to-background ratio. Another concern is the tendency of Id to decay over time rather than remain steady following the initial modulation, indicating a slow de-doping process. This problem grows with increasingly negative Vg and is most noticeable for Vg = −0.6 V (Figure 5).

Gate-to-drain leakage is maximized using Vg = +0.3 V, where we obtain the largest modulation (Figure 5). Under these conditions, with a small source-drain current, gate-to-drain leakage makes a proportionally significant contribution to the measured Id. This may contribute to the larger modulation ratio. Given that Ig at Vg ≈ +0.8 V is strongly dependent on H2O2 concentration (see Figure 4f), a positive Vg may improve overall sensitivity. On the other hand, Ig during sensing is often inconsistent between devices and highly sensitive to minor fabrication defects. Random Ig fluctuations can significantly impact Id and confound the measurement of concentration. Because of this irregularity when using positive gate voltages, and the decay in the Id over time when using large negative gate voltages, we have generally preferred to use a low, negative gate voltage (Vg = −0.3 V) as a good compromise between both extremes for sensing measurements.

We have also experimented with the effect of varied drain voltage to understand how Vg and Vds together impact the Id modulation. We recorded the Id modulation for different combinations of small (−0.3 V) and large (−1 V) gate and drain voltages, again using 30 wt% H2O2 as the analyte. Representative results are shown in Figure 6. A few general patterns can be observed. First, using either small or large Vds, varied Vg has the same effect as noted above: larger Vg produces smaller Id modulations, which decay more significantly over time. We also observe that larger Vds produces larger modulations, using either low or high Vg. Thus, optimum Id modulation is achieved with a combination of a large Vds and small Vg.

The decay in Id with time is worth considering more closely. It is more pronounced at Vg = −1 V compared with Vg = −0.6 V (Figure 6). Using the larger voltage, the decaying Id is also evident before deposition of the analyte (see inset in Figure 6b), but the analyte clearly enhances the effect. This suggests that, at a large negative Vg, the highly doped state of the channel is less stable and more sensitive to dynamic processes in the device that might modify the electric fields controlling the channel doping. Subtle modifications to the gate and PVP properties due to exposure to the oxidizing H2O2 analyte may therefore have a significant impact on Id. Closer to Vg = 0, the channel doping is less controlled by Vg and hence Id may be less sensitive to gate properties.

We observed in Figure 5 that larger modulations occurred when the initial Id was smaller, but this pattern does not hold here (see absolute Id in Figure 6b). Instead, we can identify an association between the size of the modulation and how negative the drain voltage is relative to the gate. A large Vds and small Vg both act to minimize the initial charge density of the channel (see Figure S2, Supporting Information). Cations in the PVP layer are drawn toward the P3HT above the negative drain, de-doping it. It could be an advantage to begin in a state of lowest possible channel doping. There is plausibly a point at which doping of the P3HT interface becomes saturated, so minimizing the initial doping state could effectively give the analyte more scope to operate.

The need to begin with low P3HT doping may help explain a previously observed association between the conductance (or “effectiveness”) of the gate electrode, and the size of the Id modulation in response to H2O2. Previously, we have observed large sensing modulations at Vds = Vg = −1 V, contrary to the very low modulation shown in Figure 6. In that case, we were using HIFETs with poorly conducting silica nanoparticle gate electrodes.[16] When the nanoparticles were combined with poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene) doped with poly(styrene sulfonate) (PEDOT:PSS) to improve conductance, HIFETs with greater gate conductivity and better overall device performance exhibited smaller Id sensing modulations at Vg = −1 V.[14] We have proposed that the relationship between gate conductance and device performance can be understood in terms of the “effective Vg,” which is smaller in magnitude than the applied Vg when gate conductance is poor.[25] Thus, using a poorly conductive gate electrode would have an advantageous effect similar to decreasing Vg, resulting in reduced initial doping of the P3HT layer and thus improving the sensitivity of the device to H2O2.

It is also important to consider the impact of voltages on the diffusion of ions inside the device. An earlier report on the role of Vg in the Id response found greater Id modulations in response to the analyte using Vg = −0.3 V compared with Vg = −1.0 V (Vds was maintained at −1.0 V),[26] which is in agreement with our results. The authors suggested that the increase in net electric field between gate and drain served to enhance protonic doping of the P3HT layer, as H+ generated by the electrochemical decomposition of H2O2 would be drawn toward the negatively polarized drain.[26] However, our earlier study of spatial charge distribution using confocal microscopy revealed that a negative drain voltage consistently suppressed doping of the P3HT.[18] For example, if H2O2 was deposited onto a HIFET while Vds = −1 V, charge density over the drain electrode would increase more slowly than over the source and in the channel. After the drain voltage was turned off, doping over the drain was able to increase. Conversely, if H2O2 was deposited while all voltages were off, charge density increased uniformly throughout the P3HT film, but once Vds was applied, charge density over the drain was quickly reduced, indicating a partial reversal of the doping accomplished by the H2O2. This behavior is difficult to reconcile with the earlier hypothesis that the doping process is dominated by protonation of the P3HT by H+ that is drawn toward the drain by electric fields. Our explanation of the observed de-doping effect was that, rather than protonating the P3HT, cations in the PVP accumulate at the interface and by the field effect deplete the P3HT of holes over the drain.

Additionally, our confocal experiment showed doping occurring with no voltage at drain or gate,[18] and hence without decomposition of H2O2. In Figure 6a, we also find that the second largest modulation occurs using low voltage on both drain and gate (Vds = Vg = −0.3 V). The half-reaction for the oxidation of H2O2 given in Equation 1 has a standard electrode potential of ≈0.7 V versus the saturated calomel electrode. Amperometry has revealed evidence of the onset of H2O2 decomposition around the same value.[15, 16] Thus, if the decomposition of H2O2 to H+ was necessary to the doping mechanism, we would not expect significant doping to take place while device voltages are below 0.7 V. Since doping clearly does take place at low voltages, it is unlikely that H2O2 breakdown plays a primary role in the sensing mechanism.

An important question that should be considered is whether the natural acidity of H2O2 is sufficient to provide H+ and thereby protonate the P3HT. To explore this question, we tested the HIFET response to pH buffer solutions of pH 4, 7, and 10, under standard voltages. Similar modulations were obtained using both pH 4 and 7 buffers (acidic and neutral), indicating a small doping effect little larger than the effect of deionized water, however a strong de-doping effect was observed using pH 10 (see Figure S3, Supporting Information). That the sensing modulation shows little difference between pH 4 and 7 suggests that HIFETs are not highly sensitive to H+ concentration in this range. We measure that 30 wt% H2O2 has a pH of ≈3.5, so without significant pH change occurring due to H2O2 oxidation, the H+ already present in the analyte is unlikely to contribute significantly to the observed H2O2 modulation. This indicates that the low voltage doping mechanism is unlikely to be simply due to protonation from the initial acidity of the H2O2 solution. Regarding the sensing response to the pH 10 buffer, the de-doping effect observed may indicate deprotonation of the P3HT. Given the acidity of the PVP layer and the presence of mobile H+ when hydrated, it is likely that some protonation of the P3HT at the interface with the PVP occurs during the device fabrication process. Thus, when excess OH− is introduced in the pH 10 buffer, deprotonation would occur and reduce charge density in the channel. The prior protonation of the P3HT due to the existing acidity of PVP may also help to explain the apparent insensitivity of the device to the acidic pH buffer. For future study, it would be instructive to analyze the HIFET response to stronger acids and other ions.

In summary, the changes that take place in HIFETs upon exposure to H2O2 are multifaceted and cannot be fully explained by a single, simple process. Nevertheless, we have gained important insights, with significant implications for our understanding of the primary mechanisms. There is clear evidence that the modulation in Id upon depositing H2O2 is a diffusion-controlled process leading to enhanced hole conductivity in the P3HT channel. This doping is reflected in a positive shift in estimated threshold voltage (VT) and an increase in both ON and OFF currents, but the product of mobility and capacitance (μsat × C) is largely unchanged. From this, we infer that the mechanism is likely unrelated to an enhancement of the field effect gating the channel, which can result from an increase in device capacitance or effective gate voltage. Instead, the results are consistent with a chemical change to the P3HT enhancing mobile charge density, and given the strongly oxidizing properties of H2O2, the direct oxidation of P3HT by the analyte is likely. Gate leakage currents are increased by the analyte, reflecting increased conductivity of the PVP layer. In the ‘ON’ state, this is independent of analyte concentration and is largely an effect of hydration. In the ‘OFF’ state, the Ig increases with analyte concentration and appears to be connected to the ‘OFF’ state drain current. We suggested this could be a Faradaic current related to the oxidation of H2O2 at the gate electrode, but as sensing is not usually performed in the ‘OFF’ state, this does not have direct bearing on the standing sensing mechanism. We have seen that the Id modulation is highly dependent on Vg and Vds, and that largest modulations occur when the channel is maximally de-doped.

We propose that when H2O2 solution is deposited onto the gate electrode of a HIFET (Figure 7a), H2O2 begins diffusing through the gate and PVP layer. Reaching the P3HT interface, the H2O2 strongly oxidizes the surface, increasing charge density and hence source-drain current (Figure 7b). At Vg = −0.3 V and Vds = −1 V, the device is in a stable, and relatively low gate leakage configuration that begins with the channel in a low or moderately doped condition, facilitating a strong, reliable sensing response.

Our proposed mechanism raises the question of whether the transistor structure is necessary or helpful in this application. That is, if we are only probing the change in conductivity in the P3HT layer due to direct oxidation by the analyte, the rest of the device could presumably be dispensed with. Our observations, however, indicate important advantages of the transistor structure for H2O2 sensing. Our experiments with P3HT-only samples show that it is very difficult to obtain reproducible, stable sensing responses (see Figure S4, Supporting Information). Both water and H2O2 result in large but unpredictable current modulations. The large response to water is explained by the moisture sensitivity of pristine P3HT,[27] and we suspect the variability results from the hydrophobic nature of the P3HT surface which prevents consistent wetting and interaction with the analyte in the channel area. It therefore seems necessary to use a hygroscopic interfacial layer like PVP to absorb the analyte and bring it into contact with the P3HT. The PVP also prewets and predopes the P3HT in a consistent fashion, thereby minimizing the response to water. Of course, alternative solutions could be to modify the P3HT surface or change the active layer material. However, we also find the gate has an important role to play. When we test P3HT/PVP devices without a gate electrode (Figure S5, Supporting Information), we obtain a similar, steady Id modulation, but the response is slower due to a process that initially de-dopes the device that appears to be related to hydration. Having a gate electrode means Id responds more rapidly to H2O2 upon contact between the analyte and gate. Other advantages to the transistor structure are the way the Id response can be tuned by device voltages, and a full transistor may be integrated as functional components in circuits while doubling as a sensing element. We would also like to note that HIFETs are suitable for sensing a range of different analytes, particularly salts and other ionic compounds, where the transistor structure will be more directly necessary. Our group is actively studying the sensitivity of HIFETs to other analytes.

3 Conclusion
We have conducted a detailed study of the effect of hydrogen peroxide on the properties of HIFETs. The primary effect, evident across a number of different measures, is the doping of the organic semiconductor layer, P3HT. Overall, the evidence is consistent with our hypothesis of oxidative doping of the P3HT directly by H2O2 upon contact at the P3HT interface, having diffused from the top gate electrode into the device. While this suggests that the H2O2 sensing mechanism does not take direct advantage of the inherent signal amplification properties of transistors, we have shown that the HIEFT structure is important for consistent, reliable sensing. The body of information represented here is highly significant for the understanding and future development of HIFET sensors.

HIFETs were fabricated using a previously established device structure and method.[7] Glass substrates prepatterned with indium tin oxide (ITO) source and drain electrodes (Xin Yan Technology Ltd.) were first cleaned by scrubbing with acetone and then with labware detergent (Alconox) using cotton tips, followed by ultrasonication in detergent solution, deionized water, acetone and isopropyl alcohol for 10 min each, in succession. The stack was annealed at 85 °C for 20 min.

The properties of the gate electrode and its deposition method have a significant impact on overall device performance.[7, 14, 25] In this study, PEDOT:PSS gate electrodes were used cross-linked using divinyl sulfone. It is previously shown that this approach to cross-linking gives more consistent results, with lower gate leakage current compared with another common cross-linker.[7] Cross-linked PEDOT:PSS gate electrodes were fabricated by combining, by volume, 1% divinyl sulfone (Sigma-Aldrich, V3700), 5.8% ethylene glycol, 0.1% 4-dodecylbenzenesulfonic acid (Sigma-Aldrich, 44198), and 93.1% PEDOT:PSS (Heraeus, Clevios PH 1000). Abstract
Hygroscopic insulator field effect transistors (HIFETs) are a class of solid-state, low-voltage organic thin film transistors with promising sensitivity to hydrogen peroxide (H2O2). While work has progressed on the development and understanding of HIFET-based sensors, important questions regarding the fundamental H2O2 sensing mechanisms need to be fully investigated. In this work, a detailed study of the effects of H2O2 on the transient drain currents and transfer characteristics of HIFETs, while varying analyte concentrations and device voltages, is presented. The sensing mechanism is found to be consistent with the direct oxidation of the organic semiconductor channel, poly(3-hexylthiophene-2,5-diyl), by H2O2 diffused into the device through the permeable top gate electrode and hygroscopic insulator. Further, the importance of having a transistor structure in sensing H2O2, as compared to a two-terminal device, is demonstrated. These results represent important progress in the understanding of HIFET-based sensors, revealing paths for future applications and optimization.

1 Introduction
Organic thin film transistors (OTFTs) have been developed for sensing a wide array of chemical substances and physical phenomena as diverse as DNA,[1] pressure,[2] humidity,[3] glucose,[4] dopamine,[5] pH,[6] and hydrogen peroxide (H2O2).[7] H2O2 is a small, unstable molecule with strong oxidizing properties, and its significance in different fields has made it a target for OTFT-based sensors.[8] Industrial applications of H2O2 include rocket propellant,[9] textile bleaching,[10] and sanitization of machinery for food preparation and packaging.[11] In medicine and biology, H2O2 is relevant for its cytotoxicity, hence its frequent use as a disinfectant.[12] Within organisms, it is generated by immune cells to aid the destruction of foreign microbes, and is used as a cellular signaling molecule.[8] Significantly, H2O2 is produced in enzyme-driven oxidation of biologically relevant molecules, including glucose by glucose oxidase and uric acid by urate oxidase.[13] As a result, devices sensitive to H2O2 can form the basis of a variety of medically relevant biosensors with the addition of a suitable enzyme as a recognition element.

The hygroscopic insulator field effect transistor (HIFET) is a class of OTFT which employs a hygroscopic insulating layer that can behave as a solid-state electrolyte upon absorbing moisture from the environment. The result is a simple, low-voltage, solution processable, and solid-state device, with intriguing potential for low-cost biosensing and biointerfacing applications. In oxidative enzymatic biosensors based on HIFETs, selectivity is achieved through the enzyme whereas sensitivity is achieved through sensing H2O2.15 HIFETs with promising sensitivity to H2O2 have been demonstrated.[7, 14-16] While a wide variety of H2O2 sensors exist,[17] the biocompatibility of organic materials, and the ability to create very thin, light, and flexible devices, makes OTFTs particularly suitable for biosensing applications. Due to their simple, solid-state architecture, without the need for separate reference electrodes or electrolyte containment, HIFETs are an appealing option. To realize the full potential of HIFETs in oxidative enzymatic biosensors, understanding and optimizing the H2O2 detection mechanism in HIFETs is necessary.

For HIFETs, the earliest proposed sensing mechanism was that H2O2 is oxidized at the gate electrode due to the applied voltage (above ≈0.7 V) as per the oxidation half-reaction given in equation 1, and the liberated protons (H+) then diffuse through the device and dope the poly(3-hexylthiophene) (P3HT) channel, which in turn modulates the current in the channel.[15] This mechanism fully explained our earlier investigations and reports on HIFET sensors[16]
   
 
(1)
In our recent investigation of H2O2 sensing mechanisms using confocal fluorescence microscopy, we found evidence for two other processes of doping the P3HT channel, which cannot be explained by protons doping the P3HT channel.[18] One process occurred uniformly throughout the P3HT layer in the absence of applied voltages. When a potential was applied between drain and source (Vds = −1 V), doping also occurred throughout the film, but was strengthened over the source electrode and weakened over the drain electrode. We hypothesized that the first process was direct oxidation of the P3HT layer by the concentrated H2O2 analyte, which had diffused into the device through the permeable gate electrode. The second, voltage-dependent process, was consistent with anodic decomposition of H2O2 generating charge carriers over the source electrode, and standard cation-driven de-doping over the drain electrode.[18] Our results suggest that direct oxidation of the active semiconductor layer by hydrogen peroxide plays a significant, and likely primary, role in our experiments.[18] However, this hypothesis requires further investigation and confirmation, and the exact role and contribution of the electrochemical decomposition of H2O2 needs to be explored.

In this report, we present a detailed analysis of the effect of H2O2 on HIFET characteristics, to gain deeper insight into the sensing mechanisms and evaluate our earlier proposal. Building upon previous work, we study the effect of H2O2 concentration, across four orders of magnitude, on the Id modulation and device figures of merit. We also examine the effect of different Vg and Vds values on the Id sensing modulation, to understand how electric fields in the device influence the sensing response. Considering these observations, together with insights from previous reports, we make an important advance toward understanding H2O2 sensing mechanisms in HIFETs.

2 Results and Discussion
2.1 Transistor Characteristics
The device structure for HIFETs used in this study is shown in Figure 1a and representative output and transfer characteristics for the HIFETs used in this study are given in Figure S1 (Supporting Information). A HIFET functions analogously to an electrolyte-gated OFET (EGOFET). The solid-state poly(4-vinylphenol) (PVP) insulating layer is weakly acidic and its phenol groups ionize when exposed to moisture. The aqueous mobile cations (H+) and fixed phenol anions facilitate the high capacitance of the device, and hence its low-voltage operation.[19] A negative gate voltage causes holes to accumulate in the P3HT channel. Figure 1a shows holes accumulated in the channel in the saturation regime, where the large Vds causes the formation of a depletion region near the drain electrode. As evident in Figure S1b (Supporting Information), HIFETs in this configuration exhibit a positive threshold voltage, owing to a p-type doping effect of PVP at the interface with the P3HT channel material. A positive voltage is therefore applied to accumulate cations at the interface, depleting the P3HT channel and achieving an ‘OFF’ state (Figure 1b). In HIFETs, the OFF current is generally larger than would be expected of equivalent OFETs, which may be attributed to gate-to-drain leakage current.

Figure 1c plots the gate-source voltage (Vg), drain current, (Id) and gate current (Ig) with time. The transient response of device currents to a change of gate voltage provides useful insight into fundamental HIFET behavior and functions as a useful “case study” to inform the analysis of the H2O2 sensing response that follows later in this report. In Figure 1c, the Vds is held at −1 V, but at t = 0 s Vg is stepped from 0 to +0.3, −0.3, −0.6 or −1.0 V. As expected, the device is already ‘ON’ at Vg = 0 V, and a low, negative Id is observed (holes entering drain). At the same time, there is a small, positive Ig. This indicates a leakage current through the PVP layer. In this steady-state condition, the measured current at all three device electrodes sum close to zero (Id + Is + Ig = 0), indicating that all current flowing in flows out, or is balanced. At Vg = 0 V, the current measured at the source electrode (Is) (not shown) is nearly identical to Id, but of opposite polarity. In magnitude, Id is larger than Is by approximately the size of Ig, indicating that the difference is due to the gate leakage current flowing from gate to drain.

When gate voltage is changed, Id and Ig respond rapidly, and eventually find new steady states. The long duration needed to reach a steady state, particularly for larger gate voltages, reflects the relatively slow ionic diffusion mechanism in the PVP layer that underlies the doping and de-doping of the channel. The Ig response is a sharp spike that decays, resembling the charging current of a capacitor. If a negative gate voltage is applied, cations in the PVP move toward the gate, holes are injected into the channel (Figure 1a), and correspondingly, holes are removed from the gate. The negative gate current spike thus likely reflects holes exiting the gate electrode through the gate terminal. The opposite occurs when a positive gate voltage is applied (Figure 1b). Bernards and Malliaras have described similar behavior with a transient model for OECTs.[20] They note that spike-and-recovery behavior in the gate current occurs when hole transport is slow compared with ion transport.[20] Interestingly, we observe that the sum Id + Is + Ig deviates significantly from 0 for a few seconds immediately following the voltage step. The cause of this is uncertain, but may point toward a phenomenon like charge trapping, where charge enters the device but does not exit.

2.2 H2O2 Sensitivity
To characterize the sensitivity of HIFETs to H2O2, we measured the transient Id response to 5 µL of H2O2 solution across a range of concentrations, from 30 wt% to 0.03 wt% (9.8 to 9.8 × 10−3 m). Electrode voltages, Vg and Vds, were held at −0.3 V and −1 V, respectively. Figure 2a shows representative Id response curves for each concentration, including the response to deionized water as a baseline or ‘control’ trial and Figure 2b shows the effect on Ig. The Id response is given as the percent change in Id: 100 × ΔId(t)/Id(t = 0). To quantitatively compare these responses, we plot the averages for maximum Id modulation (Figure 2c), as well as a parameter β, the ratio of total charge gained within 200 s of analyte deposition (Figure 2d) (see supporting information for details). Device response times, defined as the time taken to reach 95% of the maximum Id reached, are given in Figure 2e. The overall trend in Figure 2 indicates that the Id response is strongly dependent on concentration. At low concentrations, maximum Id modulations are poorly differentiated (Figure 2c), but the β parameter proves more sensitive (Figure 2d) as it captures the reduced overall charge due to the slower response time.

In Figure 2, Id modulations are larger and faster with increasing concentration. The increase in the magnitude of Id (in the negative direction) reflects an increase in hole current through the drain electrode, which superficially resembles the effect of applying a negative gate voltage step (Figure 1c). The increase in Id is not simply an effect of increased gate-to-drain leakage current, as a modulation of nearly identical magnitude is observed in Is (indicating the modulated Id is originating from the source and flowing through the channel) and the measured gate current is orders of magnitude smaller. Therefore, the modulation can be considered a result of the doping of the P3HT channel, arising from the presence of H2O2. The variation in response times is indicative of a diffusion-dominated doping process. This behavior in HIFET sensors has been reported before,[16] and is consistent with Fick's laws of diffusion, which describe increased diffusion rate with larger concentration gradients.[21]

The behavior of the gate current, shown in Figure 2b, is also worth considering. Before introducing the analyte, Ig is usually very small and positive, though in a few cases a larger, negative Ig is recorded. The total gate leakage current is the net sum of a negative source-to-gate leakage and a positive gate-to-drain leakage. Gate leakage is often modeled as resulting from defects in the dielectric layer.[22] In that case, random variation in the distribution of such defects may alter the magnitude of either leakage component, and hence the direction of the net flow of charge. Regardless of its starting value, once the analyte is deposited, Ig consistently shifts in the negative direction. Negative gate current indicates a net flow of holes out of (or electrons into) the HIFET through the gate terminal. Initially, this may reflect a capacitive effect to maintain charge neutrality, where holes exit the gate to compensate the increase in holes in the channel, resembling the behavior seen in Figure 2 upon stepping to a negative gate voltage. However, the continuous, elevated Ig in the sensing response indicates an additional effect. The trajectory of Ig following sensing is erratic and varies considerably between individual devices. A similar behavior is seen with water and all concentrations of H2O2, suggesting that the elevated Ig is, in large part, an effect of the hydration of the device and an increase in PVP conductivity.

2.3 Effect of H2O2 on Figures of Merit
HIFET characteristics change appreciably after deposition of the analyte. This is illustrated in Figure 3, where we show representative transfer characteristics before and after deposition of deionized water, 0.3 and 30 wt% H2O2. Deionized water usually decreases the magnitude of Id throughout the Vg sweep (Figure 3a), whereas H2O2 typically results in an increase in Id (Figure 3a,b). We gain further insight into the effect of H2O2 on HIFET device properties by extracting key figures of merit (Figure 4) from these transfer curves (Figure 3). All devices were fabricated and tested by the same means at the same time, so variations in the figures of merit of each group ‘before’ the analyte are a result of random differences between devices.

The ON current after deposition of the analyte is increased, on average, but the increase is often small compared to the standard deviations. OFF current increases more significantly and is strongly dependent on H2O2 concentration. The extracted threshold voltage (Figure 4c) also increases significantly upon deposition of H2O2, shifting in the positive direction. This reflects the increased doping of the channel, and hence the need for larger, positive gate voltages to reach a minimum Id (the OFF state).

Figure 4d shows the effect of H2O2 concentration on the product of saturation regime hole mobility (μsat) and capacitance (C). Where it is impractical to distinguish the two parameters by additional experimentation, the product can be a useful indicator of device properties and overall device performance. Significantly, in our experiment, μsat × C does not change appreciably at any H2O2 concentration. It seems unlikely that both parameters would change but happen to cancel out, so it is probable that neither μsat nor C change significantly. In the literature, different dopants for P3HT are variably reported to increase[23] or decrease[24] hole mobility. In our case, any such effect appears to be small. The mechanism of H2O2 sensing is also unlikely to be related to a change in device capacitance. Increased capacitance would be expected if the mechanism depended on some means to enhance the field effect between gate and channel. This could occur by an increase in ion concentration, or a modification of the gate electrode or dielectric that impacted the formation of electric double layers at the layer interfaces. Such mechanisms are therefore unlikely to play a significant role in the H2O2 sensing response in HIFETs. This supports the hypothesis that the P3HT layer is itself changing due to a chemical process, such as direct oxidation by H2O2.

Finally, Figure 4e,f shows the effect of H2O2 concentration on Ig, when in the ON state and OFF state, respectively. In the ON state, the negative Ig significantly increases in magnitude, and does so similarly across all analyte concentrations tested, including 0 wt% (deionized water). This reflects an increase in source-to-gate leakage current due to increased hydration of the PVP layer. Increased hydration may liberate more ions from the PVP and permit greater charge transfer between the channel and gate. By contrast, Ig recorded in the OFF state (Vg ≈ +0.8 V) increases significantly with increasing H2O2 concentration. In this state, gate-to-drain leakage is likely to predominate, along with a smaller contribution from gate-to-source leakage. Given the similar trend and magnitude, this gate leakage likely contributes significantly to the OFF current (Figure 4b). The source of this gate-to-drain leakage current is related to the H2O2 concentration. It is possible that this is a faradaic current resulting from the oxidation of H2O2 at the gate. At ≈0.8 V, the gate is the anode with respect to both source and drain, and thus electrochemical oxidation of H2O2 would likely occur. The electrons imparted to the gate electrode (see equation 1) would pass out of the device through the gate terminal and register as a positive current. The cations (H+) liberated from H2O2 at the same time may be responsible for conducting current to the drain. It should be noted that this would not be occurring to a significant extent under normal sensing conditions (Vg = −0.3 V) and so would not contribute to the standard Id response.

2.4 Effect of Gate and Drain voltages
Voltages applied to the HIFET have a significant role in determining the Id response to H2O2. Figure 5 shows representative transient responses to 30 wt% H2O2 under different gate voltages (while Vds = −1 V). There is a clear trend of decreasing Id modulation (Figure 5a) as Vg moves from positive (Vg = +0.3 V) to negative (Vg = −0.6 V). The largest modulations are achieved where the P3HT layer is initially less doped, and initial Id is lowest (Figure 5b).

Vg also impacts the Ig response to the analyte (Figure 5c). The net direction of the leakage current changes, from negative at −0.6 V to positive at +0.3 V. As Vg approaches Vds (−1 V), the component of Ig due to gate-to-drain leakage reduces, and the leakage becomes dominated by source-to-gate leakage. In theory, this may be ideal if the aim is to use Id to probe the doping of the channel, as it will minimize the contribution of Ig to Id. However, the much smaller modulation is a disadvantage, and may result in poorer device sensitivity and lower signal-to-background ratio. Another concern is the tendency of Id to decay over time rather than remain steady following the initial modulation, indicating a slow de-doping process. This problem grows with increasingly negative Vg and is most noticeable for Vg = −0.6 V (Figure 5).

Gate-to-drain leakage is maximized using Vg = +0.3 V, where we obtain the largest modulation (Figure 5). Under these conditions, with a small source-drain current, gate-to-drain leakage makes a proportionally significant contribution to the measured Id. This may contribute to the larger modulation ratio. Given that Ig at Vg ≈ +0.8 V is strongly dependent on H2O2 concentration (see Figure 4f), a positive Vg may improve overall sensitivity. On the other hand, Ig during sensing is often inconsistent between devices and highly sensitive to minor fabrication defects. Random Ig fluctuations can significantly impact Id and confound the measurement of concentration. Because of this irregularity when using positive gate voltages, and the decay in the Id over time when using large negative gate voltages, we have generally preferred to use a low, negative gate voltage (Vg = −0.3 V) as a good compromise between both extremes for sensing measurements.

We have also experimented with the effect of varied drain voltage to understand how Vg and Vds together impact the Id modulation. We recorded the Id modulation for different combinations of small (−0.3 V) and large (−1 V) gate and drain voltages, again using 30 wt% H2O2 as the analyte. Representative results are shown in Figure 6. A few general patterns can be observed. First, using either small or large Vds, varied Vg has the same effect as noted above: larger Vg produces smaller Id modulations, which decay more significantly over time. We also observe that larger Vds produces larger modulations, using either low or high Vg. Thus, optimum Id modulation is achieved with a combination of a large Vds and small Vg.

The decay in Id with time is worth considering more closely. It is more pronounced at Vg = −1 V compared with Vg = −0.6 V (Figure 6). Using the larger voltage, the decaying Id is also evident before deposition of the analyte (see inset in Figure 6b), but the analyte clearly enhances the effect. This suggests that, at a large negative Vg, the highly doped state of the channel is less stable and more sensitive to dynamic processes in the device that might modify the electric fields controlling the channel doping. Subtle modifications to the gate and PVP properties due to exposure to the oxidizing H2O2 analyte may therefore have a significant impact on Id. Closer to Vg = 0, the channel doping is less controlled by Vg and hence Id may be less sensitive to gate properties.

We observed in Figure 5 that larger modulations occurred when the initial Id was smaller, but this pattern does not hold here (see absolute Id in Figure 6b). Instead, we can identify an association between the size of the modulation and how negative the drain voltage is relative to the gate. A large Vds and small Vg both act to minimize the initial charge density of the channel (see Figure S2, Supporting Information). Cations in the PVP layer are drawn toward the P3HT above the negative drain, de-doping it. It could be an advantage to begin in a state of lowest possible channel doping. There is plausibly a point at which doping of the P3HT interface becomes saturated, so minimizing the initial doping state could effectively give the analyte more scope to operate.

The need to begin with low P3HT doping may help explain a previously observed association between the conductance (or “effectiveness”) of the gate electrode, and the size of the Id modulation in response to H2O2. Previously, we have observed large sensing modulations at Vds = Vg = −1 V, contrary to the very low modulation shown in Figure 6. In that case, we were using HIFETs with poorly conducting silica nanoparticle gate electrodes.[16] When the nanoparticles were combined with poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene) doped with poly(styrene sulfonate) (PEDOT:PSS) to improve conductance, HIFETs with greater gate conductivity and better overall device performance exhibited smaller Id sensing modulations at Vg = −1 V.[14] We have proposed that the relationship between gate conductance and device performance can be understood in terms of the “effective Vg,” which is smaller in magnitude than the applied Vg when gate conductance is poor.[25] Thus, using a poorly conductive gate electrode would have an advantageous effect similar to decreasing Vg, resulting in reduced initial doping of the P3HT layer and thus improving the sensitivity of the device to H2O2.

It is also important to consider the impact of voltages on the diffusion of ions inside the device. An earlier report on the role of Vg in the Id response found greater Id modulations in response to the analyte using Vg = −0.3 V compared with Vg = −1.0 V (Vds was maintained at −1.0 V),[26] which is in agreement with our results. The authors suggested that the increase in net electric field between gate and drain served to enhance protonic doping of the P3HT layer, as H+ generated by the electrochemical decomposition of H2O2 would be drawn toward the negatively polarized drain.[26] However, our earlier study of spatial charge distribution using confocal microscopy revealed that a negative drain voltage consistently suppressed doping of the P3HT.[18] For example, if H2O2 was deposited onto a HIFET while Vds = −1 V, charge density over the drain electrode would increase more slowly than over the source and in the channel. After the drain voltage was turned off, doping over the drain was able to increase. Conversely, if H2O2 was deposited while all voltages were off, charge density increased uniformly throughout the P3HT film, but once Vds was applied, charge density over the drain was quickly reduced, indicating a partial reversal of the doping accomplished by the H2O2. This behavior is difficult to reconcile with the earlier hypothesis that the doping process is dominated by protonation of the P3HT by H+ that is drawn toward the drain by electric fields. Our explanation of the observed de-doping effect was that, rather than protonating the P3HT, cations in the PVP accumulate at the interface and by the field effect deplete the P3HT of holes over the drain.

Additionally, our confocal experiment showed doping occurring with no voltage at drain or gate,[18] and hence without decomposition of H2O2. In Figure 6a, we also find that the second largest modulation occurs using low voltage on both drain and gate (Vds = Vg = −0.3 V). The half-reaction for the oxidation of H2O2 given in Equation 1 has a standard electrode potential of ≈0.7 V versus the saturated calomel electrode. Amperometry has revealed evidence of the onset of H2O2 decomposition around the same value.[15, 16] Thus, if the decomposition of H2O2 to H+ was necessary to the doping mechanism, we would not expect significant doping to take place while device voltages are below 0.7 V. Since doping clearly does take place at low voltages, it is unlikely that H2O2 breakdown plays a primary role in the sensing mechanism.

An important question that should be considered is whether the natural acidity of H2O2 is sufficient to provide H+ and thereby protonate the P3HT. To explore this question, we tested the HIFET response to pH buffer solutions of pH 4, 7, and 10, under standard voltages. Similar modulations were obtained using both pH 4 and 7 buffers (acidic and neutral), indicating a small doping effect little larger than the effect of deionized water, however a strong de-doping effect was observed using pH 10 (see Figure S3, Supporting Information). That the sensing modulation shows little difference between pH 4 and 7 suggests that HIFETs are not highly sensitive to H+ concentration in this range. We measure that 30 wt% H2O2 has a pH of ≈3.5, so without significant pH change occurring due to H2O2 oxidation, the H+ already present in the analyte is unlikely to contribute significantly to the observed H2O2 modulation. This indicates that the low voltage doping mechanism is unlikely to be simply due to protonation from the initial acidity of the H2O2 solution. Regarding the sensing response to the pH 10 buffer, the de-doping effect observed may indicate deprotonation of the P3HT. Given the acidity of the PVP layer and the presence of mobile H+ when hydrated, it is likely that some protonation of the P3HT at the interface with the PVP occurs during the device fabrication process. Thus, when excess OH− is introduced in the pH 10 buffer, deprotonation would occur and reduce charge density in the channel. The prior protonation of the P3HT due to the existing acidity of PVP may also help to explain the apparent insensitivity of the device to the acidic pH buffer. For future study, it would be instructive to analyze the HIFET response to stronger acids and other ions.

In summary, the changes that take place in HIFETs upon exposure to H2O2 are multifaceted and cannot be fully explained by a single, simple process. Nevertheless, we have gained important insights, with significant implications for our understanding of the primary mechanisms. There is clear evidence that the modulation in Id upon depositing H2O2 is a diffusion-controlled process leading to enhanced hole conductivity in the P3HT channel. This doping is reflected in a positive shift in estimated threshold voltage (VT) and an increase in both ON and OFF currents, but the product of mobility and capacitance (μsat × C) is largely unchanged. From this, we infer that the mechanism is likely unrelated to an enhancement of the field effect gating the channel, which can result from an increase in device capacitance or effective gate voltage. Instead, the results are consistent with a chemical change to the P3HT enhancing mobile charge density, and given the strongly oxidizing properties of H2O2, the direct oxidation of P3HT by the analyte is likely. Gate leakage currents are increased by the analyte, reflecting increased conductivity of the PVP layer. In the ‘ON’ state, this is independent of analyte concentration and is largely an effect of hydration. In the ‘OFF’ state, the Ig increases with analyte concentration and appears to be connected to the ‘OFF’ state drain current. We suggested this could be a Faradaic current related to the oxidation of H2O2 at the gate electrode, but as sensing is not usually performed in the ‘OFF’ state, this does not have direct bearing on the standing sensing mechanism. We have seen that the Id modulation is highly dependent on Vg and Vds, and that largest modulations occur when the channel is maximally de-doped.

We propose that when H2O2 solution is deposited onto the gate electrode of a HIFET (Figure 7a), H2O2 begins diffusing through the gate and PVP layer. Reaching the P3HT interface, the H2O2 strongly oxidizes the surface, increasing charge density and hence source-drain current (Figure 7b). At Vg = −0.3 V and Vds = −1 V, the device is in a stable, and relatively low gate leakage configuration that begins with the channel in a low or moderately doped condition, facilitating a strong, reliable sensing response.

Our proposed mechanism raises the question of whether the transistor structure is necessary or helpful in this application. That is, if we are only probing the change in conductivity in the P3HT layer due to direct oxidation by the analyte, the rest of the device could presumably be dispensed with. Our observations, however, indicate important advantages of the transistor structure for H2O2 sensing. Our experiments with P3HT-only samples show that it is very difficult to obtain reproducible, stable sensing responses (see Figure S4, Supporting Information). Both water and H2O2 result in large but unpredictable current modulations. The large response to water is explained by the moisture sensitivity of pristine P3HT,[27] and we suspect the variability results from the hydrophobic nature of the P3HT surface which prevents consistent wetting and interaction with the analyte in the channel area. It therefore seems necessary to use a hygroscopic interfacial layer like PVP to absorb the analyte and bring it into contact with the P3HT. The PVP also prewets and predopes the P3HT in a consistent fashion, thereby minimizing the response to water. Of course, alternative solutions could be to modify the P3HT surface or change the active layer material. However, we also find the gate has an important role to play. When we test P3HT/PVP devices without a gate electrode (Figure S5, Supporting Information), we obtain a similar, steady Id modulation, but the response is slower due to a process that initially de-dopes the device that appears to be related to hydration. Having a gate electrode means Id responds more rapidly to H2O2 upon contact between the analyte and gate. Other advantages to the transistor structure are the way the Id response can be tuned by device voltages, and a full transistor may be integrated as functional components in circuits while doubling as a sensing element. We would also like to note that HIFETs are suitable for sensing a range of different analytes, particularly salts and other ionic compounds, where the transistor structure will be more directly necessary. Our group is actively studying the sensitivity of HIFETs to other analytes.

3 Conclusion
We have conducted a detailed study of the effect of hydrogen peroxide on the properties of HIFETs. The primary effect, evident across a number of different measures, is the doping of the organic semiconductor layer, P3HT. Overall, the evidence is consistent with our hypothesis of oxidative doping of the P3HT directly by H2O2 upon contact at the P3HT interface, having diffused from the top gate electrode into the device. While this suggests that the H2O2 sensing mechanism does not take direct advantage of the inherent signal amplification properties of transistors, we have shown that the HIEFT structure is important for consistent, reliable sensing. The body of information represented here is highly significant for the understanding and future development of HIFET sensors.

4 Experimental Section
HIFETs were fabricated using a previously established device structure and method.[7] Glass substrates prepatterned with indium tin oxide (ITO) source and drain electrodes (Xin Yan Technology Ltd.) were first cleaned by scrubbing with acetone and then with labware detergent (Alconox) using cotton tips, followed by ultrasonication in detergent solution, deionized water, acetone and isopropyl alcohol for 10 min each, in succession. A ≈50 nm film of P3HT (Rieke Metals, LLC, RMI-001EE) was deposited by spin coating from a 13 mg mL−1 solution in chloroform and chlorobenzene (1:1 mixture). The P3HT film was annealed at 60 °C for 10 min. A ≈700 nm film of PVP (Sigma-Aldrich, 436224) was then spin coated from an 80 mg mL−1 solution in ethanol. The stack was annealed at 85 °C for 20 min.

The properties of the gate electrode and its deposition method have a significant impact on overall device performance.[7, 14, 25] In this study, PEDOT:PSS gate electrodes were used cross-linked using divinyl sulfone. It is previously shown that this approach to cross-linking gives more consistent results, with lower gate leakage current compared with another common cross-linker.[7] Cross-linked PEDOT:PSS gate electrodes were fabricated by combining, by volume, 1% divinyl sulfone (Sigma-Aldrich, V3700), 5.8% ethylene glycol, 0.1% 4-dodecylbenzenesulfonic acid (Sigma-Aldrich, 44198), and 93.1% PEDOT:PSS (Heraeus, Clevios PH 1000). The solution was sonicated for 1 min to mix, deposited into wells machined into PTFE (8 µL per well), and heated at 70 °C for 30 min to dry. The freestanding films were removed from the PTFE wells and attached to HIFETs using 2 µL of 0.1% (v/v) (3-glycidyloxypropyl)trimethoxysilane (GOPS) in deionized water.

To ensure an optimal state of hydration, 8 µL of deionized water was deposited onto each HIFET and allowed to stand in ambient conditions for ≈2 h prior to the start of testing. Electrical characterization was performed using a Keysight B1500A semiconductor analyzer. Transfer characteristics were measured using a sweep rate of ≈70 mV s−1 and were recorded before and after recording the sensing response. Figures of merit were extracted from the reverse transfer sweep, using standard methods (described in the supporting information).
