Interface Engineering of Metal-Oxide Field-Effect Transistors for Low-Drift pH Sensing
Huihui Ren, Kun Liang, Dingwei Li, Momo Zhao, Fanfan Li, Hong Wang, Xiaohe Miao, Taofei Zhou, Liaoyong Wen, Qiyang Lu, Bowen Zhu
First published: 17 September 2021 https://doi.org/10.1002/admi.202100314Citations: 14
SectionsPDFTools Share
Abstract
Field-effect transistors (FETs) with nanoscale channels have emerged as excellent platforms for constructing high-sensitivity biosensors. However, in typical FET-based biosensors, the electronic perturbation at channel/electrolyte interfaces results in poor stability and severe signal drifts, making long-term continuous measurements quite challenging. To address this issue, here the dielectric/semiconductor interface is tailored by depositing an ultrathin layer of Al2O3 (≈7 nm in thickness) on In2O3 FETs via solution-based processes. The results show that the Al2O3 dielectric layers effectively passivate the In2O3 channel and endow the device with low operation voltage (0.05 V), high sensitivity (61.9 mV pH−1), and low current drift (0.35 mV h−1) upon long-term and continuous pH monitoring. This work paves the way to real-time biosensing with high sensitivity, excellent stability, and low power consumption.

1 Introduction
Field-effect transistor-based biosensors (bio-FETs) are promising for a wide range of applications owing to their advantages including intrinsic signal amplification, label-free detection, ease of miniaturization, and compatibility to complementary metal oxide semiconductor (CMOS) technology.[1-4] The sensing response of bio-FETs relies on the selective binding of analytes (e.g., H+, metal ions, and biomolecules) onto the channel surface, which results in the alternations in channel conductance and electrical signals.[5-7] Therefore, semiconductor nanomaterials susceptive to surface potential changes are excellent channel materials for highly sensitive bio-FETs.[8-11] However, in contrary to conventional FETs, the nanoscale channels in bio-FETs are often exposed to the electrolytes, in which the interference of background ions in the electrolyte will disturb sensing signals, leading to high leakage current and eventually poor stability of the device over long-term measurements.[12, 13] To date, this still represents one major challenge for bio-FETs to realize continuous biosensing with low-power dissipation and long-term stability.[14, 15]

Tailoring device configurations is an effective strategy to optimize the stability of bio-FETs.[16-18] To this end, different device structures have been developed to prevent the direct exposure of liquids to the active channel materials. For example, extended gate and floating gate structures have been successfully applied to separate channels from wet electrolytes in bio-FETs.[19, 20] Nevertheless, these strategies require complicated fabrication processes including encapsulation and patterning.[21, 22] On the other hand, it has been well-documented that rational interface engineering could endow FETs with significantly improved performance. For instance, with a thin high-k dielectric layer on the surface of nanostructured channel materials, both electron mobility and device stability are enhanced.[23-26] However, these dielectric/semiconductor interfaces were usually constructed from capital-intensive vacuum-based fabrication and patterning processes, which still suffer from processing complexity, device-to-device variation, and high cost.[27-30] With regard to their emerging applications, it is highly demanded, yet still remains a great challenge, to develop facile and effective strategies to configure the interface between dielectric and semiconductor and achieve high-performance bio-FETs.

Here, we fabricated high-performance bio-FETs with rationally engineered dielectric/semiconductor interfaces of Al2O3/In2O3 from low-cost solution-based processes. Metal oxide semiconductors (e.g., In2O3, IGZO) based bio-FETs usually exhibit high electrical performance and flexibility in composition control, yet insufficient stability in liquids with high operation voltage and large leakage current.[31-35] These drawbacks could be effectively alleviated by the formation of Al2O3/In2O3 interfaces demonstrated in this work, achieving stable, continuous pH sensing with low signal drift. First, ultrathin Al2O3 film behaves as an effective passivation layer to improve the electrical performance of In2O3 FETs.[23] Second, high-k Al2O3 dielectric offers high sensitivity for pH sensing, as widely demonstrated in typical silicon-based ion-sensitive FETs (ISFETs).[36-38] Finally, Al2O3 film provides effective encapsulation for In2O3 channel underneath, which suppresses leakage current and circumventing ions diffusion and possible damage into semiconducting layers. As a result, both electron mobility and signal drift rate of the Al2O3/In2O3 interfaces-based FETs (0.80 cm2 V–1 s–1 and 0.35 mV h–1) have been improved, in comparison with those of bare In2O3 FETs (0.22 cm2 V–1 s–1 and 12.1 mV h–1). Notably, the Al2O3/In2O3 based bio-FET also exhibited low operation voltage (Vds = 0.05 V) and a high sensitivity of 61.9 mV pH–1 in the pH range from 3 to 10. Furthermore, we integrated the Al2O3/In2O3 bio-FETs with microfluidic systems to deliver a lab-on-a-chip biosensor, which enabled low-drift and continuous pH monitoring over long-term measurements. The strategy presented in this work is promising for scalable and cost-efficient bioelectronics applications.

2 Results and Discussion
2.1 Characterizations on Oxides Thin Films
The Al2O3 dielectric layer was coated onto the ultrathin In2O3 channel by sequential spin-coating and annealing (300 °C in air) processes. Prior to each spin-coating step, substrates were exposed in ultraviolet–ozone (UV–O3) treatment to clean the surface and lower interface defect concentration.[39] Figure 1a presents the cross-sectional transmission electron microscope (TEM) image of Al2O3/In2O3 interfaces fabricated on Si substrate with SiO2 dielectric layer (Si/SiO2). The layered structures of In2O3 and Al2O3 can be clearly observed. Both films were thin and flat, indicating the feasibility of solution-processes in delivering uniform metal oxide thin films. As shown in Figure 1b, the material composition in the layered structure was further characterized by energy-dispersed X-ray spectra (EDS). Figure 1c illustrates a high-resolution TEM (HRTEM) image of the Al2O3/In2O3 interfaces, where the thicknesses of In2O3 and Al2O3 films can be determined as ≈6 and ≈7 nm, respectively. The In2O3 film depicted crystallized structure with a lattice spacing of 0.29 nm, corresponding to the (222) crystal plane of In2O3, while Al2O3 film was amorphous. Corresponding fast Fourier transform (FFT) analysis further confirmed the nanocrystalline and amorphous nature of In2O3 and Al2O3 films, respectively (Figure S1, Supporting Information).

Furthermore, X-ray diffraction (XRD) analysis was carried out to investigate the layered structure. As shown in Figure 1d, the cubic In2O3 films dominantly exposed the (222) crystalline plane, which is in good agreement with the TEM results. The peaks were broadened due to the ultrathin nature (≈6 nm) of the films.[40] No characteristic diffraction peak was observed in Al2O3 film, indicating its amorphous nature. The as-prepared In2O3 film possessed highly flat surfaces with root-mean-square (RMS) roughness of only 0.4 nm, as shown in the atomic force microscopy (AFM) image (Figure 1e). After Al2O3 deposition, the Al2O3/In2O3 films exhibited a further reduced surface RMS roughness of only 0.3 nm (Figure 1f). The above characterization results indicated successful fabrication of high-quality semiconductor/dielectric interfaces on In2O3, which is desirable to achieve FETs with improved carrier transport and stability.[41]

2.2 FET Device Characterization
We fabricated In2O3 based FETs with bottom-gate, top-contact (BGTC) configurations on highly doped Si (p++) substrate with SiO2 gate dielectric layer. For FET operation in dry states, Si (p++) substrate acted as the gate electrode. The In2O3 channels were patterned via a direct light patterning (DLP) technique reported previously.[31] Source/drain (S/D) electrodes were deposited by thermal evaporation of Ni/Au electrodes to construct In2O3 FETs. Subsequently, the Al2O3 precursor solution was spin-coated on In2O3 FETs and annealed to form Al2O3/In2O3 interfaces. A schematic showing the structure of device with S/D electrodes (channel width/length = 1000/100 µm) is illustrated in Figure 2a. The transfer curves (Ids–Vgs, solid lines) of In2O3 FETs with and without Al2O3 passivation layer are presented in Figure 2b. The bare In2O3 FET showed a saturation mobility (μsat) of 0.22 cm2 V–1 s–1, a subthreshold swing (SS) of 3.7 V dec–1, and a large current on/off ratio (Ion/off) of 1.0 × 105. After passivation with Al2O3 layer, the Al2O3/In2O3 based device exhibited an enhanced μsat of 0.80 cm2 V–1 s–1, an improved SS of 2.9 V dec–1, and a larger Ion/off of 1.0 × 106 with a higher on current. The subthreshold currents of In2O3 and In2O3/Al2O3 based FETs in dry states (Si gate) were <0.62 and <3.1 µA, respectively. The leakage currents (Igs, open circles, Figure 2b) of In2O3 and Al2O3/In2O3 FETs were both below 10–9 A. The right axis in Figure 2b shows the square root plot of Ids (solid circles), and the threshold voltage (Vth) values were extracted of 19.3 and 7.1 V for bare In2O3 and Al2O3/In2O3 based FETs, respectively.

These discrepancies can be attributed to the UV–O3 treatment along with the effective passivation of ultrathin Al2O3 layer on In2O3 film.[39] The UV–O3 treatment can reduce interfacial defects by introducing hydroxyl groups that can act as electron donor to enable more free electrons.[33] On the other hand, Al2O3 passivation layer on the back channel can greatly increase the carrier concentration and enhance the channel conductivity, leading to a negatively shifted Vth and an increased mobility.[42] The output characteristics of Al2O3/In2O3 interfaces based FETs are illustrated in Figure 2c, showing clear pinch-off behaviors.

Significantly, the ultrathin Al2O3 film can provide a high-k dielectric layer that is highly sensitive to H+ and OH– concentrations, which is desirable to realize high-performance pH sensors.[43-45] Figure 2d demonstrates the device structure of the Al2O3/In2O3 bio-FET in liquid environment. The ≈6-nm-thick In2O3 was spin-coated on Si/SiO2 substrate to serve as active channel. To increase sensitive area, interdigitated electrodes with channel width/length (W/L) = 4640/200 µm were applied. After depositing S/D electrodes, the ≈7-nm-thick Al2O3 layer was spin-coated on In2O3 to generate a passivation and pH sensing layer. Prior to electrical measurements in the liquid environment, the surface of Al2O3 film was modified by a layer of (3-aminopropyl)triethoxysilane (APTES) to achieve more stable and linear pH sensitivity.[10] In a typical static measurement, a poly(dimethylsiloxane) (PDMS) reservoir (size of 0.5 × 0.5 cm2) was attached on the device surface to confine analyte solution. A commercially available Ag/AgCl reference electrode was used as the gate electrode to enable FET operation in liquid environment.

Figure 2e shows the transfer curves (Ids–Vgs) of In2O3 FETs with/without Al2O3 passivation layer measured in 1.0× phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) buffer solution (pH = 7.4) in both logarithmic (left axis, solid lines) and linear scale (right axis, solid circles). Both devices can operate effectively in electrolyte solution with low operation voltages. Similar to characteristics in dry states, the Al2O3/In2O3 FET showed improved electrical performance with higher mobility (9.5 cm2 V−1 s−1), lower SS (64.5 mV dec−1), and larger Ion/off (2.0 × 103), in comparison with these of bare In2O3 FET (8.6 cm2 V−1 s−1, 70.0 mV dec−1, and 4.0 × 102, respectively). The Vth values of 0.28 and 0.16 V were extracted for In2O3 and Al2O3/In2O3 based FETs with Ag/AgCl gate electrodes, respectively (Figure 2e). The subthreshold currents of In2O3 and In2O3/Al2O3 based bio-FETs in liquid environments were <10 and <6.1 µA, respectively. Besides, Al2O3/In2O3 FET showed lower leakage current (Igs, open circles) than that of In2O3 FET, as the passivation of Al2O3 could effectively suppress leakage currents between In2O3 channel and the electrolyte. The comparison of the electrical characteristics between In2O3 and In2O3/Al2O3 FETs in dry and liquid environments was shown in Table S1 (Supporting Information). Figure 2f depicts the output characteristics of the liquid gated Al2O3/In2O3 FET. The Al2O3/In2O3 FET showed outstanding low-voltage saturation with stable output currents, which are important for biosensing applications that require low-operation voltages and high stability.

2.3 High-Performance pH Sensing
Figure 3a shows the model illustrating the pH-induced surface charge state changes of the APTES-modified Al2O3/In2O3 bio-FETs. The pH value change in electrolyte solution induced the deprotonation or protonation of terminating groups (hydroxyl groups or amino groups) on Al2O3 surface, which alternated the potential at the solid/liquid interface and led to changes in channel conductance.[12] Specifically, solution with high pH values would cause deprotonation on Al2O3 surface which depleted the electrons in the In2O3 channel, resulting in reduced conductance and positively shifted Vth, and vice versa. Figure 3b shows the transfer characteristics (Ids–VAg/AgCl) of the liquid-gated Al2O3/In2O3 bio-FET at various pH values from 3 to 10 with a drain bias (Vds) fixed at 0.05 V. The device exhibited stable electrical performance with distinctive Vth shifts at different pH values. As a result, the Al2O3/In2O3 device provided a linear dependence between Vth and pH values with a sensitivity of 61.9 mV pH−1 and a linearity of 99.7% (Figure 3c). Remarkably, no obvious SS variation was observed (Figure S2, Supporting Information), because the Al2O3 layer behaved as effective barrier to prevent ion diffusion and water diffusion to the underneath In2O3 channel layers. Furthermore, the liquid-gated Al2O3/In2O3 bio-FET showed fast saturation and stable output characteristics with distinctive pinch-off behaviors at different pH values ranging from 3 to 10. As illustrated in Figure 3d, the output behaviors can be effectively configured with different pH values, where the saturation currents increased gradually with the decrease of pH values (accumulation of H+ ions). The Al2O3/In2O3 bio-FET enabled the continuous detection of pH values with low-operation voltages. Figure 3e illustrates the real-time drain current (Ids) responses as a function of pH changes. The Ids dropped instantly with increased pH values, indicating the fast response of Al2O3 sensitive layer to the proton perturbation.

In comparison, although bare In2O3 FET showed distinguished pH responses with high sensitivity of 69.0 mV pH–1 and good linearity of 98.6% (Figure 3f,g), the SS varied a lot in pH ranges of 3–9 (Figure S2, Supporting Information). The family of output curves of bare In2O3 FET also shows similar trends with Al2O3/In2O3 FET, but it exhibits a less stable saturation behavior at low pH value (Figure 3h). Such discrepancies could be attributed to the direct exposure of In2O3 channel to electrolyte solution, making the device more susceptive to extreme pH values. In result, the Al2O3/In2O3 device outperformed bare In2O3 FETs in both stability and linearity towards pH sensing.

In addition, we evaluated the chemical response of the Al2O3/In2O3 based bio-FET for metal ions of Na+, K+, and Ca2+. Figure S3a (Supporting Information) illustrates the real-time current-time (Ids vs time) curve of the bio-FET, and 0.5 µL PBS buffer solutions containing 0.01 m different ions of Na+, K+, Ca2+, and H+ were introduced into a 500 µL PBS buffer solution at different time, respectively. As shown in Figure S3b (Supporting Information), the metal ions only caused baseline fluctuations, and only the addition of H+ yielded remarkable responses.

To further validate the sensor's reliability, we investigated the hysteresis behaviors of the device between two pH values with repeated testing cycles. The hysteresis of FET pH sensors refer to the fluctuations of responsive current between the minimum and maximum measurements with the constant pH value at different tested cycles, which are related to surface defects of sensing film and ion interactions at the liquid–solid interfaces.[46, 47] As shown in Figure S4 (Supporting Information), the Al2O3/In2O3 bio-FET showed strong immunity against hysteresis, with a tiny hysteresis width of only ≈20 nA at pH = 7.40 during the pH switching loops of 7.40–8.10–7.40–8.10–7.40. The fast response along with low hysteresis verified the feasibility of the solution processed Al2O3/In2O3 interfaces in continuous pH monitoring.

Signal drifting is another challenge suffered by many nanomaterial-based potentiometric pH sensors, which denotes a time-dependent migration of sensing signals.[48, 49] To study the drift effect of In2O3 and Al2O3/In2O3 based FETs, we tested the transfer curves of both devices in 1.0× PBS solution (pH = 7.4) at different time. Figure 4a,b shows the corresponding transfer curves in both logarithmic and linear scales. The Al2O3/In2O3 bio-FET exhibited much more stable transfer characteristics than bare In2O3 FET over time. Specifically, as depicted in Figure 4c, the Al2O3/In2O3 device displayed a low drift rate (∆Vth vs time) of only 0.35 mV h–1, which was two orders lower than that of bare In2O3 FETs (maximum drift = 12.1 mV h–1). In addition, to further investigate the device stability, we studied its drift behaviors in 1.0× PBS buffer with different pH values over longer time. As shown in Figure S5 (Supporting Information), the Vth drift rates over 5 h were 0.44, 1.1, and 8.1 mV h–1 at pH = 7.01, 9.15, and 5.10, respectively. The Al2O3/In2O3 device showed more stable performance in neutral environment than in acid or alkali conditions. These results are comparable to those reported in ISFETs with Al2O3 sensing films (drift rate = 0.05–5 mV h–1).[19, 38]

At the same time, the relative current change of Al2O3/In2O3 device showed no obvious variation (≈2.7%) over time, outperforming bare In2O3 counterpart which shows high fluctuation with the maximum change of ≈20% within 1 h (Figure 4d). The poor drift performance of the latter may be originated from unshielded In2O3 channel surface which is vulnerable to electronic perturbation at electrolyte/channel interfaces. To further evaluate possible signal drift over long time, we measured the real-time Ids and Igs curves of Al2O3/In2O3 bio-FET in buffer solution (pH = 7.4) for >10 h. As shown in Figure S6 (Supporting Information), the device maintained stable operation with tiny Ids drift rate of only 6.7 nA h–1 during continuous measurement. Also, the sensing current Ids (670 nA) was several times higher than IAg/AgCl (Igs = 20 nA), indicating the insulating properties of Al2O3 layer which can effectively suppress gate leakage current.

In ISFETs, the sensitivity and drift performance usually improved with thinner pH-sensitive layers.[19] To explore the influence of Al2O3 thickness on FET sensors’ performance, we prepared Al2O3 films with different layer numbers by spin-coating Al2O3 precursor with different times. As shown in Figure S7a (Supporting Information), the thicknesses of Al2O3 films with one, two, and three layers were ≈7, 14, and 22 nm, respectively (measured by ellipsometry). As shown in Figure S7b (Supporting Information), the In2O3 devices with 7-nm-thick Al2O3 (one layer) outperformed that with 14-nm-thick Al2O3 (two layers), showing larger Ion/off ratio, higher μsat, lower Vth, and improved SS. The device with 22-nm-thick Al2O3 film (three layers) was inactive, which could be attributed to the severely reduced electron concentration in In2O3 channel, because the Al3+ in the Al2O3 layer could strongly combine with the oxygen vacancies in In2O3 during annealing.[50] Also, we studied the pH sensing performance of In2O3 bio-FETs with 14-nm-thick Al2O3 layer. The device exhibited degraded performance with sensitivity of 11.0 mV pH–1 and linearity of 90.5% (Figure S7c, Supporting Information), compared with those of its 7-nm Al2O3 counterpart (sensitivity of 61.9 mV pH–1, linearity of 99.7%).

A comparison of characteristics of pH sensors between the Al2O3/In2O3 bio-FET and documented sensors are listed in Table S2 (Supporting Information). Compared to the previous studies using semiconducting oxides as pH sensitive layer, we achieved higher sensitivity together with lower drift rate. Remarkably, most of Al2O3 sensitive layers in the reported ISFETs were fabricated via vacuum-based processes (e.g., atom layer deposition). In our approach, both the In2O3 channel and Al2O3 layers were fabricated by solution-based low-cost processes, illustrating the feasibility of solution processes in delivering high-performance bio-FETs.

2.4 Microfluidics for Continuous Monitoring
Integration of bio-FETs with microfluidic systems is significant to achieve real-time, miniaturized, and continuous pH sensors for lab-on-a-chip applications.[51] Nanoscale bio-FETs normally employ bulky and brittle glass-bodied Ag/AgCl reference electrodes as gate electrodes,[8, 52-54] which hindered device miniaturization and their further integration into a microfluidic chip.[32] To address this issue, we introduced a planar, microfabrication-compatible Ag/AgCl pseudo reference electrode by in situ chlorination reaction of thermally evaporated Ag films.[55, 56] A schematic illustration of the process and corresponding optical images are shown in Figures S8 and S9 (Supporting Information), respectively. With the planar Ag/AgCl as reference electrode, we successfully achieved microfluidic pH sensor system based on Al2O3/In2O3 bio-FETs.

A schematic and digital photo of the microfluidic system are illustrated in Figure 5a,b, respectively. Figure 5c illustrates a typical transfer performance of the Al2O3/In2O3 bio-FET in microfluidics gated by the planar Ag/AgCl electrode in 1.0× PBS solution (pH = 7.4), showing that the Ag/AgCl electrode could effectively configure the FET device. The microfluidic device showed a clear n-type channel characteristic over the whole voltage range with respect to the Ag/AgCl reference electrode potential, exhibiting a mobility of 6.7 cm2 V–1 s–1, a Vth of 0.12 V, and Ion/off ratio of >102. Meanwhile, the sensing current Ids of 23 µA was much higher than the leakage current IAg/AgCl of 340 nA at the same gate voltage (VAg/AgCl = 0.3 V). Moreover, with a lower gating potential (VAg/AgCl = 0.1 V), the Ids remained stable, and IAg/AgCl is neglectable (7.6 nA) over time (Figure S10, Supporting Information), illustrating the high quality and good insulating properties of as-prepared Ag/AgCl electrode and Al2O3 dielectric layer. The output characteristics are shown in Figure 5d. Still, with planar Ag/AgCl electrode, the Al2O3/In2O3 bio-FET demonstrated good pinch-off and fast saturation behaviors in analogous to that gated by commercial reference electrodes.

The microfluidic system provides an efficient sensor platform for continuous pH sensing. We then first quantified the pH responses of the sensor. As revealed in Figure 5e, the transfer curves exhibit characteristic shifts at different pH values. As a result, the sensor showed a linear response between Vth and pH values with a high sensitivity of 62.4 mV pH–1 and a linearity of 99.3% (Figure 5f). In addition, output characteristics (Figure 5g,h) also demonstrate the high sensitivity of sensor response toward pH sensing. To evaluate the stability and reliability of the Al2O3/In2O3 bio-FET based microfluidic sensor, we performed real-time measurements with switched pH loops of 7.51–6.59–7.51–6.59–7.51 over a period of 2500 s. As shown in Figure 5i, the device exhibited stable and distinguished responses to different pH values with a low hysteresis width of 120 nA, providing a powerful sensing platform for continuous pH monitoring. Based on such Al2O3/In2O3 bio-FET microfluidics, high-performance biosensors toward other chemical and biological analytes can be explored in the future.

2.5 Real-World pH Sensing Applications
The high performance of the Al2O3/In2O3 bio-FET promised pH sensing toward real-world samples. To demonstrate this, we used our sensor to measure the pH values of packaged orange juice, peach juice, and green tea available in market. The orange and peach juices were used as purchased, and the green tea sample was brewed by soaking green tea leaves in hot water for 5 h. No filtration step was performed prior pH measurement. During measurements, the bio-FETs were first immersed in PBS solution (pH 5.91), and different sample solutions were added subsequently. As shown in Figure S11 (Supporting Information), the bio-FET exhibited distinctive responses (Vth shifts) to these samples. Remarkably, our sensor demonstrated accurate results compared with pH values measured by a commercial pH meter (Table S3, Supporting Information).

3 Conclusions
In summary, we have fabricated high-performance bio-FETs by rational engineering over the Al2O3/In2O3 dielectric/semiconductor interfaces. The Al2O3 layer effectively passivated the ultrathin In2O3 channel and as compared to the bare In2O3-based FET, the Al2O3/In2O3 based bio-FET exhibited greatly improved long-term stability with low signal drift of only 0.35 mV h–1. Furthermore, we successfully integrated the Al2O3/In2O3 based bio-FET with an effective microfluidic system to deliver a lab-on-chip platform to realize real-time continuous pH monitoring. As presented in this work, the facile and low-cost solution-based strategy for tailoring the Al2O3/In2O3 interfaces provides a platform to construct stable and highly sensitive FET sensors.

4 Experimental Section
Materials
Indium (III) nitrate hydrate (In(NO3)3 · xH2O, 99.999%, Sigma Aldrich), aluminum (III) nitrate nonahydrate (Al(NO3)3 · 9H2O, 99.997%, Sigma Aldrich), APTES (98%, Alfa Aesar), 2-methoxyethanol (2-ME, 99.3%, Alfa Aesar), ammonium hydroxide (NH3 · H2O, 28%, Alfa Aesar), and acetylacetone (AcAc, 99%, Alfa Aesar) were used as obtained. PDMS (Sylgard 184) base and curing agent were purchased from Dow Corning. Highly doped silicon wafer with 100 nm thick SiO2 dielectric layer was received from Silicon Valley Microelectronics, Inc.

Metal Oxide Precursor Preparation
0.30 g In(NO3)3 · xH2O and 0.38 g Al(NO3)3 · 9H2O were dissolved in 10 mL 2-ME mixed with 100 µL AcAc and 35 µL NH3 · H2O to form 0.1 m In2O3 and Al2O3 precursor solutions, respectively. The solutions were stirred at room temperature for 24 h before use. AcAc and NH3 · H2O were added as additives to facilitate combustion reaction and thus improve electrical performance.[57]

DLP Technique
Si/SiO2 substrates were cleaned by ultrasonication in acetone, isopropyl alcohol, and deionized (DI) water sequentially for 5 min, respectively. The 0.1 m indium oxide precursor was spin-coated on UV–O3 treated substrates for 30 s at 3000 rpm min−1. The In2O3 films were pre-baked for 1 min at 100 °C to remove organic solvent, followed by exposure to deep ultraviolet (DUV) irradiation for 10 min through shadow masks. After that, substrates were placed in a mixed solution (methanol: acetic acid = 20:1, v:v) for 5 s, then washed with DI water and dried with nitrogen. Next, the patterned In2O3 films were annealed in air at 300 °C on a hot plate for 1 h.[31]

Device Fabrication
The FET devices possess a BGTC structure. The Ni and Au (thickness = 8/30 nm) were deposited on In2O3 channel patterns via a shadow mask by thermal evaporation, forming In2O3 FET with S/D electrodes. To prepare Al2O3/In2O3 interfaces, 0.1 m Al2O3 precursor was deposited on the top of the UV–O3 treated In2O3 FET by spin-coating, following by annealing for 1 h at 300 °C. For pH sensing, In2O3 and Al2O3/In2O3 FETs were immersed in 5% (volume ratio) APTES ethanol solution for 10 min to modify the APTES layer, following by rinsing with ethanol and DI water.

Fabrication of Planar Ag/AgCl Electrodes
To fabricate thin-film Ag/AgCl reference electrodes, Ni (8 nm) and Ag (200 nm) were deposited on substrates by thermal evaporation via shadow masks. Silver film was chloritized to AgCl by immersing the substrate in 0.05 m FeCl3 aqueous solution for 50 s at 25 °C. After that, the surfaces were cleaned with DI water and dried with nitrogen.

Characterization
The surface topography of In2O3 and Al2O3 films was obtained by AFM (Cypher ES, Oxford Instruments). The powder X-ray diffractometer (D8 Advance, Bruker) was used to determine surface crystallinity and phase analysis. The layered structures were characterized by TEM (Titan Themis 200) with an EDS (super-X, Bruker).

Semiconductor parameter analyzer (Keithley 4200 SCS) and/or source meter (Agilent B2912A) were used to measure the electrical performance of the FETs and bio-FETs in dry and liquid environments. Abstract
Field-effect transistors (FETs) with nanoscale channels have emerged as excellent platforms for constructing high-sensitivity biosensors. However, in typical FET-based biosensors, the electronic perturbation at channel/electrolyte interfaces results in poor stability and severe signal drifts, making long-term continuous measurements quite challenging. To address this issue, here the dielectric/semiconductor interface is tailored by depositing an ultrathin layer of Al2O3 (≈7 nm in thickness) on In2O3 FETs via solution-based processes. The results show that the Al2O3 dielectric layers effectively passivate the In2O3 channel and endow the device with low operation voltage (0.05 V), high sensitivity (61.9 mV pH−1), and low current drift (0.35 mV h−1) upon long-term and continuous pH monitoring. This work paves the way to real-time biosensing with high sensitivity, excellent stability, and low power consumption.

1 Introduction
Field-effect transistor-based biosensors (bio-FETs) are promising for a wide range of applications owing to their advantages including intrinsic signal amplification, label-free detection, ease of miniaturization, and compatibility to complementary metal oxide semiconductor (CMOS) technology.[1-4] The sensing response of bio-FETs relies on the selective binding of analytes (e.g., H+, metal ions, and biomolecules) onto the channel surface, which results in the alternations in channel conductance and electrical signals.[5-7] Therefore, semiconductor nanomaterials susceptive to surface potential changes are excellent channel materials for highly sensitive bio-FETs.[8-11] However, in contrary to conventional FETs, the nanoscale channels in bio-FETs are often exposed to the electrolytes, in which the interference of background ions in the electrolyte will disturb sensing signals, leading to high leakage current and eventually poor stability of the device over long-term measurements.[12, 13] To date, this still represents one major challenge for bio-FETs to realize continuous biosensing with low-power dissipation and long-term stability.[14, 15]

Tailoring device configurations is an effective strategy to optimize the stability of bio-FETs.[16-18] To this end, different device structures have been developed to prevent the direct exposure of liquids to the active channel materials. For example, extended gate and floating gate structures have been successfully applied to separate channels from wet electrolytes in bio-FETs.[19, 20] Nevertheless, these strategies require complicated fabrication processes including encapsulation and patterning.[21, 22] On the other hand, it has been well-documented that rational interface engineering could endow FETs with significantly improved performance. For instance, with a thin high-k dielectric layer on the surface of nanostructured channel materials, both electron mobility and device stability are enhanced.[23-26] However, these dielectric/semiconductor interfaces were usually constructed from capital-intensive vacuum-based fabrication and patterning processes, which still suffer from processing complexity, device-to-device variation, and high cost.[27-30] With regard to their emerging applications, it is highly demanded, yet still remains a great challenge, to develop facile and effective strategies to configure the interface between dielectric and semiconductor and achieve high-performance bio-FETs.

Here, we fabricated high-performance bio-FETs with rationally engineered dielectric/semiconductor interfaces of Al2O3/In2O3 from low-cost solution-based processes. Metal oxide semiconductors (e.g., In2O3, IGZO) based bio-FETs usually exhibit high electrical performance and flexibility in composition control, yet insufficient stability in liquids with high operation voltage and large leakage current.[31-35] These drawbacks could be effectively alleviated by the formation of Al2O3/In2O3 interfaces demonstrated in this work, achieving stable, continuous pH sensing with low signal drift. First, ultrathin Al2O3 film behaves as an effective passivation layer to improve the electrical performance of In2O3 FETs.[23] Second, high-k Al2O3 dielectric offers high sensitivity for pH sensing, as widely demonstrated in typical silicon-based ion-sensitive FETs (ISFETs).[36-38] Finally, Al2O3 film provides effective encapsulation for In2O3 channel underneath, which suppresses leakage current and circumventing ions diffusion and possible damage into semiconducting layers. As a result, both electron mobility and signal drift rate of the Al2O3/In2O3 interfaces-based FETs (0.80 cm2 V–1 s–1 and 0.35 mV h–1) have been improved, in comparison with those of bare In2O3 FETs (0.22 cm2 V–1 s–1 and 12.1 mV h–1). Notably, the Al2O3/In2O3 based bio-FET also exhibited low operation voltage (Vds = 0.05 V) and a high sensitivity of 61.9 mV pH–1 in the pH range from 3 to 10. Furthermore, we integrated the Al2O3/In2O3 bio-FETs with microfluidic systems to deliver a lab-on-a-chip biosensor, which enabled low-drift and continuous pH monitoring over long-term measurements. The strategy presented in this work is promising for scalable and cost-efficient bioelectronics applications.

2.2 FET Device Characterization
We fabricated In2O3 based FETs with bottom-gate, top-contact (BGTC) configurations on highly doped Si (p++) substrate with SiO2 gate dielectric layer. For FET operation in dry states, Si (p++) substrate acted as the gate electrode. The In2O3 channels were patterned via a direct light patterning (DLP) technique reported previously.[31] Source/drain (S/D) electrodes were deposited by thermal evaporation of Ni/Au electrodes to construct In2O3 FETs. Subsequently, the Al2O3 precursor solution was spin-coated on In2O3 FETs and annealed to form Al2O3/In2O3 interfaces. A schematic showing the structure of device with S/D electrodes (channel width/length = 1000/100 µm) is illustrated in Figure 2a. The transfer curves (Ids–Vgs, solid lines) of In2O3 FETs with and without Al2O3 passivation layer are presented in Figure 2b. The bare In2O3 FET showed a saturation mobility (μsat) of 0.22 cm2 V–1 s–1, a subthreshold swing (SS) of 3.7 V dec–1, and a large current on/off ratio (Ion/off) of 1.0 × 105. After passivation with Al2O3 layer, the Al2O3/In2O3 based device exhibited an enhanced μsat of 0.80 cm2 V–1 s–1, an improved SS of 2.9 V dec–1, and a larger Ion/off of 1.0 × 106 with a higher on current. The subthreshold currents of In2O3 and In2O3/Al2O3 based FETs in dry states (Si gate) were <0.62 and <3.1 µA, respectively. The leakage currents (Igs, open circles, Figure 2b) of In2O3 and Al2O3/In2O3 FETs were both below 10–9 A. The right axis in Figure 2b shows the square root plot of Ids (solid circles), and the threshold voltage (Vth) values were extracted of 19.3 and 7.1 V for bare In2O3 and Al2O3/In2O3 based FETs, respectively.

Prior to electrical measurements in the liquid environment, the surface of Al2O3 film was modified by a layer of (3-aminopropyl)triethoxysilane (APTES) to achieve more stable and linear pH sensitivity.[10] In a typical static measurement, a poly(dimethylsiloxane) (PDMS) reservoir (size of 0.5 × 0.5 cm2) was attached on the device surface to confine analyte solution. Figure 2f depicts the output characteristics of the liquid gated Al2O3/In2O3 FET. The Al2O3/In2O3 FET showed outstanding low-voltage saturation with stable output currents, which are important for biosensing applications that require low-operation voltages and high stability.

2.3 High-Performance pH Sensing
Figure 3a shows the model illustrating the pH-induced surface charge state changes of the APTES-modified Al2O3/In2O3 bio-FETs. The pH value change in electrolyte solution induced the deprotonation or protonation of terminating groups (hydroxyl groups or amino groups) on Al2O3 surface, which alternated the potential at the solid/liquid interface and led to changes in channel conductance.[12] Specifically, solution with high pH values would cause deprotonation on Al2O3 surface which depleted the electrons in the In2O3 channel, resulting in reduced conductance and positively shifted Vth, and vice versa. Figure 3b shows the transfer characteristics (Ids–VAg/AgCl) of the liquid-gated Al2O3/In2O3 bio-FET at various pH values from 3 to 10 with a drain bias (Vds) fixed at 0.05 V. The device exhibited stable electrical performance with distinctive Vth shifts at different pH values. As a result, the Al2O3/In2O3 device provided a linear dependence between Vth and pH values with a sensitivity of 61.9 mV pH−1 and a linearity of 99.7% (Figure 3c). Remarkably, no obvious SS variation was observed (Figure S2, Supporting Information), because the Al2O3 layer behaved as effective barrier to prevent ion diffusion and water diffusion to the underneath In2O3 channel layers. Furthermore, the liquid-gated Al2O3/In2O3 bio-FET showed fast saturation and stable output characteristics with distinctive pinch-off behaviors at different pH values ranging from 3 to 10. As illustrated in Figure 3d, the output behaviors can be effectively configured with different pH values, where the saturation currents increased gradually with the decrease of pH values (accumulation of H+ ions). The Al2O3/In2O3 bio-FET enabled the continuous detection of pH values with low-operation voltages. Figure 3e illustrates the real-time drain current (Ids) responses as a function of pH changes. The Ids dropped instantly with increased pH values, indicating the fast response of Al2O3 sensitive layer to the proton perturbation.

In comparison, although bare In2O3 FET showed distinguished pH responses with high sensitivity of 69.0 mV pH–1 and good linearity of 98.6% (Figure 3f,g), the SS varied a lot in pH ranges of 3–9 (Figure S2, Supporting Information). The family of output curves of bare In2O3 FET also shows similar trends with Al2O3/In2O3 FET, but it exhibits a less stable saturation behavior at low pH value (Figure 3h). In result, the Al2O3/In2O3 device outperformed bare In2O3 FETs in both stability and linearity towards pH sensing.

In addition, we evaluated the chemical response of the Al2O3/In2O3 based bio-FET for metal ions of Na+, K+, and Ca2+. Figure S3a (Supporting Information) illustrates the real-time current-time (Ids vs time) curve of the bio-FET, and 0.5 µL PBS buffer solutions containing 0.01 m different ions of Na+, K+, Ca2+, and H+ were introduced into a 500 µL PBS buffer solution at different time, respectively. As shown in Figure S3b (Supporting Information), the metal ions only caused baseline fluctuations, and only the addition of H+ yielded remarkable responses.

To further validate the sensor's reliability, we investigated the hysteresis behaviors of the device between two pH values with repeated testing cycles. The hysteresis of FET pH sensors refer to the fluctuations of responsive current between the minimum and maximum measurements with the constant pH value at different tested cycles, which are related to surface defects of sensing film and ion interactions at the liquid–solid interfaces.[46, 47] As shown in Figure S4 (Supporting Information), the Al2O3/In2O3 bio-FET showed strong immunity against hysteresis, with a tiny hysteresis width of only ≈20 nA at pH = 7.40 during the pH switching loops of 7.40–8.10–7.40–8.10–7.40. The fast response along with low hysteresis verified the feasibility of the solution processed Al2O3/In2O3 interfaces in continuous pH monitoring.

Signal drifting is another challenge suffered by many nanomaterial-based potentiometric pH sensors, which denotes a time-dependent migration of sensing signals.[48, 49] To study the drift effect of In2O3 and Al2O3/In2O3 based FETs, we tested the transfer curves of both devices in 1.0× PBS solution (pH = 7.4) at different time. Figure 4a,b shows the corresponding transfer curves in both logarithmic and linear scales. The Al2O3/In2O3 bio-FET exhibited much more stable transfer characteristics than bare In2O3 FET over time. Specifically, as depicted in Figure 4c, the Al2O3/In2O3 device displayed a low drift rate (∆Vth vs time) of only 0.35 mV h–1, which was two orders lower than that of bare In2O3 FETs (maximum drift = 12.1 mV h–1). In addition, to further investigate the device stability, we studied its drift behaviors in 1.0× PBS buffer with different pH values over longer time. As shown in Figure S5 (Supporting Information), the Vth drift rates over 5 h were 0.44, 1.1, and 8.1 mV h–1 at pH = 7.01, 9.15, and 5.10, respectively. The Al2O3/In2O3 device showed more stable performance in neutral environment than in acid or alkali conditions. These results are comparable to those reported in ISFETs with Al2O3 sensing films (drift rate = 0.05–5 mV h–1).[19, 38]

To further evaluate possible signal drift over long time, we measured the real-time Ids and Igs curves of Al2O3/In2O3 bio-FET in buffer solution (pH = 7.4) for >10 h. As shown in Figure S6 (Supporting Information), the device maintained stable operation with tiny Ids drift rate of only 6.7 nA h–1 during continuous measurement. Also, the sensing current Ids (670 nA) was several times higher than IAg/AgCl (Igs = 20 nA), indicating the insulating properties of Al2O3 layer which can effectively suppress gate leakage current.

In ISFETs, the sensitivity and drift performance usually improved with thinner pH-sensitive layers.[19] To explore the influence of Al2O3 thickness on FET sensors’ performance, we prepared Al2O3 films with different layer numbers by spin-coating Al2O3 precursor with different times. As shown in Figure S7a (Supporting Information), the thicknesses of Al2O3 films with one, two, and three layers were ≈7, 14, and 22 nm, respectively (measured by ellipsometry). As shown in Figure S7b (Supporting Information), the In2O3 devices with 7-nm-thick Al2O3 (one layer) outperformed that with 14-nm-thick Al2O3 (two layers), showing larger Ion/off ratio, higher μsat, lower Vth, and improved SS. The device with 22-nm-thick Al2O3 film (three layers) was inactive, which could be attributed to the severely reduced electron concentration in In2O3 channel, because the Al3+ in the Al2O3 layer could strongly combine with the oxygen vacancies in In2O3 during annealing.[50] Also, we studied the pH sensing performance of In2O3 bio-FETs with 14-nm-thick Al2O3 layer. The device exhibited degraded performance with sensitivity of 11.0 mV pH–1 and linearity of 90.5% (Figure S7c, Supporting Information), compared with those of its 7-nm Al2O3 counterpart (sensitivity of 61.9 mV pH–1, linearity of 99.7%).

A comparison of characteristics of pH sensors between the Al2O3/In2O3 bio-FET and documented sensors are listed in Table S2 (Supporting Information). Compared to the previous studies using semiconducting oxides as pH sensitive layer, we achieved higher sensitivity together with lower drift rate. Remarkably, most of Al2O3 sensitive layers in the reported ISFETs were fabricated via vacuum-based processes (e.g., atom layer deposition). In our approach, both the In2O3 channel and Al2O3 layers were fabricated by solution-based low-cost processes, illustrating the feasibility of solution processes in delivering high-performance bio-FETs.

2.4 Microfluidics for Continuous Monitoring
Integration of bio-FETs with microfluidic systems is significant to achieve real-time, miniaturized, and continuous pH sensors for lab-on-a-chip applications.[51] Nanoscale bio-FETs normally employ bulky and brittle glass-bodied Ag/AgCl reference electrodes as gate electrodes,[8, 52-54] which hindered device miniaturization and their further integration into a microfluidic chip.[32] To address this issue, we introduced a planar, microfabrication-compatible Ag/AgCl pseudo reference electrode by in situ chlorination reaction of thermally evaporated Ag films.[55, 56] A schematic illustration of the process and corresponding optical images are shown in Figures S8 and S9 (Supporting Information), respectively. With the planar Ag/AgCl as reference electrode, we successfully achieved microfluidic pH sensor system based on Al2O3/In2O3 bio-FETs.

A schematic and digital photo of the microfluidic system are illustrated in Figure 5a,b, respectively. Figure 5c illustrates a typical transfer performance of the Al2O3/In2O3 bio-FET in microfluidics gated by the planar Ag/AgCl electrode in 1.0× PBS solution (pH = 7.4), showing that the Ag/AgCl electrode could effectively configure the FET device. The microfluidic device showed a clear n-type channel characteristic over the whole voltage range with respect to the Ag/AgCl reference electrode potential, exhibiting a mobility of 6.7 cm2 V–1 s–1, a Vth of 0.12 V, and Ion/off ratio of >102. Meanwhile, the sensing current Ids of 23 µA was much higher than the leakage current IAg/AgCl of 340 nA at the same gate voltage (VAg/AgCl = 0.3 V). Moreover, with a lower gating potential (VAg/AgCl = 0.1 V), the Ids remained stable, and IAg/AgCl is neglectable (7.6 nA) over time (Figure S10, Supporting Information), illustrating the high quality and good insulating properties of as-prepared Ag/AgCl electrode and Al2O3 dielectric layer. The output characteristics are shown in Figure 5d. Still, with planar Ag/AgCl electrode, the Al2O3/In2O3 bio-FET demonstrated good pinch-off and fast saturation behaviors in analogous to that gated by commercial reference electrodes.

2.5 Real-World pH Sensing Applications
The high performance of the Al2O3/In2O3 bio-FET promised pH sensing toward real-world samples. To demonstrate this, we used our sensor to measure the pH values of packaged orange juice, peach juice, and green tea available in market. The orange and peach juices were used as purchased, and the green tea sample was brewed by soaking green tea leaves in hot water for 5 h. No filtration step was performed prior pH measurement. During measurements, the bio-FETs were first immersed in PBS solution (pH 5.91), and different sample solutions were added subsequently. As shown in Figure S11 (Supporting Information), the bio-FET exhibited distinctive responses (Vth shifts) to these samples. Remarkably, our sensor demonstrated accurate results compared with pH values measured by a commercial pH meter (Table S3, Supporting Information).

3 Conclusions
In summary, we have fabricated high-performance bio-FETs by rational engineering over the Al2O3/In2O3 dielectric/semiconductor interfaces. The Al2O3 layer effectively passivated the ultrathin In2O3 channel and as compared to the bare In2O3-based FET, the Al2O3/In2O3 based bio-FET exhibited greatly improved long-term stability with low signal drift of only 0.35 mV h–1. Furthermore, we successfully integrated the Al2O3/In2O3 based bio-FET with an effective microfluidic system to deliver a lab-on-chip platform to realize real-time continuous pH monitoring. As presented in this work, the facile and low-cost solution-based strategy for tailoring the Al2O3/In2O3 interfaces provides a platform to construct stable and highly sensitive FET sensors.

4 Experimental Section
Materials
Indium (III) nitrate hydrate (In(NO3)3 · xH2O, 99.999%, Sigma Aldrich), aluminum (III) nitrate nonahydrate (Al(NO3)3 · 9H2O, 99.997%, Sigma Aldrich), APTES (98%, Alfa Aesar), 2-methoxyethanol (2-ME, 99.3%, Alfa Aesar), ammonium hydroxide (NH3 · H2O, 28%, Alfa Aesar), and acetylacetone (AcAc, 99%, Alfa Aesar) were used as obtained. PDMS (Sylgard 184) base and curing agent were purchased from Dow Corning. Highly doped silicon wafer with 100 nm thick SiO2 dielectric layer was received from Silicon Valley Microelectronics, Inc.

Metal Oxide Precursor Preparation
0.30 g In(NO3)3 · xH2O and 0.38 g Al(NO3)3 · 9H2O were dissolved in 10 mL 2-ME mixed with 100 µL AcAc and 35 µL NH3 · H2O to form 0.1 m In2O3 and Al2O3 precursor solutions, respectively. The solutions were stirred at room temperature for 24 h before use. AcAc and NH3 · H2O were added as additives to facilitate combustion reaction and thus improve electrical performance.[57]

DLP Technique
Si/SiO2 substrates were cleaned by ultrasonication in acetone, isopropyl alcohol, and deionized (DI) water sequentially for 5 min, respectively. The 0.1 m indium oxide precursor was spin-coated on UV–O3 treated substrates for 30 s at 3000 rpm min−1. The In2O3 films were pre-baked for 1 min at 100 °C to remove organic solvent, followed by exposure to deep ultraviolet (DUV) irradiation for 10 min through shadow masks. After that, substrates were placed in a mixed solution (methanol: acetic acid = 20:1, v:v) for 5 s, then washed with DI water and dried with nitrogen. Next, the patterned In2O3 films were annealed in air at 300 °C on a hot plate for 1 h.[31]

Device Fabrication
The FET devices possess a BGTC structure. The Ni and Au (thickness = 8/30 nm) were deposited on In2O3 channel patterns via a shadow mask by thermal evaporation, forming In2O3 FET with S/D electrodes. To prepare Al2O3/In2O3 interfaces, 0.1 m Al2O3 precursor was deposited on the top of the UV–O3 treated In2O3 FET by spin-coating, following by annealing for 1 h at 300 °C. For pH sensing, In2O3 and Al2O3/In2O3 FETs were immersed in 5% (volume ratio) APTES ethanol solution for 10 min to modify the APTES layer, following by rinsing with ethanol and DI water.

Fabrication of Planar Ag/AgCl Electrodes
To fabricate thin-film Ag/AgCl reference electrodes, Ni (8 nm) and Ag (200 nm) were deposited on substrates by thermal evaporation via shadow masks. Silver film was chloritized to AgCl by immersing the substrate in 0.05 m FeCl3 aqueous solution for 50 s at 25 °C. After that, the surfaces were cleaned with DI water and dried with nitrogen.

Characterization
The surface topography of In2O3 and Al2O3 films was obtained by AFM (Cypher ES, Oxford Instruments). The powder X-ray diffractometer (D8 Advance, Bruker) was used to determine surface crystallinity and phase analysis. The layered structures were characterized by TEM (Titan Themis 200) with an EDS (super-X, Bruker).

Semiconductor parameter analyzer (Keithley 4200 SCS) and/or source meter (Agilent B2912A) were used to measure the electrical performance of the FETs and bio-FETs in dry and liquid environments.
