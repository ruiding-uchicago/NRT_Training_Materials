Graphical Abstract
Liquid-phase sensing of various organic solvents is performed for the first time, using a solvent-resistant organic-transistor platform. Sensors composed of a cross-linked poly(3-hexylthiophene) (P3HT)–azide co-polymer and a calixarene derivative exhibit highly sensitive and selective sensing behavior, owing to the selective binding effects of the liquid analytes with the calixarene-functionalized P3HT–azide, extending the range of their use in practical applications.

Sensors based on organic field-effect transistor (OFET) platforms have recently attracted great interest due to their use as flexible, light-weight, low-cost, and large-area electronics.1-4 Functional properties of organic active layers can be tailored by rational molecular design or surface functionalization to enhance selectivity.5-8 Compared with conventional sensing devices composed of two terminals, FET-type sensors enable easy amplification of the detected signals and facile electrical tuning of the sensitivity by controlling the applied voltage on the third electrode; i.e., the gate terminal.9-12 To date, various OFET-based sensors have been developed for sensing a wide range of analytes including chemical compounds,4, 13 biological species,14, 15 pressure,16, 17 light,18, 19 and food,20 as well as for health monitoring.3 Our group recently reported highly sensitive OFET-based chemical sensors using highly crystalline pentacene layers with macroporous structures.21

Nonetheless, there is a substantial limitation in the practical applications of OFET-based sensors; namely, the poor resistance of organic semiconductors in common organic solvents. Although OFET-based sensors for detecting vapor-phase organic solvents and aqueous-phase analytes have been reported,5, 13, 22, 23 the direct sensing of liquid-phase analytes—especially organic solvents—has not yet been achieved using OFET-based sensors. Approaches are available to prevent organic semiconducting layers from dissolving in organic solvents: i) passivation24 or ii) chemical/physical cross-linking25, 26 of the organic semiconductor layers. However, the passivation of organic active layers often decreases the sensitivity, and bulky cross-linking agents with additional functional groups tend to disturb their molecular packing and hamper efficient charge transport.27

We recently developed solvent-resistant OFETs and thermally stable organic solar cells using azide-functionalized poly(3-hexylthiophene) (P3HT) co-polymers.26 The azide functional groups were utilized as an efficient photo-cross-linker to generate stabilized organic electronic devices without a loss of electrical performance. The photo-cross-linking of azide functional groups could be achieved by exposure to UV irradiation for a few minutes, leading to enhanced durability of the semiconducting layer in organic solvents. Sensors that detect liquid-phase organic solvents may eventually be utilized in harsh environments where the in-situ monitoring of harmful chemicals is required, substantially expanding their practical applications. In addition, to increase the selectivity of OFET-based sensors, several approaches including the immobilization of DNA anchors by physical absorption,14, 28 rational design of organic semiconductors,13 and the introduction of selectivity-enhancing molecules,22, 29, 30 have been developed. In particular, calixarene derivatives have been used to improve the selectivity of sensors due to their cavity structure and specific binding events with analytes (e.g., hydrophilic (O–H···O) and lipophilic (C–H···π) interactions).22, 31, 32 Moreover, the structural tunability of calix[n]arene derivatives provides compatibility with various analytes.

Having demonstrated the feasibility of solvent-resistant OFETs based on P3HT–azide co-polymers, we used this system as a liquid-phase sensing platform for chemical species. Herein, we present OFET-based sensors that can detect organic solvents and pH solutions in the liquid phase using a cross-linked polymer semiconductor; namely, a P3HT–azide co-polymer. The solvent-resistant polymer semiconducting films enable direct detection of toxic liquid-phase chemicals. Moreover, a thin layer of calix[n]arene was introduced on top of the semiconducting layer to enhance selectivity toward target analytes. To the best of our knowledge, this report demonstrates the first liquid-phase sensing of toxic analytes using solution-processed OFET-based sensors.

The P3HT–azide co-polymer, synthesized from 20 mol% of 3-(azidohexyl)thiophene monomer, was used as the semiconducting layer of the OFET-based sensors because of its optimized solvent-resistance and electrical property (Figure 1a).26 The surface of the P3HT–azide co-polymer layer was modified with the container molecule, calix[8]arene (C[8]A), to enhance selectivity. Among the various calixarene derivatives, C[8]A was selected because of its well-known selectivity for a variety of organic solvents and its commercial availability.22 The solvent-resistant semiconducting layers (≈50 nm in thickness) were prepared on n-octadecyltrimethoxysilane (OTS)-modified SiO2/Si substrates using a P3HT–azide co-polymer solution in chloroform (2 mg mL−1) via drop-casting, and photo-cross-linking with UV irradiation (with a wavelength of λ = 254 nm) was subsequently performed. Gold source and drain electrodes, as well as the C[8]A layer (3-nm thickness), were thermally deposited sequentially (see the Experimental Section for details). The C[8]A layer was deposited after the gold electrodes were formed, in order to avoid poor charge injection between the electrode and semiconducting layer.

Typical transfer and output characteristics are shown in Figure 1b and c, respectively. Prior to deposition of the C[8]A thin layer, the P3HT–azide co-polymer OFETs showed a mobility of 0.031(±0.008) cm2 V−1 s−1 and an on/off ratio of 1.6(±0.8)×106. After functionalization with the C[8]A thin layer, the average field-effect mobility and on/off ratio of the OFETs based on the P3HT–azide co-polymer were 0.032(±0.004) cm2 V−1 s−1 and 1.4(±0.5)×106, respectively. Interestingly, the current level of the OFETs with C[8]A was higher than that of devices without C[8]A. This may be due to the p-type doping effect of the semiconducting layer, which is related to the induced polarization by contact with the polar C[8]A layer on the surface of the P3HT–azide films. The threshold voltage of the OFETs shifted from 2.6(±4.4) to 11.5(±8.1) V after introduction of the C[8]A layer, revealing the easier turn-on due to the electron withdrawing characteristics of the C[8]A layer. The linear regime operation also showed obvious field-effect behavior (Figure S1a,b in the Supporting Information (SI)). The long-term air-stability of OFET sensing devices based on cross-linked P3HT–azide co-polymers was reasonably higher than non-cross-linked pristine P3HT (Figure S1c, SI).26 This finding indicates that our cross-linking approach can improve air-stability to some degree in addition to providing solvent-resistivity, most likely due to the fact that the cross-linked top surfaces might slow down the diffusion of oxygen into the channel.26

We designed a novel sensing system for the detection of various organic solvents and pH solutions, as illustrated in Figure 2a. A poly(dimethylsiloxane) (PDMS) container was introduced in the channel area to prevent liquid analytes from spreading out. After a stabilizing time (≈50 s), the organic solvent to be analyzed was injected into the mounted PDMS blocking container. Sensing experiments were performed under ambient conditions by monitoring the drain current as a function of time at a fixed drain–source voltage (VDS) and gate–source voltage (VGS) (VDS = −2 V, VGS = −20 V). The sensing signals were obtained by dividing the drain current by the baseline current (ID/IBASE).

Figure 2b shows that the liquid-phase direct-sensing behavior of methanol depends on the C[8]A layer. The OFET-based sensor based on P3HT–azide co-polymer showed enhanced drain current after injection of liquid-phase methanol. In polymer OFET-based sensors, charge-hopping transport may increase after the absorption of polar solvents because the distance between polymer molecules can be decreased due to dipole–dipole interactions between polymer molecules and polar analytes.33-35 Interestingly, the ID/IBASE intensity of the response of the P3HT–azide sensor with the C[8]A layer toward methanol was an order of magnitude higher than that of the device without C[8]A because the number of charge carriers increased due to the enhanced electron withdrawing characteristics and coulombic interactions between methanol and C[8]A, confirming the effectiveness of C[8]A. The inset of Figure 2b represents the plot of the statistical data for the sensing results of the OFET-based sensors with and without C[8]A. The saturated IDS/IBASE values after 20 s from the injection were utilized. S represents the drain current divided by the base current (ID/IBASE). The box and error bar show the average and standard deviation of the sensing results, respectively.

Furthermore, the sensing signals of various organic solvents, such as methanol, ethanol, deionized (DI) water, toluene, and n-hexane were monitored and compared, as shown in Figure 2c. While positive sensing behaviors—in which the drain current is enhanced after binding events with the analytes—were observed for methanol, ethanol, and DI water; negative sensing behaviors, where the drain current is reduced after binding events with the analytes, were observed for toluene and n-hexane. When solvents containing hydroxyl groups (i.e., polar solvents) were injected, the C[8]A/hydroxyl group binding events tended to increase the electron-withdrawing characteristics. The number of holes (h+) that functioned as charge carriers would increase due to induced surface–dipole interactions, resulting in an increased drain current for the p-channel P3HT–azide OFET-based sensor. On the other hand, enhanced electron-donating characteristics played a role in the change of drain current for non-polar liquid analytes because of the lipophilic interactions between C[8]A and non-polar solvents. Moreover, the charge-carrier-trapping effect may occur in the presence of non-polar solvents. It has been reported that non-polar solvent molecules can infiltrate into the channel area through grain boundaries and cause a charge-trapping phenomenon.10, 34, 36, 37 Consequently, the drain current would decrease if non-polar solvents were introduced onto the surface of the semiconducting layer of the P3HT–azide sensor. These effects could lead to well-resolved sensing results for various organic solvents (Figure 2c). The sensing test and statistical analysis of the sensors with and without the C[8]A layer for solvents other than methanol also revealed the effectiveness of the C[8]A layer for enhancing the sensitivity (Figure S2, SI).

The surface morphologies of the films of pristine P3HT–azide and C[8]A-functionalized P3HT–azide before/after methanol sensing were investigated using tapping-mode atomic force microscopy (AFM) (Figure S3, SI). The cross-linked P3HT–azide film showed a smooth surface with a root-mean-square (rms) roughness of 0.782 nm (Figure S3a,b; SI). The C[8]A was deposited in granular form on the cross-linked P3HT–azide layer (Figure S3c,d; SI). AFM analysis revealed that the surface of the active layer of the sensor was maintained after direct exposure to organic solvents (Figure S3e,f; SI). The results of the AFM analysis clearly support the durability and stability of the cross-linked P3HT–azide films in organic solvents.

Although DI water is the most polar solvent used in this study (as shown in Table 1),38, 39 the change in the drain current upon exposure to DI water was not larger than those for methanol and ethanol. This may result from interactions between C[8]A and the analytes. The alcohols (i.e., methanol and ethanol) form both lipophilic and hydrophilic interactions with C[8]A, which increases the number of charge carriers in the channel area. In the case of DI water, only hydrophilic interactions were available with C[8]A. Therefore, the signals of DI water could be smaller than those of the alcohols. Methanol showed a larger signal than ethanol due to the larger polarity. In contrast, increased electron-donating characteristics and charge-trapping effects play a role in changing the drain current for the other two analytes with relatively non-polar characteristics: less-polar (i.e., toluene) or non-polar (i.e., n-hexane) solvents.38, 39 Non-polar solvents absorbed into the polymer film could be trapped in the grain boundary and/or enlarge the charge-hopping distance between polymer molecules, reducing the conductivity of the polymer film.33, 34

The sensitivity of the sensors has been investigated with three organic solvents (methanol, ethanol, and n-hexane) using toluene as the base medium (Figure 2d and Figure S4a,b in the SI). Toluene was adopted as a representative medium of the solvents because it could be blended abundantly with the three other solvents (i.e., methanol, ethanol, and n-hexane). Various concentrations of the organic solvents were injected into the toluene medium (Figure 2d). The slope of the sensing signal with respect to the rate of signal change was also plotted to verify the detection. The sensors with C[8]A showed a detection limit of ≈1 vol% for these three solvents. The magnified signal changes and slopes for the detection of n-hexane are shown in the SI (Figure S4a). For comparison, the sensing signal of the sensors without the C[8]A layer was also investigated by injecting methanol in the base medium of toluene (Figure S4b, SI). In contrast with the sensors with C[8]A that showed a clear response from ≈1 vol% of methanol, the sensors without C[8]A showed a detection limit of ≈4 vol% with a lower signal intensity. Furthermore, the detection of toluene using methanol as the medium was performed (Figure S4c, SI). In this case, the detection limit of toluene in methanol was ≈2 vol%, and the signal was rather unstable compared with the opposite case. It might result from the higher induced dipole moments of methanol than those of toluene. The sensing demonstration of methanol in DI water showed only small changes in the output current (Figure S4d, SI). We surmise that DI water molecules as the base medium have already interacted with C[8]A molecules sufficiently, and the signal change of the sensors was not significant upon injection of methanol.

The signal intensities of the sensors with and without the C[8]A layer for solvent blend systems were investigated (Figure S4e, SI). Two miscible solvents were mixed with a volume ratio of 1:1. The sensors with C[8]A showed higher signal intensities than those without C[8]A, indicating the effectiveness of the C[8]A layer in terms of selectivity. The relative comparison for the interactions between analyte and C[8]A was performed by monitoring the intensity and the enhancement direction of the signals. These experimental results revealed that the interactions between alcohols and C[8]A were typically higher than those between non-polar solvents and C[8]A. Moreover, the consecutive injections of different solvents in a single sensing test were also performed (Figure S4f, SI). After the stabilization period (≈50 s), 10 μL of toluene and ethanol were injected alternately to the sensors with and without C[8]A at intervals of 50 s. Negative and positive sensing behaviors were demonstrated by injecting toluene and methanol, respectively. The sensors with C[8]A showed enhanced signal intensity, while those without C[8]A exhibited minimal signal changes after one cycle of injection, which are supportive of the enhanced selectivity of the sensors via the introduction of the C[8]A layer.

The reusability of the sensors has been investigated with two organic solvents, i.e., methanol and toluene for positive and negative sensing, respectively (Figure 2e,f). After a sensing test, we dried the residual solvent and the devices at the temperature equivalent to 70% of the boiling point of each solvent. The sensors with cross-linked P3HT–azide films showed moderate reusability with ≈36.2% and ≈35.8% degradation in the relative sensitivity after three sensing tests for methanol and toluene, respectively. However, the devices prepared from non-cross-linked P3HT–azide films showed a significantly decreased stability with ≈85% degradation in the relative sensitivity after three sensing tests for methanol. The sensing test for toluene with the non-cross-linked P3HT–azide films could not be performed because of the lift-off of the electrodes and the solvated semiconducting layer (Figure S5, SI). Morphological analysis has also been performed with AFM (Figure S6, SI). The surfaces of the active layers of the sensors with cross-linked P3HT–azide remained rather intact after three repetitions of the sensing test for methanol (Figure S6a,b; SI) and toluene (Figure S6c,d; SI), with the average surface roughness of 0.767 and 0.587 nm, respectively. In contrast, the active layer of the sensor with non-cross-linked P3HT–azide showed an increased average surface roughness (0.903 nm) and more degradation after three tests for sensing methanol (Figure S6e,f; SI). These results are supportive of the enhanced stability of the solvent-resistant OFET-based sensors.

Figure 3a shows statistical data for the sensing results of the liquid-phase analytes. The saturated ID/IBASE values at 70 s after turning on were collected and analyzed for more than 10 sensor devices. The symbols and error bars in Figure 3a represents the average and standard deviation of the ID/IBASE, respectively. Atomistic observation also agreed with a posteriori conclusion via both molecular dynamics (MD) and density functional theory (DFT) calculations. It was found that the average dipole moment from the MD study followed the trend of ID/IBASE, as shown in Figure 3a. To further examine this trend, the charge-transfer was investigated using DFT calculations, which provided information on the varying partial charges of the solvent and C[8]A molecules when they were in an energetically configured interaction (see Figure 3b and Figure S7 and S8 in the SI). In polar solvents (i.e., methanol, ethanol, and DI water), C[8]A becomes electron-deficient due to the electron-withdrawing nature of the solvents that originated from the hydroxyl group. In non-polar solvents (i.e., toluene and n-hexane), C[8]A becomes electron-rich due to the electron-donating nature of the solvents, where the benzene group of toluene and the C–H group of n-hexane functioned as an electron-donor. The relative sensitivity of the sensor and the induced dipole moments of the liquid analytes calculated by the molecular simulation are summarized in Table 1.

In addition to sensing with organic solvents, we examined various pH solutions (see SI). A comparison of the drain current change depending on the pH solution is available in the SI (Figure S9a). The change in the ID/IBASE was in good agreement with the change in pH, with larger intensity at lower pH (i.e., more acidic) conditions. This result is in agreement with our hypothesis for a pH sensing test, which is similar to the results of previous reports in which infiltrated hydronium ions (H3O+) through grain boundaries of the P3HT–azide films improve the performance of OFETs through a p-type doping effect of the organic semiconducting layer.13 A statistical analysis of the sensing results was also performed (Figure S9b, SI).

We also fabricated P3HT–azide sensors that can operate at low voltages (VDS = –0.6 V, VGS = –0.5 V) using a polymer gate dielectric. A cross-linked poly-4-vinylphenol (PVP) layer was introduced as a high-k polymer dielectric layer (Figure 4a). The results of the sensing tests for various organic solvents with low-voltage operating sensors showed identical trends as SiO2 dielectric-based sensors described above (Figure 4b). Furthermore, flexible sensors with a polymer substrate and a polymer dielectric were fabricated. The flexible sensor platform was fabricated with indium tin oxide (ITO)-coated polyethylene naphthalate (PEN) as the polymer substrate and cross-linked PVP as the polymer dielectric. The PVP dielectric was cross-linked with 4,4′-(hexafluoroisopropylidene) diphthalic anhydride (HDA) and used as the gate dielectric. A photograph of the flexible sensor is shown in Figure 4c, which showed ample electrical performance (Figure S10a,b; SI). The detailed fabrication method of the flexible sensors is described in the SI. The low-voltage operating sensors showed lower signal intensities than silicon-based sensors, which is presumably due to the lower level of the output current of the devices operated at a lower voltage condition and the rougher surface of the PVP dielectric than the OTS-treated SiO2, which can affect the morphology of the semiconducting layer (Figure S10c−f, SI).

In conclusion, we demonstrated the direct sensing of liquid analytes with solvent-resistant cross-linked P3HT–azide co-polymer. C[8]A, a calixarene derivative, was introduced to enhance the selectivity and sensitivity of the OFET-based sensors. Various liquid-phase organic solvents and pH solutions were detected using the solvent-resistant OFET-based sensors. Well-defined sensing results were demonstrated based on the combined effects between the liquid-phase analytes and the sensor, the C[8]A/functional group binding of the analytes, and the enhanced electron-withdrawing/donating characteristics depending on the liquid-phase analytes. The experimental results agreed with computational studies at the atomistic level. Furthermore, solvent-resistant flexible OFET sensors for detecting liquid-phase chemical species were fabricated with a polymer substrate and a polymer dielectric. This is the first demonstration of directly sensing liquid-phase analytes using an OFET-based sensor, which is challenging because of the poor resistance of organic semiconductors in organic solvents. This report provides a viable methodology for the fabrication of OFET-based sensors that can detect liquid-phase organic solvents and extends the possibility of their practical use.

Experimental Section
OFET-Based Sensor Fabrication and Characterization: The P3HT–azide co-polymer was synthesized with the method that we reported previously.26 The OFET-based sensors were fabricated on the heavily n-doped silicon (Si) wafers with thermally grown, 300-nm SiO2 layers (capacitance of gate dielectric per unit area, Ci = ca. 10 nF cm−2). The P3HT-azide co-polymers containing 20 mol% of azide units were solvated with chloroform (2 mg mL−1), and the solution was stirred at 40 °C for over 6 h under a nitrogen atmosphere to dissolve the polymer into the solution completely. Then the solution was filtered with a polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE) syringe filter with a pore diameter of 0.22 μm and deposited on the OTS-modified SiO2/Si substrate by the drop-casting method. Details about the fabrication of the self-assembled monolayer (SAM) of OTS are described in the SI. The films were then thermally annealed at 150 °C for 30 min under a nitrogen atmosphere. Then, the annealed films were chemically cross-linked using a UV lamp (λ = 254 nm) for 10 min. After cross-linking, 40-nm gold electrodes were thermally deposited on top of the films under a pressure below 5.0×10−6 Torr. The channel length (L) and width (W) of the electrodes were 50 μm and 1 mm, respectively. C[8]A (Sigma-Aldrich) was deposited thermally with a nominal thickness of 3 nm (rate: ≈0.2–0.3 Å s−1). For the measurements of the electrical characteristics and sensing performance of OFETs, a Keithley 4200 semiconductor parameter analyzer was used in a N2-filled glove box for the electrical characterization and under ambient atmosphere for the sensing test, respectively. Prior to deposition of the C[8]A thin layer, the P3HT–azide co-polymer OFETs showed a mobility of 0.031(±0.008) cm2 V−1 s−1 and an on/off ratio of 1.6(±0.8)×106. After functionalization with the C[8]A thin layer, the average field-effect mobility and on/off ratio of the OFETs based on the P3HT–azide co-polymer were 0.032(±0.004) cm2 V−1 s−1 and 1.4(±0.5)×106, respectively. Interestingly, the current level of the OFETs with C[8]A was higher than that of devices without C[8]A. This may be due to the p-type doping effect of the semiconducting layer, which is related to the induced polarization by contact with the polar C[8]A layer on the surface of the P3HT–azide films. The threshold voltage of the OFETs shifted from 2.6(±4.4) to 11.5(±8.1) V after introduction of the C[8]A layer, revealing the easier turn-on due to the electron withdrawing characteristics of the C[8]A layer. The linear regime operation also showed obvious field-effect behavior (Figure S1a,b in the Supporting Information (SI)). The long-term air-stability of OFET sensing devices based on cross-linked P3HT–azide co-polymers was reasonably higher than non-cross-linked pristine P3HT (Figure S1c, SI).26 This finding indicates that our cross-linking approach can improve air-stability to some degree in addition to providing solvent-resistivity, most likely due to the fact that the cross-linked top surfaces might slow down the diffusion of oxygen into the channel.26

We designed a novel sensing system for the detection of various organic solvents and pH solutions, as illustrated in Figure 2a. A poly(dimethylsiloxane) (PDMS) container was introduced in the channel area to prevent liquid analytes from spreading out. After a stabilizing time (≈50 s), the organic solvent to be analyzed was injected into the mounted PDMS blocking container. Sensing experiments were performed under ambient conditions by monitoring the drain current as a function of time at a fixed drain–source voltage (VDS) and gate–source voltage (VGS) (VDS = −2 V, VGS = −20 V). The sensing signals were obtained by dividing the drain current by the baseline current (ID/IBASE).

Figure 2b shows that the liquid-phase direct-sensing behavior of methanol depends on the C[8]A layer. The OFET-based sensor based on P3HT–azide co-polymer showed enhanced drain current after injection of liquid-phase methanol. In polymer OFET-based sensors, charge-hopping transport may increase after the absorption of polar solvents because the distance between polymer molecules can be decreased due to dipole–dipole interactions between polymer molecules and polar analytes.33-35 Interestingly, the ID/IBASE intensity of the response of the P3HT–azide sensor with the C[8]A layer toward methanol was an order of magnitude higher than that of the device without C[8]A because the number of charge carriers increased due to the enhanced electron withdrawing characteristics and coulombic interactions between methanol and C[8]A, confirming the effectiveness of C[8]A. The inset of Figure 2b represents the plot of the statistical data for the sensing results of the OFET-based sensors with and without C[8]A. The saturated IDS/IBASE values after 20 s from the injection were utilized. S represents the drain current divided by the base current (ID/IBASE). The box and error bar show the average and standard deviation of the sensing results, respectively.

Furthermore, the sensing signals of various organic solvents, such as methanol, ethanol, deionized (DI) water, toluene, and n-hexane were monitored and compared, as shown in Figure 2c. While positive sensing behaviors—in which the drain current is enhanced after binding events with the analytes—were observed for methanol, ethanol, and DI water; negative sensing behaviors, where the drain current is reduced after binding events with the analytes, were observed for toluene and n-hexane. When solvents containing hydroxyl groups (i.e., polar solvents) were injected, the C[8]A/hydroxyl group binding events tended to increase the electron-withdrawing characteristics. The number of holes (h+) that functioned as charge carriers would increase due to induced surface–dipole interactions, resulting in an increased drain current for the p-channel P3HT–azide OFET-based sensor. On the other hand, enhanced electron-donating characteristics played a role in the change of drain current for non-polar liquid analytes because of the lipophilic interactions between C[8]A and non-polar solvents. Moreover, the charge-carrier-trapping effect may occur in the presence of non-polar solvents. It has been reported that non-polar solvent molecules can infiltrate into the channel area through grain boundaries and cause a charge-trapping phenomenon.10, 34, 36, 37 Consequently, the drain current would decrease if non-polar solvents were introduced onto the surface of the semiconducting layer of the P3HT–azide sensor. These effects could lead to well-resolved sensing results for various organic solvents (Figure 2c). The sensing test and statistical analysis of the sensors with and without the C[8]A layer for solvents other than methanol also revealed the effectiveness of the C[8]A layer for enhancing the sensitivity (Figure S2, SI).

The surface morphologies of the films of pristine P3HT–azide and C[8]A-functionalized P3HT–azide before/after methanol sensing were investigated using tapping-mode atomic force microscopy (AFM) (Figure S3, SI). The cross-linked P3HT–azide film showed a smooth surface with a root-mean-square (rms) roughness of 0.782 nm (Figure S3a,b; SI). The C[8]A was deposited in granular form on the cross-linked P3HT–azide layer (Figure S3c,d; SI). AFM analysis revealed that the surface of the active layer of the sensor was maintained after direct exposure to organic solvents (Figure S3e,f; SI). The results of the AFM analysis clearly support the durability and stability of the cross-linked P3HT–azide films in organic solvents.

Although DI water is the most polar solvent used in this study (as shown in Table 1),38, 39 the change in the drain current upon exposure to DI water was not larger than those for methanol and ethanol. This may result from interactions between C[8]A and the analytes. The alcohols (i.e., methanol and ethanol) form both lipophilic and hydrophilic interactions with C[8]A, which increases the number of charge carriers in the channel area. In the case of DI water, only hydrophilic interactions were available with C[8]A. Therefore, the signals of DI water could be smaller than those of the alcohols. Methanol showed a larger signal than ethanol due to the larger polarity. In contrast, increased electron-donating characteristics and charge-trapping effects play a role in changing the drain current for the other two analytes with relatively non-polar characteristics: less-polar (i.e., toluene) or non-polar (i.e., n-hexane) solvents.38, 39 Non-polar solvents absorbed into the polymer film could be trapped in the grain boundary and/or enlarge the charge-hopping distance between polymer molecules, reducing the conductivity of the polymer film.33, 34

The sensitivity of the sensors has been investigated with three organic solvents (methanol, ethanol, and n-hexane) using toluene as the base medium (Figure 2d and Figure S4a,b in the SI). Toluene was adopted as a representative medium of the solvents because it could be blended abundantly with the three other solvents (i.e., methanol, ethanol, and n-hexane). Various concentrations of the organic solvents were injected into the toluene medium (Figure 2d). The slope of the sensing signal with respect to the rate of signal change was also plotted to verify the detection. The sensors with C[8]A showed a detection limit of ≈1 vol% for these three solvents. The magnified signal changes and slopes for the detection of n-hexane are shown in the SI (Figure S4a). For comparison, the sensing signal of the sensors without the C[8]A layer was also investigated by injecting methanol in the base medium of toluene (Figure S4b, SI). In contrast with the sensors with C[8]A that showed a clear response from ≈1 vol% of methanol, the sensors without C[8]A showed a detection limit of ≈4 vol% with a lower signal intensity. Furthermore, the detection of toluene using methanol as the medium was performed (Figure S4c, SI). In this case, the detection limit of toluene in methanol was ≈2 vol%, and the signal was rather unstable compared with the opposite case. It might result from the higher induced dipole moments of methanol than those of toluene. The sensing demonstration of methanol in DI water showed only small changes in the output current (Figure S4d, SI). We surmise that DI water molecules as the base medium have already interacted with C[8]A molecules sufficiently, and the signal change of the sensors was not significant upon injection of methanol.

The signal intensities of the sensors with and without the C[8]A layer for solvent blend systems were investigated (Figure S4e, SI). Two miscible solvents were mixed with a volume ratio of 1:1. The sensors with C[8]A showed higher signal intensities than those without C[8]A, indicating the effectiveness of the C[8]A layer in terms of selectivity. The relative comparison for the interactions between analyte and C[8]A was performed by monitoring the intensity and the enhancement direction of the signals. These experimental results revealed that the interactions between alcohols and C[8]A were typically higher than those between non-polar solvents and C[8]A. Moreover, the consecutive injections of different solvents in a single sensing test were also performed (Figure S4f, SI). After the stabilization period (≈50 s), 10 μL of toluene and ethanol were injected alternately to the sensors with and without C[8]A at intervals of 50 s. Negative and positive sensing behaviors were demonstrated by injecting toluene and methanol, respectively. The sensors with C[8]A showed enhanced signal intensity, while those without C[8]A exhibited minimal signal changes after one cycle of injection, which are supportive of the enhanced selectivity of the sensors via the introduction of the C[8]A layer.

The reusability of the sensors has been investigated with two organic solvents, i.e., methanol and toluene for positive and negative sensing, respectively (Figure 2e,f). After a sensing test, we dried the residual solvent and the devices at the temperature equivalent to 70% of the boiling point of each solvent. The sensors with cross-linked P3HT–azide films showed moderate reusability with ≈36.2% and ≈35.8% degradation in the relative sensitivity after three sensing tests for methanol and toluene, respectively. However, the devices prepared from non-cross-linked P3HT–azide films showed a significantly decreased stability with ≈85% degradation in the relative sensitivity after three sensing tests for methanol. The sensing test for toluene with the non-cross-linked P3HT–azide films could not be performed because of the lift-off of the electrodes and the solvated semiconducting layer (Figure S5, SI). Morphological analysis has also been performed with AFM (Figure S6, SI). The surfaces of the active layers of the sensors with cross-linked P3HT–azide remained rather intact after three repetitions of the sensing test for methanol (Figure S6a,b; SI) and toluene (Figure S6c,d; SI), with the average surface roughness of 0.767 and 0.587 nm, respectively. In contrast, the active layer of the sensor with non-cross-linked P3HT–azide showed an increased average surface roughness (0.903 nm) and more degradation after three tests for sensing methanol (Figure S6e,f; SI). These results are supportive of the enhanced stability of the solvent-resistant OFET-based sensors.

Figure 3a shows statistical data for the sensing results of the liquid-phase analytes. The saturated ID/IBASE values at 70 s after turning on were collected and analyzed for more than 10 sensor devices. The symbols and error bars in Figure 3a represents the average and standard deviation of the ID/IBASE, respectively. Atomistic observation also agreed with a posteriori conclusion via both molecular dynamics (MD) and density functional theory (DFT) calculations. It was found that the average dipole moment from the MD study followed the trend of ID/IBASE, as shown in Figure 3a. To further examine this trend, the charge-transfer was investigated using DFT calculations, which provided information on the varying partial charges of the solvent and C[8]A molecules when they were in an energetically configured interaction (see Figure 3b and Figure S7 and S8 in the SI). In polar solvents (i.e., methanol, ethanol, and DI water), C[8]A becomes electron-deficient due to the electron-withdrawing nature of the solvents that originated from the hydroxyl group. In non-polar solvents (i.e., toluene and n-hexane), C[8]A becomes electron-rich due to the electron-donating nature of the solvents, where the benzene group of toluene and the C–H group of n-hexane functioned as an electron-donor. The relative sensitivity of the sensor and the induced dipole moments of the liquid analytes calculated by the molecular simulation are summarized in Table 1.

In addition to sensing with organic solvents, we examined various pH solutions (see SI). A comparison of the drain current change depending on the pH solution is available in the SI (Figure S9a). The change in the ID/IBASE was in good agreement with the change in pH, with larger intensity at lower pH (i.e., more acidic) conditions. This result is in agreement with our hypothesis for a pH sensing test, which is similar to the results of previous reports in which infiltrated hydronium ions (H3O+) through grain boundaries of the P3HT–azide films improve the performance of OFETs through a p-type doping effect of the organic semiconducting layer.13 A statistical analysis of the sensing results was also performed (Figure S9b, SI).

We also fabricated P3HT–azide sensors that can operate at low voltages (VDS = –0.6 V, VGS = –0.5 V) using a polymer gate dielectric. A cross-linked poly-4-vinylphenol (PVP) layer was introduced as a high-k polymer dielectric layer (Figure 4a). The results of the sensing tests for various organic solvents with low-voltage operating sensors showed identical trends as SiO2 dielectric-based sensors described above (Figure 4b). Furthermore, flexible sensors with a polymer substrate and a polymer dielectric were fabricated. The flexible sensor platform was fabricated with indium tin oxide (ITO)-coated polyethylene naphthalate (PEN) as the polymer substrate and cross-linked PVP as the polymer dielectric. The PVP dielectric was cross-linked with 4,4′-(hexafluoroisopropylidene) diphthalic anhydride (HDA) and used as the gate dielectric. A photograph of the flexible sensor is shown in Figure 4c, which showed ample electrical performance (Figure S10a,b; SI). The detailed fabrication method of the flexible sensors is described in the SI. The low-voltage operating sensors showed lower signal intensities than silicon-based sensors, which is presumably due to the lower level of the output current of the devices operated at a lower voltage condition and the rougher surface of the PVP dielectric than the OTS-treated SiO2, which can affect the morphology of the semiconducting layer (Figure S10c−f, SI).

In conclusion, we demonstrated the direct sensing of liquid analytes with solvent-resistant cross-linked P3HT–azide co-polymer. C[8]A, a calixarene derivative, was introduced to enhance the selectivity and sensitivity of the OFET-based sensors. Various liquid-phase organic solvents and pH solutions were detected using the solvent-resistant OFET-based sensors. Well-defined sensing results were demonstrated based on the combined effects between the liquid-phase analytes and the sensor, the C[8]A/functional group binding of the analytes, and the enhanced electron-withdrawing/donating characteristics depending on the liquid-phase analytes. The experimental results agreed with computational studies at the atomistic level. Furthermore, solvent-resistant flexible OFET sensors for detecting liquid-phase chemical species were fabricated with a polymer substrate and a polymer dielectric. This is the first demonstration of directly sensing liquid-phase analytes using an OFET-based sensor, which is challenging because of the poor resistance of organic semiconductors in organic solvents. This report provides a viable methodology for the fabrication of OFET-based sensors that can detect liquid-phase organic solvents and extends the possibility of their practical use.

Experimental Section
OFET-Based Sensor Fabrication and Characterization: The P3HT–azide co-polymer was synthesized with the method that we reported previously.26 The OFET-based sensors were fabricated on the heavily n-doped silicon (Si) wafers with thermally grown, 300-nm SiO2 layers (capacitance of gate dielectric per unit area, Ci = ca. 10 nF cm−2). The P3HT-azide co-polymers containing 20 mol% of azide units were solvated with chloroform (2 mg mL−1), and the solution was stirred at 40 °C for over 6 h under a nitrogen atmosphere to dissolve the polymer into the solution completely. Then the solution was filtered with a polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE) syringe filter with a pore diameter of 0.22 μm and deposited on the OTS-modified SiO2/Si substrate by the drop-casting method. Details about the fabrication of the self-assembled monolayer (SAM) of OTS are described in the SI. The films were then thermally annealed at 150 °C for 30 min under a nitrogen atmosphere. Then, the annealed films were chemically cross-linked using a UV lamp (λ = 254 nm) for 10 min. After cross-linking, 40-nm gold electrodes were thermally deposited on top of the films under a pressure below 5.0×10−6 Torr. The channel length (L) and width (W) of the electrodes were 50 μm and 1 mm, respectively. C[8]A (Sigma-Aldrich) was deposited thermally with a nominal thickness of 3 nm (rate: ≈0.2–0.3 Å s−1). For the measurements of the electrical characteristics and sensing performance of OFETs, a Keithley 4200 semiconductor parameter analyzer was used in a N2-filled glove box for the electrical characterization and under ambient atmosphere for the sensing test, respectively.
