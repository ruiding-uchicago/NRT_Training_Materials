Abstract
This article proposes a calibrated methodology as well as computational analyses for designing an explosive sensor for
trinitrotoluene (TNT) detection using ion-sensitive feld-efect transistor (ISFET) through technology computer-aided design
(TCAD) tools. Although industrial device simulators are quite efcient in computing the various electrical parameters of a
semiconductor device such as MOSFET, the absence of electrochemical models has posed a challenge to the investigation
of ISFETs through TCAD simulations. A nanowire MOSFET has been calibrated using thirteen diferent concentrations of
TNT from a fabricated sensor, and diferent sensitivities based on on current, threshold voltage and conductance have been
proposed. The efect of gate oxide on the diferent sensitivities is also reported.
Keywords ISFET · TNT · Sensitivity · Sensor · TCAD · Simulation
1 Introduction
The advances in microelectronics have pushed the frontiers of applications of MOSFETs into diverse domains.
Applications of MOSFETs as chemical and label-free sensors have acquired much interest from the research community as well as the industry due to the ability to shrink
their dimensions and express sensitivities through diferent
electrical parameters. Over the decades, explosive detection has become a potential problem in order to counter
terrorist attacks and landscape contamination. Security has
now become one of the most sensitive elements in global
scenario, especially when technological advancements
have reached the thresholds of terrorism. Apart from this,
the use of landscape which has previously been exposed
to explosive tests and military usage needs proper identifcation and cleansing. In such situations, it is important to
work on novel sensors which can sense explosives in real
time. The traditional methods used for explosive detection
consume additional time and need transfer of analytes from
the site to the laboratory involving bulky and expensive
equipments [1, 2].
Diferent methods have been proposed so far in order
to detect explosives. Of those, optical sensing techniques
[3, 4] and electrochemical sensing techniques [5, 6] are
the most signifcant ones. The most common explosives
which have been used till date are trinitrotoluene (TNT) or
trinitrotriazine (RDX) due to easy manufacturing and low
vapour pressure [7, 8]. Ion-sensitive feld-efect transistors
(ISFETs) are generally identifed as modifed architectures
of metal–oxide–semiconductor feld-efect transistor (MOSFETs), which are employed for sensing specifc materials
through chemical interactions, particularly on the gate structure [9, 10]. The change in architecture involves the use of
charge modifed gate [11], pH sensing events [12, 13] and
gate modifers [14].
Till date, many ISFETs have been proposed for TNT
sensing [15–20]. However, to the best of knowledge of the
authors, ISFET model for calibrated TNT sensing using
industrial device simulator has not yet been reported. Since
the problem of TNT sensing is a concern of international
security, it is utmost necessary that there is a simulation
model for a device so that the sensitivities and performance of the ISFETs may be analyzed before fabricating
the device. The greatest challenge in developing an ISFET
on an industrial device simulator is the absence of electrochemical models. Hence, the event of charge transfer

2 Calibration and simulation methodology
The gate of an ISFET must be modifed to immobilize the
target molecules [21]. The detection of the target molecules
is caused by the formation of charge-transfer donor–acceptor
complexes on the gate of the ISFET. In the article reported
by Sharon et al. (ISFET-1), the gate was customized by
using a modifed APTES layer which was deposited atop
the oxide layer and acted as a binder for the TNT molecules.
On immobilizing the TNT molecules, there occurs development of charges on the sensing surface between the TNT and
APTES, which infuences the electrical parameters of the
device (current, conductance, threshold voltage, etc.) [19].
In order to represent the ISFET on simulation, proper
calibration with fabricated devices is utmost necessary. This
section describes the calibration of the ISFET on TCAD
tool. The calibration methodology is shown in the fowchart
in Fig. 1, where two fabricated ISFETs particularly for TNT
sensing have been analyzed. The frst is an ISFET for explosive detection proposed by Sharon et al. [19], and the second is the silicon nanowire p-FET reported by Engel et al.
[20]. The former is referred to as ISFET-1, and the latter as
ISFET-2 in the entire text of this article.
The steps of calibration are explained here.
a. The TCAD tool does not employ electrochemical models which determine the interaction of molecules and
the charge transfers [22]. So this event must be modeled
and calibrated in the simulator through user-defned
method. Therefore, we propose a calibrated simulation

3 Architecture of the proposed TNT sensor
The 2-D schematic for the TNT sensor taken up for the
work is shown in Fig. 3. The doping concentration for the
source and drain is uniformly kept at 1019 cm−3 and the
bulk is kept at 1016 cm−3. The gate workfunction is kept at
4.5 eV corresponding to silver. The APTES layer is taken
as 1 nm as mentioned from experimental results in [20].
4 Results and discussion
This section presents the results of the TNT sensor through
three subsections. The frst subsection reports the drain
current characteristics of the sensor for diferent TNT
concentrations. The TNT concentrations are taken from
Sharon et al.’s article [19] as listed in Table 1. The second subsection reports the diferent sensitivities of the
TNT sensor and discusses the range which each sensitivity parameter can reach for the minimum and maximum
concentrations. Also, some sensitivities exhibit a linear
ft for the logarithmic representation of the normalized
TNT concentration. Considering the voltages at which the
sensitivities have been reported, such linear fts can help
one to predict the sensitivity of the reported sensor for
any TNT concentration. The third subsection extends the
previous subsection into analyzing the sensitivities for the
TNT sensor with HfO2 as gate dielectric instead of Al2O3.
4.1 Drain current characteristics
The drain current versus drain voltage characteristics for
diferent TNT concentrations are shown in Fig. 4a. With
increasing TNT concentration, the drain current increases.
This is due to the increase in charge at the semiconductor
surface arising out of the APTES–TNT interaction. The
charges have been extracted from ISFET-1 and verifed for
ISFET-2 from the calibration as explained in Sect. 2.
The transfer characteristics of the proposed TNT sensor
for diferent TNT concentrations are shown in Fig. 4b. Similar to Fig. 4a, the drain current increases with the increase
in concentration.

5 Conclusion
In this article, we have reported a methodology to calibrate
a TNT sensor using ISFET on simulation tool by taking into
account the results from two fabricated ISFET devices. We
have applied the calibration models to a nanowire ISFET
sensor for TNT sensing and proposed diferent sensitivity
parameters using deionized water as a reference material.
With the increase in TNT concentrations, the drain current
increases due to the increase in positive charge at the interface. The various calibration methods are verifed for the
diferent electrical parameters. Of all the sensitivities, the
conductance sensitivity provided the highest range of values
between minimum and maximum concentrations. The use of
high-k dielectric as gate oxide was also analyzed using HfO2,
and it was observed that on current sensitivity was lesser
than that of Al2O3 due to higher on current in the former and
the sensitivity being a ratioed parameter. However, in case
of threshold voltage sensitivity and conductance sensitivity, HfO2-based sensor showed higher values than its low-k
counterpart. The methods and results direct in the way of
simulating and calibrating any generalized ISFET device for
industrial applications.
