A fluorescent organic cage for picric acid
detection†
Koushik Acharyya and Partha Sarathi Mukherjee*

Received 8th August 2014,
Accepted 21st October 2014
DOI: 10.1039/c4cc06225f
www.rsc.org/chemcomm

Dynamic covalent imine chemistry has been utilized to synthesize a
fluorescent [3+2] self-assembled nanoscopic organic cage. The
fluorescent nature of the reduced analogue of the cage was further
exploited for the highly selective detection of the explosive picric
acid (PA).

In recent times, the development of cheap and eﬃcient sensory
materials for the detection of explosives has undoubtedly been one
of the most attractive fields of research around the globe due to the
continuous increase in terrorist activities. The common principal
constituents of many land mines are nitroaromatic compounds
(NACs) such as trinitrotoluene (TNT), 2,4,6-trinitrophenol (TNP/
picric acid), dinitrophenol (DNP), etc.1 Various techniques such as
gas chromatography, Raman spectroscopy, ion-mobility spectrometry and fluorescence spectroscopy have so far been employed to
track down NAC explosives.2 Among these diﬀerent techniques,
fluorescence based sensing methodologies are the first choice
owing to their high sensitivity, specificity and fast response times.3
In past few years, many diﬀerent fluorescent polymers,4 MOFs5
and small molecule-based sensors6 have been developed for the
detection of NACs, especially TNT. Surprisingly, little attention has
been given toward the selective and sensitive detection of picric
acid (PA), even though it is as strong an explosive as TNT.7 In
addition to this, PA can cause various health problems including
damage to respiratory organs, skin irritation, nausea and skin
allergies.8 Furthermore, it is widely used in the pharmaceutical
industry, dye industry, chemical laboratories and for the manufacture of rocket fuel, fireworks and so on.9 Owing to its widespread
use it can easily contaminate ground water and soil. Therefore, the
development of new materials for the selective and eﬃcient detection of PA for the purpose of preventing environmental pollution and
terrorist attacks is in high demand.

The first calorimetric and fluorescent receptor for PA based
on hydrogen-bonding and p–p interactions was reported in
2011.10 Later, Kumar, Zheng and others published work on
PA sensors,11 but many of these systems have limitations which
do not allow them to achieve high selectivity for PA over other
nitro phenolic explosives, especially DNP.
In a continuation to our earlier endeavour12 in this direction,
herein we report a nanoscopic fluorescent organic cage derived
from a dialdehyde and a triamine synthesised by utilizing
dynamic covalent imine chemistry13 followed by an imine bond
reduction, which results in very high selectivity towards PA.
In our recent publications14 we have demonstrated that the
dynamic nature of the imine bond can be exploited to selectively
synthesize one cage from a random reaction mixture of two dialdehydes and one triamine and that such a process can be controlled by
non-covalent interactions, especially hydrogen-bonding. Moreover,
the imine bond allows cage to cage conversion.
Covalent organic cages have attracted considerable attention
in recently due to their potential applications in anion sensing,
catalysis, gas storage and separation.15 However, to the best of
our knowledge, they are yet to be explored as a sensor for
nitroaromatic explosives.
Cage 4 was synthesized by employing a one-pot Schiﬀ base
condensation of 1,3,5-tris(aminomethyl)-2,4,6-trimethylbenzene
(2) with 4,4 0 -diformyltriphenylamine (1) followed by sodium
borohydride reduction of the imine bonds (Scheme 1). The
formation and purity of the synthesized cage 4 were fully
examined on the basis of 1H NMR, 13C NMR, FTIR, ESI-HRMS
and X-ray crystallography. These results unambiguously corroborate the formation of a [3+2] self-assembled cage (Fig. 1).
Single crystals of the cage molecule were obtained by slow
vapour diﬀusion of n-pentane into a chloroform solution of cage 4.
The cage crystallized in a monoclinic crystal system with a C2/c
space group. The asymmetric unit is comprised of a half of cage 4
along with several disordered solvent molecules. Solid state structural examination revealed that the benzene rings of amine 2 are
in an almost eclipsed conformation and B13 Å apart, which can
be considered to be the length of the cage. The distance between

The UV-vis spectrum of cage 4 in dichloromethane exhibited

maxima at 305 nm. Upon excitation at 305 nm it showed a very
high Stokes shift of 130 nm with an emission maxima at

The emissive nature of cage 4 both in solution (F = 0.28 in
DCM) and as a solid (F = 0.20) encouraged us to investigate its
potential application as chemosensor for the detection of
nitroaromatics. Thus, we carried out a fluorescence titration
of cage 4 with various nitroaromatics such as picric acid
(PA), 2,4-dinitrophenol (2,4-DNP), 4-nitrophenol (4-NP), TNT,
2,4-dinitrotoluene (2,4-DNT), 3,4-dinitrotoluene (3,4-DNT),
4-nitrotoluene (4-NT), 4-nitrobenzoic acid (4-NBA), 1,3-dinitrobenzene (1,3-DNB) and nitrobenzene (NB). Among these nitroexplosives, notably only PA exhibited a substantial quenching
eﬃciency. As shown in Fig. 2, with gradual addition of PA to a
1  105 M solution of cage 4 in dichloromethane, the fluorescence intensity dramatically attenuated. Only 2 eq. of PA reduced
the initial fluorescence intensity of cage 4 by B96%. By contrast,
the other nitroaromatics exhibited negligible eﬀects on the fluorescence intensity of cage 4 (Fig. 3). This result clearly demonstrates
the high selectivity and sensitivity (detection limit = 6.4 ppb) of
cage 4 towards PA over other nitroaromatics.

In order to determine the quenching constant of cage 4 with
PA, the fluorescence intensity ratio (I0/I) was plotted against the
concentration of PA, which resulted in a non-linear curve
(Fig. 4). Therefore, we applied an exponential quenching equation I0/I = Aek[Q] + B.16 From this non-linear curve fitting, the
quenching constant was calculated to be 2.2  105 M1. This
high value for the quenching constant indicates the superquenching ability of PA towards cage 4.

plot from linearity indicates that quenching is either static or a
combination of static and dynamic. One way to determine whether a
dynamic mechanism is involved or not, is to measure the fluorescence life time of the sensor in both the presence and absence of the
quencher. In the case of static quenching, the quencher binds with

In the case of dynamic quenching, the fluorescence lifetime
of the sensor decreases via the diﬀusion controlled collision
between the excited sensor and the quencher. As portrayed in
Fig. S11 (ESI†), the fluorescence lifetime of cage 4 remains
unchanged in the presence of PA, which suggests that fluorescence
quenching occurs through a static mechanism. Perturbation in the
UV-vis spectrum of the cage with the appearance of a new band at
B420 nm upon gradual addition of PA also shows good agreement
with ground state charge transfer complex formation (Fig. S12,
ESI†). Moreover, there was a sharp colour change from colourless
to pale yellow observed with the naked eye upon the addition of PA
to the solution of cage 4. In the presence of other nitrophenolic
compounds such as 2,4-DNP and 4-NP we did not observe any
considerable change in UV-vis spectral pattern or the colour
of the cage solution (Fig. S13 and S14, ESI†). These experimental results clearly indicate that there is a strong interaction
between PA and the cage compared to other nitro analytes in
the ground state.
In order to gain valuable insight into the mode of interaction
between cage 4 and PA in solution, the 1H NMR spectrum of cage
4 was recorded in the presence of PA. The NMR spectrum
revealed a change in the spectral pattern of the aromatic protons
along with a downfield shift and broadening of the signals
corresponds to the –CH2 moieties of the cage (Fig. S15, ESI†).
We believe that the bare secondary amine moieties in cage 4
interact with PA, which is corroborated by the downfield shift of
the –CH2 protons adjacent to the amine functionalities. PA,
being highly acidic in nature, has a tendency to transfer its
acidic phenolic –OH proton to the basic amino group to form a
strong ion pair (a protonated cage and picrate) complex. 1H NMR
spectral analysis of the cage–PA adduct revealed that up to 6 PA
can form a complex with each cage molecule (Fig. S17, ESI†).
This strong complex formation may further facilitate ground
state charge transfer between the picrate and the protonated
cage. Gas phase DFT calculations revealed that ground state
charge transfer is feasible from the highest occupied molecular

orbital (HOMO) of the picrate to the lowest unoccupied molecular
orbital (LUMO) of the protonated cage (Fig. S16, ESI†). We also
investigated if protonation of the cage rather than PA was the
decisive factor behind the fluorescence quenching of the cage.
To this end, the fluorescence of the cage was recorded in the
presence of 2 eq. of trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) (which is slightly
more acidic than PA), which showed a negligible eﬀect on the
fluorescence intensity of the cage compared to PA (Fig. S18
and S28, ESI†). In a competitive environment PA also induces
higher quenching of the fluorescence of the cage than other
nitroaromatics (Fig. S29, ESI†).
However, the high sensitivity of the cage towards PA
indicates that ground state charge transfer complex formation
is not the sole mechanism behind the fluorescence quenching.
UV-vis spectra of the nitroaromatics (including PA) and the
emission spectrum of the cage do not show any significant
overlap (Fig. S30, ESI†), which rules out the possibility of an
energy transfer process. However, there is large spectral overlap
when picric acid exists as picrate in the presence of the amine
(Fig. S31, ESI†). Therefore, in the presence of the cage, which is
furnished with amine moieties, there is a high possibility of
resonance energy transfer (RET)17 from the protonated cage to
the picrate (Fig. S33, ESI†). Close proximity due to ion-pair
complex formation increases the energy transfer process.
As such, it is quite clear that PA can eﬃciently quench the
fluorescence of the cage via both ground state charge transfer
as well as an energy transfer process unlike the other nitro
aromatics, where fluorescence quenching is only due to an
electron transfer process.
In addition to solution phase detection we also performed
contact mode analysis, which is particularly useful in the field
of forensic and analytical sciences. For this purpose, we prepared test strips by dip coating a solution of cage 4 in DCM onto
Whatman filter paper. A small spot of PA solution (10 mL, 1 mM)
was applied onto the dried filter paper strip, which left a dark
spot under a portable UV light. Moreover, black patches could
be observed on the paper strip under UV light upon rubbing a
small amount of a solid PA sample onto it (Fig. S38, ESI†). We
also checked the possibility of aqueous phase detection of PA by
using a filter paper strip methodology. For this purpose, a filter
paper coated with cage 4 was dipped into an aqueous solution
(tap water, 1 mM) of PA, which completely quenched the initial
fluorescence intensity, whereas in presence of only tap water it
remained unchanged (Fig. S39, ESI†).
In conclusion, a fluorescent triphenyl amine-based covalent
organic cage has been synthesized utilizing dynamic covalent
imine chemistry followed by imine bond reduction. This cage
displayed high sensitivity and selectivity towards picric acid
over a number of other nitroaromatic explosives. Moreover,
it has the potential to discriminate between highly explosive PA
and other nitrophenolic compounds. In addition to solution
phase sensing, this cage compound also showed an excellent
ability to detect PA in the solid phase. The experimental results
demonstrated that the high quenching ability of the picric acid
was due to the formation of a ground state complex with the
cage as well as an excited state energy transfer process.
