Methylene chloride sensing studies

This paper describes the sensing studies of QCM sensors with coated some calixarene derivatives bearing
different functional groups for some selected Volatile Organic Compounds (VOCs) such as acetone,
acetonitrile, carbon tetrachloride, chloroform, methylene chloride (MC), N,N-dimethylformamide, 1,4dioxane, ethanol, ethyl acetate, xylene, methanol, n-hexane and toluene. The initial experiments have
revealed that whole the calix[4]arene modiﬁed QCM sensors exhibited strongest sensing ability to MC
emissions. Thus, the detailed studies were performed for only MC emissions after the determination of
relatively more effective calix-coated QCM sensors for MC emissions in aqueous media. The results demonstrated that QCM sensor coated with calix-7 bearing both amino and imidazole groups was most
useful sensor for MC emissions with 54.1 ppm of detection limit. Moreover, it was understood that cyclic
structures, H-bonding capabilities and also good preorganization properties of calixarene derivatives
played an important role in VOC sensing processes.

1. Introduction
Many developments in industry have brought to a plenty of
new products and services. These advancements cause not only
producing useful technology but also more usage of chemicals. As
is known to all, many of processes involved chemical which are
adequately harmful to the human health and environment when it
is released. Volatile Organic Compounds (VOCs) have signiﬁcant
importance in organic contaminant due to their bad health impact
to human at even low concentrations [1]. VOCs can be released
easily to the environment via human activities and operating of
natural resources [2]. Sensor technologies have been developed to
sense VOCs and eliminate their adverse affect upon healthy [3].
Macromolecules and polymers can be widely used as a sensor so
as to sensing of vapor molecules [4–16].
Calixarenes, well-known macrocyclic molecules which have
almost unlimited derivatization potential and a unique three-dimensional structure with outstanding shape recognition possibilities [17]. They can be synthesized quite easily by condensation of
p-tert-butylphenol with formaldehyde under alkaline conditions
[18]. Calixarenes became promising materials for sensor applications due to their sensing and selectivity abilities. Thin ﬁlms of
calixarenes have been widely used in chemical sensors [19–21].

2. Experimental
2.1. Materials and equipments
Calix[4]arene based coating materials (calix-1-9) and noncyclic
analog a (Fig. 1) were synthesized according to previously published procedures [24–30].
An Ez-Melt apparatus in a sealed capillary was performed to
ﬁnd out all melting points of the synthesized compounds. A Varian
400 MHz NMR spectrometer, a PerkinElmer 100 FTIR spectrometer, and an elemental analysis branded with Leco CHNS-932
analyzer were used to determine structure of all synthesized
products. The reactions were monitored by using TLC analyses
obtained from Merck (Kieselgel 60 F254). All starting materials
and reagents which are standard analytical grade from Merck or
Aldrich were used without further puriﬁcation. The calixarene
imprinted QCM sensor surface was characterized by an atomic
force microscope (AFM). AFM images were taken by using an AFM
(NT-MDT, NTEGRA Spectra, Moscow, Russia).
A time-resolved QCM was used to measure the frequency
change of quartz crystals between gold electrodes with the model
of CHI400B Series from CH Instruments (Austin, USA). Using a
crystal with a 7.995-MHz fundamental frequency, a net change of
1 Hz corresponds to 1.34 ng of materials adsorbed or desorbed
onto the crystal surface of an area (A) of 0.196 cm2.
2.2. Preparation of modiﬁed QCM sensors
Solutions of coating materials were prepared by dissolving in
1.0 mM of chloroform for initial experiments. After the crystal
surface was cleaned by acetone and ethanol, it was subjected to
ultra pure nitrogen to supply dryness. Then QCM crystal surface
was coated with the solutions of these calix[4]arenes by dropcasting method ant the solvent evaporated at 60 °C for 30 min in
drying-oven. Thus the modiﬁed QCM sensors were prepared to use
in sensing studies of some selected VOCs such as acetone, acetonitrile, carbon tetrachloride (CTC), chloroform, methylene

A home-made measuring system (Fig. 2) was used in this study.
Working principle of a QCM device can be explained by the mechanical stresses generated while the analyte is sorbed by sensing
ﬁlm surface are released during sensing. For this way, other
acoustical modes of the QCM which can be observed by higher
harmonic of resonant frequency are actuated. The QCM device
converts the harmonic motion to an electrical signal. The frequency counter measures the frequency which is possessed by
electrical signal [31]. In this system, the coated crystal was placed
in QCM cell to gain frequency responses versus time. For initial
experiments, the volumetric ﬂask was ﬁlled with VOCs. The variation of the QCM frequency was monitored as a function of time
while the sensor was exposed to dry air for reaching equilibrium
or it was exposed to VOCs in the volumetric ﬂask for 1.5 min and
then the sensor was cleaned with dry air at the same interval. In
preliminary studies, the VOC which had highest response was
determined, and thus calixarene derivatives giving considerably
responses towards selected VOC emissions were speciﬁed as best
coating materials in non-aqueous media. In subsequent experiments, these calixarene derivatives were used as coating materials
for sensing of VOC emissions in aqueous media. In MC sensing
studies, the QCM sensors coated with best calixarene based coating materials were exposed to both water and aqueous MC vapors
with different concentrations such as 5, 100, 500, 1000, 2000 ppm,
respectively, in order to observe responses of QCM sensors in
aqueous samples. Here, MC was injected upon water in the ﬂask
during the experiments. During the exposure of aqueous samples,
frequency changes took place proportional with the mass of adsorbed molecules in the sensitive layer. The sensor sensitivity
(S) was found slope of graph which is the frequency change versus
concentration of VOC solutions (in units of Hz/ppm) [9].

an important role in sensing process.

4. Conclusion
In conclusion, the sensing studies of QCM sensors with coated
some calixarene derivatives bearing different functional groups for
some selected Volatile Organic Compounds (VOCs) such as acetone, acetonitrile, carbon tetrachloride, chloroform, methylene
chloride (MC), N,N-dimethylformamide, 1,4-dioxane, ethanol,
ethyl acetate, xylene, methanol, n-hexane and toluene were successfully performed. Initial experiments indicated that all QCM
sensors coated with calix[4]arene derivatives were most favorable
for MC emissions rather than other VOCs. The next studies demonstrated that QCM sensor coated with calix-7 bearing both
amino and imidazole groups was most useful sensor for MC
emissions with 54.1 ppm of detection limit. Consequently, it was
understood that cyclic structures, H-bonding capabilities and also
good preorganization properties of calixarene derivatives played
an important role in VOC sensing processes. On the other hand,
the random arranging of calix[4]arene molecules on surfaces of
QCM crystal affected the sensing efﬁciency. Therefore, it is considered that other coating techniques such as Langmuir-Blodgett
and spin coating can be used to obtain more homogeneous and
neat coated surfaces. Moreover, the ﬁnding of that new sensors is a
hopeful development for electronic nose systems.
